# Chapter 7 — Flag Surfacing and Policy Review

## 1. Why a Flags Document Existed at All

The previous chapter closed with a cross-repository map and a running findings log. The map was a clean technical reference organized by proof-of-concept scope. The log was the map's reasoning trail. Between them, they held most of what the synthesis produced. They did not, however, hold everything.

The synthesis surfaced a class of observations that did not belong in either artifact. Some were candidate findings where the structural evidence pointed toward a conclusion the drafting thread had already overstepped into. Some were terminology collisions that called for an immediate mechanical correction. Some were scope questions the drafting thread could not answer on its own authority and needed to route to the engagement lead. Some were architectural ambiguities the synthesis could narrow but only a source-level read could resolve — and source-level reading was a downstream-phase affordance, not a synthesis-phase one. Some were housekeeping corrections to the synthesis' own phrasing. A few, on closer examination, turned out to be non-findings that deserved retraction rather than action.

A single flat list of these items would have been unusable. The observations differed not just in content but in kind: they called for different decisions, from different parties, with different levels of urgency, and against different evidence thresholds. Appending them to the map would have contaminated the map's technical register. Scattering them as ad-hoc messages would have produced a shallow review on individually shallow inputs. Neither approach honored the structure the observations actually had.

The response was a dedicated flags document, consolidated from the synthesis' output and reviewed as its own artifact. The document's function was to hold candidate items long enough for each to be classified, surrounded with context, and walked through one at a time with the engagement lead. Its audience was narrow — the engagement lead first, the drafting thread after. Its lifespan was short — once each flag was decided, retracted, or handed off to the downstream phase, the document's work was done. The artifact itself was the review's staging ground, not a deliverable in the durable sense the handoff documents and map were.

## 2. Classification Scheme

Before the review could begin, each flag was tagged with a classification describing the kind of decision it called for. Classification was done by the drafting thread, on synthesis, before the engagement lead saw the document. This staging had a purpose: the classification told the engagement lead, at a glance, what kind of response was expected on each item, which in turn calibrated the depth of attention each flag warranted.

The classifications were kept distinct from one another. A flag carried exactly one. The distinctions were functional — what decision the flag called for — not thematic. Two flags about the same underlying question could carry different classifications depending on where each flag sat in its own resolution lifecycle.

| Classification | What it means | What is asked of the engagement lead |
|---|---|---|
| Safe to act on | Evidence sufficient for an immediate mechanical change. No investigation required, no judgment call, no risk from acting. | Confirm the change and, where a small choice is embedded (e.g., picking among equivalent alternatives), select one. |
| Needs validation | Evidence suggests something, but source-level confirmation is the appropriate bar before acting. Tree-report structural signals alone do not rise to the decision threshold. | Decide whether to act now on the synthesis evidence or defer acting until the downstream phase confirms. |
| Requires engagement-lead decision | The path forward depends on a scope, policy, or stakeholder-routing call that the drafting thread cannot make. | Make the call, or defer it to a named future moment. |
| Downstream-phase investigation | The source-access phase of work is the right venue for resolution. The flag is surfaced so the downstream phase knows to investigate; no synthesis-phase action is being asked. | Confirm the item is correctly routed to the downstream phase and note it for the kickoff materials. |
| Factual observation | A finding worth recording for context. No action is being asked. The flag exists so the observation lives in the record rather than evaporating. | Acknowledge. Optionally, note where in the handoff materials the observation should be reflected. |
| Housekeeping | An internal correction to the synthesis' own phrasing, classification, or epistemic register. The drafting thread can apply it unilaterally; the flag surfaces it for transparency. | Note and acknowledge. |
| Withdrawn | A flag previously surfaced that, on review, did not hold up. Retraction is recorded so the history of the flag's existence is preserved even as the flag itself ceases to be active. | Acknowledge; confirm the retraction's reasoning. |

The classifications map onto a gradient of epistemic confidence and scope of authority. Safe to act on and housekeeping sit at one end: the drafting thread has both the evidence and the authority, and the engagement lead's role is light oversight. Requires engagement-lead decision sits at the far end: the drafting thread has neither sufficient authority nor, typically, sufficient evidence to decide alone. Needs validation and downstream-phase investigation sit in between, distinguished by whether the next resolution step is a fresh piece of evidence or a decision about timing. Factual observation sits off the axis of action entirely — it is a record-keeping category. Withdrawn is a terminal state, applied retroactively to flags that do not survive scrutiny.

Classifying before review is cheaper than discovering the classification during review. A review that opens each flag cold spends its first minutes figuring out what the flag is asking for. A classified flag enters the review with that orientation complete. The engagement lead can then use their attention on the judgment the flag actually wants, not on locating the judgment point inside the flag's prose.

## 3. The Walking-Through Pattern

The review was conducted one flag at a time, in an order chosen by the drafting thread, with full context supplied per flag. Full context meant, at minimum: the flag's short name, its classification, the evidence the synthesis had surfaced in its favor, the counter-evidence or uncertainty that kept the flag from being self-resolving, the decision being requested, and the party to whom the decision belonged.

The order was not arbitrary. Flags that would unblock other flags came first. A naming-collision flag whose resolution affected a handoff document's prose came before flags whose resolution required reading that same document; a library-versus-application flag that gated updates to two other handoff documents came before the batch of flags that depended on it. Decision-blocking flags came before confirmations. Housekeeping items — which the drafting thread would apply unilaterally — came at the end as acknowledgments, not decisions.

Each flag was walked through with its full context presented before any decision was sought. The context was paragraph-length prose, not a bulleted summary. The evidence was named at the level of specific files and specific structural observations. Counter-evidence was stated with the same specificity. If the flag referenced a handoff document, the relevant section of that document was named. If the flag's resolution depended on another flag, the dependency was stated.

Presenting the flag in this shape meant the engagement lead's decision was made against the actual material, not against a label. A flag reduced to its short name — "chassis gap" — is a menu item. A flag reduced to classification plus short name — "needs validation: chassis gap" — is an index entry. A flag presented with its evidence, counter-evidence, and decision request is a decision. The review treated the distinction as load-bearing. Walking a batch of flags at once, even of the same classification, would have compressed the decisions into surface-level calls — acknowledgment rather than judgment. Decisions that deserved judgment received judgment; decisions that were surface-level confirmations were grouped and batched at the end of the walk-through so the cognitive cost matched the decision weight.

## 4. Why Full Context Per Flag

The drafting thread's role in the review was to surface enough material for the engagement lead's judgment to be well-founded. The engagement lead's role was to exercise judgment the drafting thread could not — either because the judgment required authority outside the drafting thread's scope, or because it required context about the engagement's commercial, political, or stakeholder shape that sat above the drafting thread's aperture.

The division of labor presumed a handoff: the drafting thread hands over evidence and framing; the engagement lead supplies judgment. Shortcutting the evidence or the framing defeated the handoff. An engagement lead handed a terse summary without evidence must either trust the drafting thread's implicit reasoning or re-derive the evidence to verify it. Neither is a good option. Trusting without seeing the evidence abdicates the judgment role. Re-deriving from scratch is a waste of the context the drafting thread has already built.

Full paragraphs of context per flag are therefore not a courtesy. They are the mechanism by which the engagement lead's decision rests on the same material the drafting thread's framing rests on. The decision and the evidence meet in the same place. When the engagement lead decides, the decision references the evidence; when the drafting thread records the decision, it records it against the same evidence frame. The review's output is internally consistent because its inputs were presented in a shape that made consistency mechanical rather than effortful.

The pattern also protects against a subtle failure mode: framings that look innocuous at summary length but contain hidden assumptions at full length. A one-line flag description can carry an embedded conclusion that neither the drafting thread nor the engagement lead notices because the conclusion is folded into the noun choice. The same flag at full-paragraph length exposes the noun choice to scrutiny. The walking-through pattern, by forcing every flag into full-length presentation, made the assumptions visible and therefore challengeable.

## 5. The First Correction: Scope Posture

Not every flag in the initial document was framed correctly. Early in the walk-through, the engagement lead identified a systemic drift in how the flags had been written.

Several flags — particularly those classified as requires engagement-lead decision or needs validation — contained drafting-thread recommendations. Phrases like "recommendation leans toward option A because it mirrors the branding," or "best guess, by volume and recency, is the newest generation." Some flags included investigation plans addressed to the downstream phase, prescribing the order in which the downstream phase should open specific files and the questions it should answer from each. Some flags carried an implicit "my lean" voice, as though the drafting thread were a participant in the decision rather than the party surfacing context for someone else's decision.

The correction was clear and it was immediate. The drafting thread's role is to surface starting context. The engagement lead decides where a decision is needed; the downstream phase designs its own investigation where the investigation is its responsibility. Recommendations from the drafting thread on questions it cannot decide authoritatively are overreach. Investigation plans written by the drafting thread for the downstream phase are also overreach, because the downstream phase has source access and the drafting thread does not — the downstream phase is better positioned than the drafting thread to decide what reads are most efficient, in what order, and with what stopping criteria.

After the correction, the review continued with the remaining flags reframed. Each flag's evidence-for and counter-evidence-or-uncertainty stayed in place; those are observations, which the drafting thread is positioned to provide. Recommendations were removed. Investigation plans were stripped or, where genuinely useful as starting context rather than prescription, rewritten as "items the downstream phase will likely consider" rather than "steps the downstream phase should take." The decision-request section was pared back to a clear statement of what choice was being asked and of whom. The "my lean" language was removed in its entirety.

The correction was not only applied to the flags under review. It was lifted into the operating-model refinements captured at the end of the review, because the drift was systemic and the remedy had to be structural, not item-by-item.

## 6. Worked Examples

Three flags from the review illustrate the pattern in operation. One is a safe-to-act-on flag that resolved cleanly with a small mechanical choice. One is a needs-validation flag that the engagement lead resolved with a third option the drafting thread had not surfaced. One is a flag that was withdrawn on review because its premise did not hold.

### 6.1 A Naming Collision

The synthesis observed that the word "classic," which the handoff package had adopted as shorthand for the rebuild theme, already carried a meaning in the modern codebase's design system. Two stylesheet files in the shell repository used the term as a tier name — the design system's own light and dark variants named with a "-classic" suffix. Adopting "classic" as the rebuild's class prefix, directory name, or theme identifier would have collided with this existing usage on every file search, every code review, and every conversation about the rebuild's CSS.

The flag was classified safe to act on. The evidence was sufficient — the collision was observable in the tree reports, the consequence of not correcting it was predictable, and the remedy was mechanical. The flag named three candidate prefixes as alternatives: a product-branded prefix that tied to the legacy product's name, a generic prefix that named the classic UI role, and a legacy-view prefix. The flag stopped short of selecting among them; the drafting thread, after the scope-posture correction, did not state a preference.

The engagement lead confirmed an alternate prefix — the product-branded one — and that selection flowed immediately into the corrections pass on the relevant handoff document. The document's theme-toggle section was updated in place to use the selected prefix throughout, with a dated footer note recording the change and the reason. The flag was then marked resolved in the flags document. Resolution was recorded, not silently applied; the flag's history stayed visible in the artifact even after its active state closed.

### 6.2 A Library Versus an Application

A second flag described an architectural ambiguity. The modern side's primary working repository had, in the handoff narrative, been described as the primary application for the classic rebuild — the place where the rebuild's feature pages would live. The tree-report extraction, read fresh, told a different story. The repository's layout suggested a publishable Angular library, not an application: its projects directory used the library-package convention, a public-API file exported the library's surface, and the repository contained no main entry point, no root HTML file, and no root application module at the expected locations. The existing alarms UI for the modern product, further, lived not in this repository but in the shell repository one level up.

The flag was classified needs validation. The evidence was strong — the tree-report signals aligned consistently with a library shape — but the handoff narrative also had weight, because it reflected a conversation with a stakeholder who had pointed at this repository as the primary work target. The two readings were not necessarily in conflict; a stakeholder saying "work happens on this UI" could reasonably describe a library where feature pages live, even when the routes and navigation that mount those pages register in a shell application one repository away. The flag asked a two-part decision: whether the drafting thread should update the two handoff documents that currently assumed the application interpretation, and whether that update should happen now based on tree-report evidence or later after source-level confirmation.

The engagement lead's resolution was neither of the two options as posed. The path taken was a third: update both handoff documents, but update them to present both readings as equal-weight unresolved inputs rather than committing to either. The library reading would appear in the documents as the synthesis-observed shape; the application reading would appear as the handoff-narrative shape; both would be labeled unresolved; the downstream phase, with source access, would close the question by opening the library's packaging manifests and the shell's dependency manifest and confirming the actual publication-and-consumption relationship. The resolution and its reasoning — that the drafting thread's evidence was strong but not conclusive, that the stakeholder statement could be reconciled with either shape, and that letting the question close on source was the lowest-regret path — went into dated footer notes on both handoff documents.

This flag's resolution also surfaced a secondary cleanup. A related flag in the review document had been written as "depends on the library-versus-application flag," stating that if the library interpretation held, targeted paragraphs in two handoff documents would need correction. With the parent flag's resolution — present both readings as equal-weight inputs — the dependent flag's action was folded into the same edit pass. Rather than a conditional "update if the library reading holds," the edit was a definite "present both readings in both documents with footer notes." The dependent flag was closed with a note pointing at the parent flag's resolution.

### 6.3 A Retraction

A third flag, originally surfaced during the synthesis, described an architectural contradiction on the modern backend side. A specific programming language, referenced in passing in a walkthrough transcript that sat inside the handoff-package context, appeared nowhere in the modern side's tree reports. Several backend repositories in the modern set were in another language entirely; the referenced language simply did not surface. The synthesis had noted this as a possible architectural gap: the walkthrough mentioned the language, and no evidence of the language existed in the swept repositories.

The engagement lead pointed out, on review, that the flag was a category error. The engagement is a user-interface rebuild. The backend stack underneath the modern product is out of scope regardless of what languages and services it contains. Hardware-integration backend work, device-management backend work, and any service implementation-language choices that do not touch the user-interface rebuild are not the engagement's concern. The walkthrough reference that the synthesis had latched onto was an incidental detail in a transcript about the product's broader architecture; it was not a commitment or a scope inclusion.

The flag was withdrawn. The retraction was recorded in the flags document with a short note stating who retracted it, why, and when. The synthesis' own record was updated: the map's cross-cutting-findings section was scrubbed of references to the language question, and the dated footer notes on any handoff documents that had picked up the reference were updated to remove it. The running findings log recorded the retraction as well, so that a later reader working back through the log could see that the finding had been surfaced, considered, and retracted, rather than silently disappearing.

The retraction illustrated two points of methodology. First, not every cross-cutting observation the synthesis surfaces is a finding; some are artifacts of the synthesis drawing too broad a circle around its scope. Second, retraction is a first-class state, not a cleanup activity. The retracted flag stays in the document, marked retracted, rather than being deleted. Its history is part of the review's record and part of the drafting thread's calibration for future synthesis work.

## 7. The Corrections Applied

Walking through the flags produced a set of concrete corrections that the drafting thread then applied across the affected artifacts. The corrections were applied in a single pass following the review, so that the handoff materials reached a consistent post-review state rather than being updated incrementally across overlapping reviews.

A handoff document's theme-toggle section was updated to use the engagement-lead-selected prefix throughout. The change was a search-and-replace within the document's relevant section, followed by an eye-over for places where the prefix appeared embedded in prose rather than in code-shaped identifiers. A dated footer note at the bottom of the document recorded the change, the reason, and the flag from which the decision came.

The technical-landscape handoff document and the repositories-and-access handoff document were updated to present the library-versus-application question as equal-weight unresolved inputs. The edits to each document included: the section originally describing the repository as the primary working application was rewritten to describe the apparent library-versus-application ambiguity, to state the evidence on each side, and to route the resolution to the downstream phase. Both documents received dated footer notes recording the change.

References to the retracted backend-language question were removed from every document where the synthesis had inserted them. This included the map's cross-cutting-findings section, a dated footer note in the technical-landscape document that had mentioned the question, and the flags document itself, where the retraction was recorded in place rather than by deletion.

Two classifications in the synthesis' own open-questions disposition were corrected. The synthesis had marked two handoff-open-questions as "resolved" on the basis of tree-report file-name evidence. The review corrected these to "strong signal, needs source-read verification" — the rationale being that a file name is evidence of where the answer lives, not the answer itself. Until the downstream phase opens the file and enumerates the contents, the question is refined rather than closed. The map's section on open-question disposition was updated in place, and the handoff document's open-questions section continued to list the items as open, because the strong-signal status had not yet closed them.

Each correction preserved the chain of reasoning. A reader opening any affected document after the review could see what had changed and why, without having to cross-reference the flags document to reconstruct the edit's justification. The footer-note convention was the mechanism: dated, reason-stated, flag-referenced.

## 8. Operating-Model Refinements That Emerged

The review produced per-flag decisions. It also produced a set of refinements to how the drafting thread was expected to operate on future work — generalizations lifted from the review's observed patterns into explicit standing policies. These refinements were captured in the kickoff-context document, so that future sessions and future contributors would work under the clarified posture.

**No drafting-thread recommendations on non-drafting-thread decisions.** Where a question is the engagement lead's to decide or the downstream phase's to resolve, the drafting thread presents the evidence and the framing without stating a preference. "My lean" language is removed. Recommendations are removed. Option enumerations are kept, because option enumeration helps the decider see the space; but the enumeration does not rank the options.

**No investigation plans written for the downstream phase.** The downstream phase has source access and designs its own investigation. The drafting thread may surface items the downstream phase will plausibly want to read, framed as context rather than prescription, but it does not write step-by-step investigation sequences. The downstream phase is the authority on its own working plan.

**Observation versus conclusion, in every flag.** The rule is already operative for the map; the review extended it to every flag the drafting thread raises. Evidence is observation. Interpretation of evidence into a claim about implementation is conclusion. A flag that conflates the two — by presenting a name-suggested role as a structural fact, or by presenting an apparent gap as a confirmed gap — fails the test. The review's pattern of separating evidence-for from counter-evidence-or-uncertainty is the minimum frame; richer flags extend the pattern into the recommended-action section as well, explicitly noting where a proposed action depends on a conclusion rather than an observation.

**Record everything; rely on the log, not on ephemeral conversation.** Flags are captured in the flags document. Decisions are recorded against the flag. Retractions are recorded in place. Corrections are recorded with dated footer notes on the affected artifacts. The running findings log captures the reasoning trail. Nothing significant lives only in conversational memory. If an item is raised, captured, decided, retracted, or deferred, the state of it exists in a file that a later contributor can read.

**Handoff documents are living but tracked.** Edits are applied in place, not held in parallel drafts. A dated footer note records what changed, when, and why. Silent rewrites are not permitted. This convention matches the engagement's broader posture of favoring artifacts with visible history over artifacts whose current state is the only state.

These refinements were not invented by the review; several had been implicit in the engagement's existing working conventions. The review made them explicit, named them as standing policy, and wrote them into the kickoff-context document so that the drafting thread's future operation could be checked against them.

## 9. Output Artifacts

The review produced a consolidated set of artifacts that together represented the engagement's post-review state.

The flags document itself, after the walk-through, held each flag with its classification, its evidence, its decision (if any), and its resolution status. Withdrawn flags remained in the document, marked as retracted with a short note on the retraction's reasoning. Resolved flags remained, marked as resolved with a short note on the decision and its date. Open flags — those that had been deferred to the downstream phase for resolution — remained, marked as open with a pointer to the phase expected to close them. The document's summary table was updated to reflect the post-review states.

The handoff documents affected by the review were updated in place. The theme-toggle section of the relevant document carried the new prefix throughout, with a dated footer note. The technical-landscape and repositories-and-access documents presented the library-versus-application question as equal-weight unresolved inputs, also with dated footer notes. The scrubbing of the retracted backend-language references was complete across all affected documents.

The running findings log received new entries capturing the review's cross-cutting observations and the operating-model refinements the review produced. The log retained its function as the reasoning trail, so that a later contributor could see not only what the handoff documents said but how they had arrived at what they said.

The kickoff-context document, which had been accumulating context for the downstream phase's handoff, was updated with the operating-model clarifications that emerged from the review. The scope-posture correction, the rule against drafting-thread recommendations on non-drafting-thread decisions, the rule against drafting-thread investigation plans for the downstream phase, and the record-everything discipline all appeared in the kickoff-context document under a section describing the engagement's operating model.

With these artifacts updated, the handoff materials reached a ready state. The downstream phase's kickoff could proceed against a set of documents that had passed the flag review, were consistent with one another, carried visible histories for any recent changes, and presented the unresolved questions as unresolved rather than as prematurely closed. The next phase of the engagement — the production of kickoff and handoff materials for the downstream phase itself — could now begin.

## 10. Why the Pattern Matters Methodologically

A synthesis phase produces findings. The findings span a spectrum from those that are safe to act on immediately to those that genuinely depend on a decision from outside the drafting thread's authority. Somewhere in the middle sit findings that need source-level validation, findings that require a scope or policy call, and findings that belong in the downstream phase's investigation plan rather than being resolvable in the synthesis itself.

Mixing these into a flat list is wasteful in both directions. A decision-maker looking at a flat list spends attention on routine corrections that the drafting thread could have applied alone, and under-spends attention on the items where judgment is actually required. The drafting thread, in turn, either over-escalates (asking for decisions on items within its own authority) or under-escalates (silently acting on items that should have routed to the engagement lead). Both failure modes degrade the review.

Classifying flags before review — deciding, for each, what kind of decision it calls for — resolves the mixing problem. The classifier does the triage once; the review then proceeds with the triage already in place. Items that the drafting thread can act on alone are acted on alone and surfaced only for transparency. Items that require engagement-lead judgment receive engagement-lead attention. Items that belong to the downstream phase are routed there without being mistakenly decided in the synthesis.

Walking the flags one at a time, with full context, sharpens the pattern further. Each flag receives the attention its classification implies. A safe-to-act-on flag receives a brief confirmation. A requires-engagement-lead-decision flag receives the full paragraph of context and the deliberate judgment that the context supports. A withdrawn flag is recognized as a category error and retracted cleanly. The cost of attention is calibrated to the weight of the decision.

The pattern generalizes. Any synthesis phase that hands findings to a review phase benefits from the same structure: classify before review, walk through one at a time with full context, separate the drafting side's authority from the deciding side's authority, record everything in place, and preserve retractions alongside decisions. The engagement used the pattern for a specific set of synthesis findings in a specific review. The pattern itself is reusable any time a synthesis phase produces outputs that will be consumed by a decision-making phase downstream.
