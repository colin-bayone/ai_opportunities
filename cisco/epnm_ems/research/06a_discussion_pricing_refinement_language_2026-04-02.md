## The Problem

The $500K was estimated without code access. We had to make assumptions about per-screen complexity, backend state, and codebase structure based on conversations only. Those assumptions are stated on the pricing proposal.

We want Guhan to understand:
1. This is a realistic, good-faith estimate based on what we know today
2. Discovery will give us real data to refine the estimate
3. Refinement could go in either direction (less complex = lower, more complex = same or higher)
4. The biggest driver of cost reduction is timeline flexibility, not scope cuts
5. Fewer resources may be possible once we understand the codebase

We do NOT want Guhan to think:
- The price is padded and will definitely come down
- We are uncertain about our pricing and don't trust our own numbers
- Budget approval should wait until after discovery
- We are leaving the door open for a significant price drop
- This is wishy-washy or non-committal pricing

## The Tension

We want to be honest partners ("this could be refined") without undermining our own price ("so don't commit to it yet"). The framing has to land as confidence with transparency, not hedging.

## Colin's Guidance

- We are being asked to assess per-screen effort without having seen the code
- We can refine the quote at the end of discovery, but as a change request (not a renegotiation)
- We will take every opportunity to expedite and provide higher value
- We cannot lowball ourselves; things could be more complex than assumed
- They could also be less complex, but we should not lead with that expectation
- The biggest cost driver that could change is timeline (fewer resources if we understand the codebase better)
- We should come across as partners saying "here's a realistic number; it could adjust slightly once we have real data, but this is a solid basis for planning"

## Framing Principles

1. **Lead with confidence in the number.** The $500K is the planning number. It is not a placeholder or a rough guess. It is based on real methodology and experience with similar conversions.

2. **Position discovery as calibration, not re-pricing.** Discovery refines our understanding of complexity. If the codebase is simpler than assumed, there may be an opportunity to adjust. If it is more complex, the pricing holds because the risk reserve accounts for that.

3. **Any adjustment flows through the change request process.** This is already in the proposal. Refinement is not renegotiation. It is a formal process triggered by new information, not by procurement pressure.

4. **The biggest lever is timeline, not price.** If the codebase turns out to be simpler, the most likely outcome is faster delivery (which benefits Cisco), not a lower price. We can potentially do it with fewer parallel resources over the same timeline, or the same resources over a shorter timeline.

5. **Do not volunteer a range.** Saying "it could be $400-500K" gives procurement $400K as the anchor. The number is $500K. If discovery reveals simplification opportunities, we will proactively share that. But the starting commitment is $500K.

6. **Frame conservatism as responsible, not excessive.** "We estimated conservatively because we have not yet seen the codebase. This protects both parties from scope surprises. Once we have access, we will have a clearer picture within two weeks."

## Language to Use (Draft, Needs Refinement)

For the breakdown document or verbal conversation:

"This engagement is priced at $500,000 based on our current understanding of the EPNM and EMS architectures as discussed with your team. Since we have not yet had access to the codebase, our estimates are calibrated conservatively to account for complexity that is not visible from documentation or conversation alone. This approach protects both BayOne and Cisco from unexpected scope challenges.

During the discovery phase, we will gain firsthand understanding of the codebase structure and screen complexity. If that analysis reveals opportunities to streamline delivery, we will share those findings proactively. Any formal adjustments would follow the change request process outlined in our proposal.

We are committed to delivering the highest value within this engagement and will look for every opportunity to accelerate the timeline and maximize efficiency as our understanding deepens."

## What This Language Does

- Says the price is solid and reasoned (not guessed)
- Explains WHY it's conservative (no code access yet) without apologizing for it
- Positions any future adjustment as BayOne proactively sharing good news, not Cisco negotiating down
- Keeps the change request process as the mechanism (formal, not casual)
- Leads with "protecting both parties" so conservatism is collaborative, not self-serving
- Does NOT say "the price could come down" or give a lower number
- Does NOT say "we'll know the real price after discovery"
- Does NOT invite waiting for discovery before committing

## Where This Language Goes

This framing should appear in:
1. The pricing breakdown document (as a brief note, not a long paragraph)
2. Colin's verbal communication with Guhan
3. Any follow-up email about the pricing

It should NOT be the lead. It should come after the four-bucket breakdown, almost as a closing note that reinforces partnership and transparency.
