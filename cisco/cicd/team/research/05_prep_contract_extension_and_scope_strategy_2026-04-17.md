## 1. The Trigger: Saurav's Scope-Drift Concern

Midway through the prep meeting, Saurav raised a concern that had been building through the week's discoveries. He observed that Cisco is running many parallel repositories, tools, and side projects, and that the team "just keep[s] building them on." His specific worry: the CI/CD project the BayOne team has been actively scoped around was never mentioned in the first conversations. The knowledge-graph work that is now dominating the conversation only surfaced in the last two weeks. Saurav's fear was explicit: tomorrow a different Cisco stakeholder could arrive, point at yet another newly built tool, and redirect the BayOne team onto that while abandoning the work already in flight.

This is the pivot point of the meeting. Colin's response moved through two deliberately sequenced parts: a "scary" commercial framing first, then the good news that neutralizes the fear. He flagged the sequence explicitly before delivering it: "I'm going to say something scary and then I'm going to say something good for us."

---

## 2. The "Scary" Commercial Framing (Colin's Scope-Change Strategy)

Colin's first move was to reframe the scope-drift risk in commercial rather than technical terms. The engineering team sees the drift as frustrating and confusing. Colin's position is that the drift is a contractual problem, and that BayOne has a standard vendor playbook for it.

### 2.1 Deliver-to-Deliverables Principle

Colin reminded the team of a structural truth about how BayOne's solutions engagements work: payment is tied to delivery of what the contract specifies. This is not a salaried internal arrangement. If Cisco does not define the scope cleanly, Colin cannot turn around and justify BayOne's invoices to procurement. The question procurement will inevitably ask is: "Have you delivered what you said you'd deliver?" If the goalposts have moved throughout the quarter, that conversation becomes very difficult to win.

### 2.2 The Change Request Lever

Colin stated explicitly that he will carry this framing into the afternoon meeting with Srinivas. His position:

- If Srinivas wants to redirect the team onto new work, that work must be attached to the defined goals.
- If the scope keeps twisting, Colin's response will be the vendor-standard move: put a change request in place, because the new work is not what was agreed to.
- Adding scope means adding money. Cisco cannot shift goalposts without a commercial adjustment. "If they're going to add on scope like that, we're going to need more money from them, frankly, because they can't just change the goalposts."

### 2.3 The "If They Had Their Stuff Together" Framing

Colin was blunt with his internal team about Cisco's posture: "If they don't have their stuff together, they shouldn't have signed a contract." The purpose of this framing is not to insult the client but to establish the mental stance the BayOne team should carry. Cisco is the party that put a scope on paper. If Cisco cannot hold that scope together, that is Cisco's problem to resolve via change request, not BayOne's problem to absorb silently.

### 2.4 Not Backwards Accountable

The strongest phrase Colin used: "We are not going to be backwards accountable for them not having an actual plan for this." The team should not internalize Cisco's planning gaps as their own failure. The engagement cannot be run as "work on this for five minutes this week and then work on something else next week." That cadence may function for salaried Cisco engineers, but it does not work for a contracted delivery team that must map hours to artifacts.

### 2.5 Not Loaned Employees

Colin made the distinction explicit for the team: BayOne did not come into Cisco as employees loaned into Srinivas's team. The engagement exists around a specific objective and a specific purpose. Srinivas does not have free rein to redirect those resources. If he tries, the vendor playbook applies.

## 3. The Good News: Anand's Contract Extension

After delivering the hard commercial framing, Colin shifted to the news that transforms the BayOne team's strategic position. The day prior (Thursday, April 16), Colin met with Anand (Srinivas's boss). Anand granted the contract extension on the spot.

### 3.1 What Anand Granted

- A full next-quarter extension, covering the entire following three months.
- Anand indicated he would have signed a full-year scope, but chose to hold to three months explicitly to give the access-issue problems time to resolve before committing beyond that.
- The spirit of Anand's message: Cisco knows there are problems. The BayOne team is doing good work. Three more months is the floor, with more likely to follow once the access situation clears.

### 3.2 Colin's Reaction

Colin's honest assessment to the team: "I wasn't expecting it to be that easy." The extension came without pushback, without a negotiation, and without conditions attached to deliverable milestones. Anand proactively acknowledged the problems on the Cisco side rather than pinning them on BayOne.

### 3.3 What the Extension Implies About Cisco's Self-Awareness

Anand's posture confirms something Colin has suspected. Cisco's leadership already knows: "They know that there's problems. They know that the process isn't there. They know that it's all over the place." The extension is effectively Cisco buying runway to get its own house in order while BayOne continues to deliver where it can.

## 4. The Accountability Chain

This is the most strategically important piece of Colin's framing and the one that most directly shapes how he will handle Srinivas on the call.

### 4.1 Colin Reports to Anand, Not Srinivas

Colin was unambiguous with the team: "It's not really so much about listening to what Srinivas says, because at the end of the day, I'm not accountable to Srinivas, I'm accountable to his boss." The contractual accountability runs to Anand. Srinivas is the day-to-day engineering counterpart, but he does not control the commercial relationship.

### 4.2 The Escalation Path

If Srinivas issues instructions that fold back on themselves or that pivot constantly, Colin's move is to do what any vendor would do: file a change request, re-scope the project, require additional funding for additional work. If Srinivas refuses to align around the originally contracted purpose, Colin raises that to Anand directly. Anand has already signaled openness to this by extending the contract unprompted.

### 4.3 The Strategic Implication for the Team

The team should not feel pressured to accept every redirection Srinivas proposes in the moment. The BayOne engagement has a defined purpose, and Colin owns the boundary on that purpose. Engineers on the team can engage technically with whatever Srinivas raises without committing BayOne to absorb it as in-scope.

---

## 5. The "Six Items Originally Agreed On"

Colin referenced the contracted deliverables explicitly as the reference point for any scope conversation with Srinivas: "How do we make this align with those six items that we originally agreed on? Because if we're going to do completely different work, then that contract needs re-scoped."

The posture Colin will carry into the Srinivas meeting:

- Start by mapping whatever Srinivas is asking about back to the six originally contracted items.
- If it maps, proceed.
- If it does not map, that is not a blocker for Cisco but a commercial event for Cisco. "That doesn't affect us at all. That just means that they're going to have to pay more money because you are not allowed to do that to contractors."

The language "that doesn't affect us at all" is important. It signals that Colin is not negotiating from fear. Either the work stays in scope and BayOne delivers, or the work expands and Cisco pays more. Both outcomes are acceptable to BayOne.

## 6. Assessment of Cisco's Internal Posture

Colin shared his read of Cisco's engineering maturity to help the team calibrate expectations.

### 6.1 Rui's Code and the "Discovered Fire" Framing

When Colin looked at Rui's existing Nexus-T code (the CI/CD repo Srinivas shared access to), his read was that Rui's team had recently gotten Codex (Colin self-corrected from Copilot to Codex) and was in the early excitement phase with it. His characterization: "This is a team that got Copilot, or excuse me, Codex for the first time, and is like, oh my gosh, I've discovered fire."

Translation: Cisco's internal teams are still operating in POC mode on all of this work. They are not running a disciplined production-engineering effort. They are experimenting, seeing what sticks, and naming things inconsistently (Pulse, Scribble, Scribbler, Scrubber all appeared as names for related or overlapping pieces during the week).

### 6.2 What This Means for the Meeting

Because Cisco is in POC mode, Srinivas is unlikely to have crisp answers to scope questions. He is genuinely figuring things out in real time. This is not malice; it is immaturity of the engagement on Cisco's side. The BayOne response to this is not to get frustrated, but to hold the contractual line while remaining technically helpful.

### 7.1 The "Healthy" Framing

Colin's closing summary: "We are already good for the next quarter anyway, which is great. ... It's good for us. We're already healthy. We're okay." The team should approach the Srinivas meeting from a position of commercial strength, not commercial fragility.

## 8. Strategic Implications for the Srinivas Meeting

### 8.1 BayOne Has the Runway to Hold the Line

The contract extension secured the commercial foundation for the next quarter before the Srinivas meeting happens. Colin can take a firm stance on scope without risking the engagement. This is the critical asymmetry: Srinivas likely does not yet know that Anand has already extended, which means Srinivas has less leverage than he thinks he has while Colin has more leverage than Srinivas expects.

### 8.2 The Posture for the Meeting

- Engage technically and collaboratively on whatever Srinivas raises.
- Map everything back to the six original deliverables.
- For anything that does not map, flag it as a potential change-request item rather than silently absorbing it.
- Do not argue with Srinivas about scope in the moment. The escalation path to Anand exists if Srinivas will not align.

### 8.3 The Rui Overlap Question

Colin will push Srinivas for clarity on what to do about Rui's existing Nexus-T work: is Rui still actively developing it, is he handing it off to BayOne, or is BayOne expected to collaborate with him? This ambiguity has existed since the engagement started (Srinivas was supposed to connect BayOne to Rui at the beginning and it was delayed by over a month). This is the specific instance where Colin will test whether Srinivas can give a clean answer or whether the pattern continues.

### 8.4 The Access-Blocking Leverage

The deep-site access issue (permanent ADS machine, now roughly four weeks unresolved since first committed in the week of March 23rd) gives Colin a concrete, defensible reason to draw a line. BayOne has followed the process, escalated to Mahavir, and followed up with Srinivas three times during the week. The process itself is broken on Cisco's side. Colin will use this as the explicit justification for why deliverables have not advanced as far as Cisco might expect, pre-empting any suggestion that BayOne has underperformed. This reinforces the "not backwards accountable" stance without making it adversarial.

### 8.5 The Team Should Not Fear Pushing Back

The sub-message Colin was delivering to his own team in this portion of the meeting: when you engage with Srinivas, you do not need to accept every redirection as a commitment. The commercial layer is handled. You can focus on doing good engineering work without absorbing anxiety about the scope keeping moving. That is Colin's problem to manage, and he has the lever to manage it.

## 9. Summary of Colin's Strategic Frame

The meeting crystallized a five-part posture that Colin will carry into the Srinivas call:

1. **Deliver-to-deliverables.** BayOne gets paid for contracted work. Period.
2. **Change request for scope shifts.** New scope equals new money. Standard vendor move.
3. **Not backwards accountable.** Cisco's planning gaps are Cisco's problem, not BayOne's burden.
4. **Accountability to Anand.** Srinivas is the daily counterpart; Anand is the contractual boss. Escalation path is real and already warm.
5. **Commercial runway secured.** The quarter extension means BayOne can hold the line without existential risk to the engagement.

This is the frame that will determine how Colin hears every scope suggestion Srinivas raises in the afternoon meeting. It is invisible to Srinivas and must stay invisible.
