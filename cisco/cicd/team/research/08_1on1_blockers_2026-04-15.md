## 1. Primary Blocker: Saurav's Cisco MacBook Dead After macOS Update

The dominant blocker discussed in this session is the complete failure of Saurav's Cisco-issued MacBook, which went dead over the weekend following a macOS update. The ticket of record is INC10796337, previously flagged as Priority 5 in Team Set 02e. Saurav's account of the Cisco IT response reflects poorly on the service desk's handling. He reached out to Cisco support shortly after the failure. There was no dedicated channel for hardware issues, so support created a case and placed it in a queue. Roughly a full day passed before the case was assigned to a first representative. That representative escalated to a second representative, and the second day was consumed entirely by what Saurav described as "basic troubleshooting" consisting of the power-and-hold sequence and a MagSafe cable swap. Saurav had already exhausted these steps before opening the ticket, including walking through them with Claude. In his words: "2 days are gone with nothing, and then on the third day, they are like... Yeah, come to our office."

The Cisco IT verdict delivered on April 15 is that repair will take one to two months (Saurav's account: "it might take at least a month or at max two or three months"), with a loaner laptop offered in the meantime. Saurav was instructed to come in person to the Cisco office to collect the loaner and to submit his current dead MacBook. A Cisco IT contact number was provided at the desk; Saurav pinged that contact and waited roughly an hour and a half with no reply, then followed up asking for pickup timing and whether he should bring the dead laptop or expect home pickup. As of the call, no response had been received.

The policy root cause surfaced in the IT conversation. Saurav was classified as a "red badge employee," which the support representative explained means contracted employee. Per Cisco policy, red-badge contractors receive refurbished laptops, and Cisco caps the repair cost allowable on that refurbished hardware. If the repair cost exceeds the cap, the loaner simply converts into the permanent replacement. Saurav's phrasing: "if like the repair is what you call, they are not ready to do the repairs because of the cost or whatever thing, then like they will transfer the loaner as like my laptop."

Lost work is non-trivial. Saurav had been running local development on the Cisco MacBook, and a weekend of work is currently stranded on the dead machine. Specific items named: research material for the WebEx Software Development Kit, the full Docker-to-Podman deployment conversion work, and architecture documentation for the DCN tools repository. Saurav's own words: "there were like research material on that, like for WebEx SDK, which we did like the whole Docker deployment and other things also, like, yeah, for repo also the documentation I did for the architecture and all the things surrounding the repo of DCN tools that was also on there. So, yeah, there were like a lot of things which might will make it easier if, like, we have current access, but yeah, those all are like kind of gone, I would suppose, at this point in time." Saurav is currently working from the BayOne-issued laptop only, with no Cisco environment access while the Cisco MacBook is offline.

## 3. Escalation Plan Colin Designed

Colin used the majority of the early session to construct the escalation playbook Saurav will execute. The mechanics are as follows.

Saurav will write a fact-driven email to Colin. The structure Colin specified: opening statement that an issue exists, day-by-day timeline of events, the Cisco IT verdict stated explicitly and formatted in bold, and a closing impact statement. Colin's direction on tone was to avoid editorial coloring: "don't add any color commentary to it. Just be very factual. Just say, hi, Colin, I'm having an issue. I wanted to let you know, you know, the history of this and, you know, kind of what's been going on. I'm unable to resolve it on my end." The one formatting emphasis Colin required is bolding the two-month repair turnaround, because that figure carries the argument on its own.

Colin will then forward the escalation upward to Srinivas Pitta and to Anand. The framing Colin will use in that forward is designed to present a binary: "either you lose a person here with no change order to the thing, or, you know, you do the right thing in the 1st place. That's unacceptable." Colin is explicit that he will be blunter with Anand in private and more diplomatic in the formal email, trusting that Anand will read between the lines.

The fallback position is timeboxed at one week. If Cisco has not resolved the situation within that window, Colin will exercise the Standard Approval Limit purchase option and instruct Cisco to reimburse. Colin's internal script for that conversation: "at this point, can I take it into my control and just buy the laptop under the SAL because your IT is incompetent?"

The expected outcome Colin is negotiating toward is threefold: a brand new machine for Saurav (not another refurbished unit), priority recovery of the dead machine treated as a data-extraction exercise only, and no return of the old machine to Saurav for ongoing use. Colin's phrasing: "I want you to get a brand new machine. I want them to make it a priority case to recover the old machine. I don't want you to get that one back. I just want them to give it to you so you can get the files off of it and then continue to use the new hardware."

Colin coached Saurav specifically on the impact statement at the bottom of the email, because Saurav's initial instinct was to minimize the loss on the grounds that he had also been working from his BayOne machine. Colin's correction: "don't be yourself here. I know yourself you're going to say, I already was working on in my Bay 1 machine, so I didn't really lose anything. Here, just say that." Colin then scripted the phrasing: "phrase it so that it sounds big. Don't just say like I lost some research, but say like I lost, you know, about, you know, this many days worth of work that was, you know, something for this. I will have to redo that work if it's not returned. That is an immediate item. Even if they come back in a week, it won't matter at that point because we'll have to, you know, have things. So it's, you can't just wait. You have to pretty much redo it."

The included-or-not dilemma concerned whether to name individual Cisco IT representatives in the escalation. Saurav was hesitant, reasoning that the representatives were following standard procedure and that an escalation to a Vice President could cause problems for them personally: "for them, it is like a regular thing, and certainly there is an escalation for your ticket to a VP, so yeah, that might be problematic for them." Colin's diplomatic coaching was that naming is appropriate when a representative has been actively unhelpful, since these representatives carry their own Service Level Agreements and being accountable is part of the role: "I would include them if that person has been a problem or unhelpful. Even if you don't want to get them in trouble, it's part of their job. They have SLAs themselves." The landing position, agreed between Colin and Saurav, is to include the ticket number (INC10796337) without naming individual representatives. This allows Cisco to pull the full case history on the back end if they choose, without Saurav feeling responsible for consequences to individual contributors.

## 6. Political Dynamics Around the Escalation

Colin is using the laptop incident as leverage for the broader engagement renewal conversation. The renewal window is imminent, and Colin's positioning is that this is an opportunity to make quality-of-service expectations explicit with both Srinivas and Anand before any further patience is tested. Colin's opening framing in the session: "I'm going to let Serenibus know about the state for the laptop. Just because we have that renewal coming up and I want him to be aware, you know, hey, these are silly things that have nothing to do with us."

The name-dropping resolution (ticket number yes, individual representatives no) reflects a deliberate compromise between Colin's preference for specificity and Saurav's concern for the individual IT contributors. Colin's principle is that specificity makes the escalation more real: "it makes it more real. So instead of saying like, I reached out to support, you could say I reached out to support and got a hold of, you know, person A on, you know, this day and time." Saurav's counter-principle is that the support representatives were operating within the constraints of a poorly designed process, not failing individually, and the escalation path to a Vice President carries consequences disproportionate to individual fault. The compromise preserves traceability through the ticket number while shielding individual representatives from named exposure.

Colin's broader posture toward the engagement's stakeholders is that BayOne will be a good partner on the record but will not absorb Cisco's operational failures silently. On Srinivas specifically, Colin is willing to be direct about action-item ownership. On Anand, Colin expects cooperative escalation support, with Anand reading the diplomatic framing and understanding the harder position underneath. On Cisco IT, Colin has concluded that the functional route to resolution runs through procurement override rather than through continued engagement with the IT service desk, and he is positioning the one-week timeline as the trigger for that pivot.
