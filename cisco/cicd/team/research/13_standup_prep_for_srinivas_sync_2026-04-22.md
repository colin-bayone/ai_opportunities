## Srikar Dashboard Walkthrough and Coaching

Srikar opened with a brief recap of where the NX-OS issue categorizer dashboard currently stood. He reported that the analysis had identified approximately 78 total categories, broken into 12 top-level parent categories and roughly 66 sub-level categories beneath them. The dashboard, which currently resides on his Cisco laptop and therefore could not be screen-shared on the BayOne WebEx room during the standup, includes a range of visualizations covering resolved versus unresolved threads, monthly trend comparisons over three-month, six-month, and nine-month windows, heat maps, and category-over-time views.

Colin then delivered coaching across several dimensions. On pacing and sequencing, he told Srikar: "Definitely go through the diagrams. Don't just stay with that first one, because the first one is by far the least rich of the diagrams." He followed with: "Flash all of them. You don't need to spend a ton of time explaining each and every graph. He's going to get it." And: "Call them out at a high level, what's on them, what they're representing." The intent is breadth of exposure rather than depth on any single chart, with Srinivas given enough to see the range of the analysis without the walkthrough bogging down.

On construction and provenance, Colin told Srikar to cover how the dashboard was built, not just what it shows. That means introducing the skill that produced the analysis, which is now in the CI/CD repository alongside the other skills, and showing the SQLite backend with its tables. Colin indicated he would also show the database on screen directly via the SQLite Viewer VS Code extension, because doing so would make the solution tangible to Srinivas and draw a direct line toward a production-ready, rerunnable system rather than a one-off language model read of the chat history.

Colin's most emphatic coaching concerned insight delivery. His words: "You have to talk about the insights Srikar. So you have to talk about, you know, what we're gaining out of it, not just here's the last three months of data and no context beyond that. You have to explain them. You've been looking at the data for three days. So I'm expecting you to have that clarity with him on this call." The coaching moves Srikar from chart-narrator to analyst. Srikar confirmed he would cover these points and take notes.

Colin also reviewed the specific document Srikar had shared on WebEx the night before. He called out the need to disambiguate labels, noting the distinction between an unresolved thread (no response at all) and an ambiguous thread (a response was received but it was not indicative of a concrete solution). He flagged a scaling issue on one of the stacked charts where the baseline was relative to the previous series rather than zero, which made the legend appear misaligned. He recommended either fixing the relative-to-zero scaling or reordering the series so the smallest appears at the bottom. He recommended removing charts numbered 13, 14, 15, and 17 because they were prototype artifacts or were not rendering correctly. He coached Srikar on which charts to emphasize in the walkthrough, specifically the categories-over-time chart, the what-fixes-repeat solution-pattern chart, the hierarchy tree that makes the 78-category structure self-evident, the calendar heat map that surfaces specific bad weeks such as the October incident where Xflow issues spiked, and the bar race that tells the evolution story in about fifteen seconds.

On the closing statistic, Colin drilled in that only 44 percent of NX-OS chat threads receive a response, and that this number must be named on the call. The implication is that the historical chat data is not as rich a training corpus as it might appear, that only a subset of categories will be suitable for automated triage based on historical reference, and that for the remainder the team will either need more sample data or will need Cisco to improve response behavior in the chat.

## Namita's Log Classification and Dependency Graph Update

Colin framed Namita's Srinivas update around two items: the log classification workstream including outreach to Mahaveer Jinka for ADS machine access, and the follow-up with Justin on anything newly emerged from the dependency graph and commit-traceability work.

Namita reported that she and the team met Justin the previous day. Justin showed them where to look, given a build, to identify which commits were part of that build. However, identifying which specific commit among those caused a problem is not yet a solved problem. That step requires a strategy the team still needs to develop, likely starting with manual inspection of the build and build logs.

Namita then walked through her current understanding of what Srinivas wants, which she planned to articulate on the call. The target is a fallback strategy that activates when a commit or PR causes a build failure. It involves watching the pipelines, detecting the failure, identifying the culprit PR and commit, identifying the dependency PRs that would need to be rolled back along with the culprit, defining the rollback strategy, and notifying users through a human-gated workflow. Namita also noted Srinivas's interest in three distinct graph types: a call graph answering which code depends on what, a knowledge graph capturing historical context, and a context graph capturing current state.

Colin validated the framing as consistent with what Srinivas described on Friday but redirected attention to execution. In his words, Srinivas already said what to do on Friday. What Srinivas will want on Wednesday is how the team will do it and what they will use to do it. Colin recommended leaning on any existing artifact from Justin on the dependency graph side, explicitly because Srinivas finds comfort in Justin's involvement, and extending or leveraging that work to cover the full PR-and-commit dependency graph. Namita noted that when she had asked Justin about a PR dependency graph the prior day, he indicated that nothing currently existed. Colin guided her to surface that fact carefully on the call, phrasing it as "per Justin, it doesn't seem like that work has been completed so far" rather than anything confrontational, so that the information lands with Srinivas without defensiveness from Justin's side.

Colin also walked through the knowledge graph terminology carefully, noting that both the Cisco side and the BayOne side have been using the term imprecisely. A knowledge graph in the proper AI definition requires stateful nodes, edges, meaningful connections, and semantic relationships, typically backed by libraries such as Neo4j or hierarchical navigable small world structures. What Justin showed Srikar and Namita, which showed file-to-file connections with no semantic meaning attached to each edge, is closer to a call graph or a concept map than a true knowledge graph. Colin coached Namita to surface this distinction on the call without making it feel like a correction of anyone, because a true knowledge graph carries substantial cost. Saurav reinforced the cost point, noting that knowledge graph construction scales exponentially with repository count because the indexing cost doubles with each significant expansion. The implicit message for Srinivas is that the team should agree on terminology before the team commits to an approach whose cost profile is very different depending on what was meant by the word knowledge graph.

Colin pasted a link into the team chat pointing to existing GitHub functionality that already produces PR and commit dependency information in a structured form. His coaching was for the team to investigate this before the call, to test it on a small repository rather than immediately on a large production codebase, and to surface the option on the call as something the team will investigate and leverage if suitable rather than as a dismissive dismissal of Justin's work.
