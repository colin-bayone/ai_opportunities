## The Leadership Ask

The most important content in this 1:1 is Colin's explicit ask of Namita to step into a leadership posture for Srikar. The ask is framed in three connected moves. First Colin separates Namita's situation from Srikar's situation, validating that Namita had a structural reason for limited progress this week while Srikar did not. Second Colin names the autonomy pattern as the underlying issue rather than work ethic. Third Colin asks Namita directly to take on the leadership posture, grounding the ask in the contrast between Namita's profile and Srikar's profile.

Colin opens this section of the conversation by drawing the distinction. As he puts it, "I think for you, there's some buffer here because I know that ambiguity would have been a little bit much. But for him, I need to get him on the right path. And that's probably where I need to ask you to help." He then makes the ask explicit. "And this is me telling you, you know, I want you to step up and be a leader for him. Because for him, you didn't have an IT incident this week. And he did struggle to make progress this week. So you had a reason, right? For him, he didn't have as much of a reason."

The framing does two things at once. It absolves Namita of the same accountability Colin is applying to Srikar, and it elevates Namita into a peer-leadership role on the team. The contrast is not subtle. Namita had a reason. Srikar did not. Colin then turns that contrast into the basis of the ask. As he puts it, "Now, you are the opposite, and that's why I want you to help him. And I want you to be kind of a leader for him, and keep him on that path."

Colin closes the ask with an affirmation of his original team-composition decisions. "He's more than capable, and I don't think this is going to be a long-term thing. I think it's just, I probably freaked him out today a little bit, I'm sure. But I got everyone on this team for a reason. And it wasn't by mistake. It wasn't because I had to. It wasn't because I didn't have options. I chose for a reason. And I stand by those choices."

This is a meaningful sentence. Colin is not opening the door to a personnel change. He is reaffirming that Srikar belongs on this team and that the issue at hand is a coachable execution pattern, not a fit problem. The leadership ask of Namita is positioned as the coaching mechanism, not as a precursor to a performance action.

## Srikar's Pattern as Colin Reads It

Colin's read on Srikar is precise. The issue is not effort and is not capability. The issue is autonomy under ambiguity. As Colin frames it, "It's not a work ethic problem at all. It is simply how he can be autonomous whenever there's an ambiguous path ahead. So, you know, his problem is by no means, you know, working hard. I know he works hard. I think his problem is that whenever he's not sure what to do, or he gets stuck, or he's unsure, he stalls."

The mechanism Colin describes is a stall pattern. When the path forward is clear, Srikar executes. When the path forward is unclear, Srikar pauses, explores in directions that do not produce forward motion, and waits for either clarification or a different prompt. The Codex anecdote in this 1:1 illustrates the pattern. Srikar reported to Colin that he had been "exploring Codex" rather than asking Codex a specific question, which Colin frames as the difference between research-as-motion and research-as-stall.

Colin's prescription for Srikar is small wins. As he puts it, "for him, I think he just needs to get some wins under his belt so he can have that confidence to push forward." The implication is that the autonomy gap is partly a confidence gap, and that the way to close it is sequenced delivery of completable units of work where Srikar can experience the loop of decide, execute, complete. The leadership ask of Namita is in service of this. Namita is not being asked to do Srikar's work. Namita is being asked to be the person Srikar can think out loud with when he stalls, so that the stall does not extend into a full week of no forward motion.

## The Three-Hour Coaching Session That Did Not Stick

Colin references a prior coaching attempt that did not produce the change he was hoping for. As he describes it, "even on Monday, it was either Friday or Monday, whichever one makes sense, it was like a three-hour meeting I had with him to explain. And then I came back the next day, and no progress there. And he was saying, oh, I was exploring Codex. And I was like, man, you can't explore Codex. Ask Codex a question."

The detail matters because it shows Colin has already invested significant individual coaching time, and the autonomy pattern persisted. Three hours of one-on-one explanation did not translate into autonomous next-day execution. This is the specific gap that the Namita leadership ask is meant to address. Colin is not adding another three-hour session. Colin is positioning a peer who can model the autonomy behavior in real time, in the day-to-day flow of the work, rather than in a separate coaching context.

Colin connects this to the broader engagement posture. As he puts it, "we're not in this academic study AI type of project, where the expectation is to come into this as an expert and to be an expert. And I know he can. I know that. I wouldn't have gotten him if I had any lack of confidence that he could push forward like that. But I think he does need someone to keep him accountable and to help him if he gets stuck, to kind of think around outside the box on what he should do."

The phrase "academic study AI type of project" is the implicit contrast. This engagement is execution work, not exploration work. The expectation is forward motion against ambiguous prompts, not exhaustive understanding before any action. Srikar's stall pattern reads as exploration-mode behavior in an execution-mode engagement. The leadership ask of Namita is the structural fix.

Colin is contrasting his own paste-and-go behavior, which he sees Namita as sharing, with Srikar's exploration-mode behavior. The leadership ask is grounded in this contrast.

The ask is also a recognition of where Namita is in the team. Namita has been the most consistent independent executor on the team since onboarding. ## Vaishali and Tanuja: Held Back Deliberately as Performative Theatrics

This is one of the most internally candid passages in the entire engagement chain. Colin describes a deliberate decision to hold Vaishali and Tanuja back from Cisco work that they could technically begin today, in order to perform a posture of deference toward Cisco IT after the credential incident, and in order to absorb that concession in a way that protects Namita's continued role on the project.

The plan as Colin describes it is structural. As he puts it, "The one thing that I'm going to put in place for both Visalia and Tunisian, and I know I owe them both back a lot of messages and meetings and things, is they're going to get their laptops by next Friday. At that point, only am I going to assign them work for Cisco. Until that point, they can be in observer mode. They can talk, they can listen, but I'm not going to allow them to do any further work until they have their Cisco laptop in hand."

Colin is explicit that this is a posture rather than a hard constraint. As he puts it, "what we have to do, I can only describe this theatrics a little bit. This will put on a performance. And say, we've learned so much from this, a wise and powerful Cisco IT. We've learned so much for this. And this has impacted us so much that what we are going to do is we are going to be so careful." The intent is to create a visible artifact of compliance for Cisco IT to see, so that the credential-incident narrative resolves as BayOne taking IT seriously and adjusting its posture accordingly.

Colin is also explicit that this is a self-imposed cost. As he puts it, "It's kind of performative in a way. Even though it's true, I'm not saying it's not true, but putting on a show and saying like, look, we could, you know, get by Shiley and Tymoujin on this today. They have WebEx accounts at this point with Cisco IDs. I'm going to specifically not do that to our own disadvantage just for performance, so that they can say that there was something that they wanted about this."

The strategic logic is in the next sentence. As Colin puts it, "that's my much better version of the other thing that they want to do about this, which would be to take you out of the project. So they never ask for that. I'm not going to offer that. But that's some theatrics that some managers do. I'm not like that." This is the load-bearing piece. The hold-back of Vaishali and Tanuja is the substitute concession. Cisco IT's instinct in incidents like this is often to demand removal of a contractor. Colin is preempting that by offering a different concession that costs BayOne some capacity but preserves the team composition.

Several things matter about this framing. The decision is deliberate and acknowledged as such. The decision costs BayOne and Cisco real capacity for one week, since both Vaishali and Tanuja are technically enabled today. The decision protects Namita's role specifically by shifting the visible incident-response toward access discipline rather than personnel removal. The decision is being made unilaterally by Colin, without offering Cisco IT the personnel-removal concession even as a pre-empt. As Colin puts it, "they never ask for that. I'm not going to offer that."

This is a clean example of the engagement-protection posture Colin holds at the BayOne side. The team Colin chose stays. The concession is structural. The posture toward IT is performative deference. None of this is visible in the client-facing material from the same day.
