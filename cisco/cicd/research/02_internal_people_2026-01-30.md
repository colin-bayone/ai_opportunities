The Set 01 analysis reasonably assumed this referred to a Cisco-internal engineer named Colin, distinct from Colin Moore at BayOne. **This transcript resolves the ambiguity in the opposite direction.** Colin Moore is clearly BayOne's technical lead on the engagement. ### 2. Colin Moore

**Role:** BayOne Director of AI. Technical lead for the Cisco CI/CD engagement. Will lead discovery, define the technical approach, and oversee delivery.

**What this source tells us:**

- **This transcript definitively establishes Colin Moore as a BayOne employee, not a Cisco engineer.** He discusses his travel plans to the Bay Area ("I will be in the area from starting on the 9th up until the 20th"), his hiring activities ("I already started the hiring process for people in general this week"), and his role leading discovery. There is no ambiguity.
- He has already spoken with Sarang (the current Cisco-side resource): "I did connect with Sarang today." His assessment is candid and blunt.
- He demonstrates deep technical fluency with the engagement scope. When Rahul asks if networking background is needed, Colin gives a nuanced answer: the CI/CD work is "mostly about their GitHub workflow, connecting with their Airflow instance" and is largely "nonspecific to their code base," with the exception of "the automated triage and resolution of errors."
- He defines the two specific skill sets needed: (1) "someone with real agentic AI experience in the CI/CD space, but not someone that's just doing prompt engineering, someone that's actually doing kind of a system-level automation" and (2) "someone with Airflow experience, because that'll be the single biggest thing to connect to, because that involves APIs, that involves Airflow itself."
- He offers to write the job descriptions himself: "I can write the job descriptions if that's helpful."
- He is confident about his ability to lead discovery solo: "Discovery is fairly easy if you know what you're looking for." And: "If it came down to the wire and I was the only person involved in discovery, that would be no issue."
- He provides a thoughtful analysis of the on-site role. He distinguishes between a pure developer (risks getting pigeonholed, as happened with Sarang) and an "interface layer" between the on-site team and India-based team. He recommends the latter: "if they're partially a dev, but more so like an interface layer, that is going to be probably the best bang for the buck."
- He flags the budget concern honestly: "$100K per quarter is okay if we have enough time. Otherwise it'd be low. If they want something like delivered in two, three quarters, I think that would be low." He suggests a ramp model: "start out with something like a hundred and then move it up as things get more involved."
- He notes from his rate questions document that this is his first time doing resource costing at BayOne: "I am not sure what we charge for/pay to offshore or onshore resources, since this is my first time doing this at BayOne."

**Signals about working style:**

- Technically hands-on and confident. He has already engaged with the Cisco resource, evaluated the technical landscape, and formed opinions about what the team needs -- all before being formally tasked.
- Blunt in internal assessments. His characterization of Sarang is unvarnished (see Sarang entry below). His assessment of the Cisco team's development workflow is equally direct: "they have a very, very, very just unhealthy way of working... these guys are pasting code to each other in a WebEx channel. They're not even using GitHub properly."
- Proactive. He had travel booked, hiring in motion, and had already spoken with Sarang before this call. He is not waiting for direction -- he is already executing.
- Strategic thinker. His analysis of on-site role definition (interface layer vs. pure dev) shows he is thinking about engagement success patterns, not just filling a headcount req.
- First engagement of this type at BayOne. He is new enough to BayOne that he does not know their rate cards or margin expectations. This is relevant context for understanding his position in the organization.

## Key Observations for Engagement Strategy

1. **Sarang's situation is a liability and a lesson.** His placement failed due to isolation (no communication from US team, no GitHub access, stuck in a junior role). The next placement must avoid these patterns. Colin's recommendation for an "interface layer" role rather than a pure developer role is a direct response to this failure.

2. **Srinivas is the wild card.** He controls the technical domain but operates with non-standard workflows. Whether BayOne can maintain its own quality standards while working within his organization will depend on engagement governance -- specifically, whether BayOne's resources take direction from BayOne or from Srinivas.

3. **The Gaurav precedent creates both risk and opportunity.** Cisco is cautious because of a prior failed placement. This means BayOne has less room for error, but it also means that competent delivery will stand out dramatically against a low baseline of expectations.

4. **Rahul and Colin Moore have complementary but distinct perspectives.** Rahul thinks in terms of commercial positioning, client confidence, and competitive dynamics. Colin thinks in terms of technical execution, team structure, and delivery risk. The engagement needs both, and this call shows them collaborating effectively.

5. **Physical presence is non-negotiable for both sides.** Rahul insists on it based on his knowledge of Cisco's culture. Colin's experience with Sarang's isolation confirms it. The on-site Bay Area resource is not optional -- it is a core part of the engagement model.

6. **Colin Moore is the engagement's single point of technical authority.** He is leading discovery, defining the team, writing job descriptions, and setting the technical direction. If he is also involved in "other areas, other activities" (as Rahul notes), his bandwidth becomes a risk factor, just as Colin/Sarang's bandwidth was flagged as a risk in Set 01 for the Cisco side.

7. **Budget math needs attention.** $100K/quarter must cover Colin Moore's time, an on-site Bay Area resource (expensive), and offshore team members. Colin flagged this may be low depending on timeline expectations. The supplementary rate questions document confirms Colin does not yet have BayOne's rate cards, making cost modeling an immediate dependency.
