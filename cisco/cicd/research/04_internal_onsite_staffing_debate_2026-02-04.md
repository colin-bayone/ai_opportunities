## Context

This is an internal strategy call between Zahra (BayOne business development) and Colin Moore (Director of AI), taking place on February 4, 2026 -- the same day Zahra has a meeting scheduled with Anand (Cisco-side decision-maker) at 11:00 AM. The call is a last-minute alignment session. Zahra is literally driving to the meeting while they speak; by the end of the call she asks Colin to type up notes she can use when she walks in.

The central question of this call is how many people BayOne should place on-site at Cisco in the Bay Area, what that costs, and whether the investment is justified. This question sits at the intersection of several prior discussions:

- **Document Set 01** (Zahra's January 16 email) established a staffing model of 1-1.5 FTE onshore initially, scaling to 4-5 offshore.
- **Document Set 02** (Rahul/Colin internal call, January 30) debated whether the on-site person should be a pure developer or an interface layer, identified the Sarang failure as a cautionary precedent, and established that on-site presence was non-negotiable given Cisco's culture of ignoring remote resources.
- **Amit Grover** (BayOne operations/delivery) has apparently proposed placing two people on-site. This is the specific proposal Zahra is interrogating on this call.

The budget context is critical: at the time of this call, the confirmed budget is $100K per quarter, with Zahra planning to ask Anand for $150-200K. Two Bay Area on-site resources would consume the vast majority of even the higher budget figure, leaving minimal headroom for the offshore team that would do the actual development work.

The transcript is speech-to-text and contains multiple rendering errors. ## 1. Zahra's Opening Question: Amit's Two On-Site Proposal

Zahra frames the on-site staffing question as the very first substantive item of the call. She has been given conflicting guidance from different BayOne stakeholders and wants Colin's unfiltered assessment before walking into the meeting with Anand.

### 1a. The Direct Question

Zahra asks plainly:

> "Did Amit propose having two on-site people? Are you in agreement with what Amit proposed in terms of the -- yeah, it's a very important question."

She immediately explains why she is coming to Colin rather than relying on Amit or Rahul:

> "Here's why I'm asking you this. I know Rahul's gonna tell you something. I know Amit's gonna say something. And I will go with whoever is giving -- I don't trust Amit and his judgment. I'm being very straightforward with you and I've said this to Rahul."

This is a notable declaration. Zahra is the person walking into the meeting. She has the relationship with the Cisco-side decision-maker. She explicitly states she does not trust Amit's judgment and is choosing Colin as her source of truth for how to position staffing.

### 1b. Zahra's Framing of the Stakes

Zahra distinguishes between Cisco's interest and BayOne's interest and positions herself as responsible for both:

> "Cisco's pain point is what? They want to save money, right? I am there to make sure that we make money."

She frames the two-on-site question specifically as a cost-versus-value problem:

> "The reason that that number is important -- because if Amit is saying two on-site because we'll make more money that way, that is not how I would... I want to make money. Don't get me wrong. I'm a sales person, right? But do you think, obviously, it's more cost than eating and having two people on site? Is that necessary?"

Zahra's concern is that Amit's proposal for two on-site resources is driven by a simplistic revenue calculation (more people = more billing) rather than a considered assessment of what the engagement actually requires. She wants to know whether two on-site resources serve a delivery purpose or are just an expensive way to inflate the deal size.

## 2. Colin's Core Argument Against Two On-Site

Colin responds with what he characterizes as "even probably more than what you wanted." His argument proceeds in several layers, moving from general skepticism about the onshore/offshore balance to a specific cost-based objection to two on-site resources.

### 2a. BayOne Is Guessing

Colin opens with a candid admission about the state of BayOne's engagement planning:

> "I can tell early on that we do not have much experience in this thing. I think we've done stuff for Cisco in the past, but even just talking to Amit, I get the same feeling. I think we're kind of flying by the seat of our pants a bit. And that's okay. I mean, we have to start somewhere."

He extends this to the onshore/offshore question specifically:

> "From the onshore versus offshore, I think we're guessing a lot. And I think we're trying to give people things in order to build them a confidence that we can't really deliver on."

This is a significant observation. Colin is saying that the two-on-site proposal is not based on an analysis of the work or the Cisco environment; it is a guess made to project confidence to the client, without a realistic plan to back it up.

### 2b. On-Site Presence Must Be Functional, Not Performative

Colin draws a line between on-site presence that serves a real interface function and on-site presence that is just "showing face":

> "Hiring people in person is good if there's an actual realistic experience to interface with the team. But if you just want someone to go there and show face, and it's not someone who's going to be, you know, day to day talking to people, you know, we're chewing up a substantial part of that, in my opinion, very limited $100K a quarter just to have people onshore."

The key phrase is "very limited $100K a quarter." Colin is anchoring the decision to the budget reality. Even if the engagement budget grows (Zahra is about to ask for $150-200K), the cost of two Bay Area resources is disproportionate to the total available spend. An on-site person who is there for optics rather than function is burning money that could fund additional offshore delivery capacity.

### 2c. The "Guarantee" Threshold for Two On-Site

Colin states his condition for accepting two on-site resources in absolute terms:

> "The only way it makes sense to me to have two people on shore is if there is a guarantee -- not just a 'we hope fingers crossed' -- but a guarantee that there will be additional business afterwards, or at least one other team at Cisco that we have certainty that we will get business from. That's the only way to justify that commitment."

This is a high bar. Colin is not saying "probable" or "likely" additional business. He is saying the only justification for the investment is certainty of expansion. Anything less, and the two-on-site model is an unacceptable risk.

### 2d. The Cascading Cost Impact

Later in the call, Colin returns to the structural problem with adjusting onshore/offshore ratios, this time addressing it as a complaint about how the decision was reached internally:

> "Whenever we talk about resources and you say you want to adjust this and have two in the US and two in India, I'm like, you understand what you're really getting at here. You're -- it's not just like you're getting different people in different locations. You're changing the number of the team, what their roles are, the communication strategy. You're changing so many things from a costing perspective and it doesn't really make sense. It just kind of feels like an idea someone had while they were getting a shower in the morning."

This is a direct critique of Amit's proposal methodology. Colin's point is that shifting the onshore/offshore ratio is not a simple swap of geography; it cascades into role definitions, communication architecture, team size, and cost structure. Making that change without a documented justification is not strategic -- it is whimsical.

He reinforces this:

> "I will challenge you. I want to see the justification. You say that we want two onshore and two offshore? Great. Show me what drove that decision for you. And it can't just be a win. It has to be more substantial."

## 3. Colin's View of One On-Site Person: The "Wash" Assessment

While Colin opposes two on-site resources, he acknowledges that one on-site person serves a purpose -- but characterizes the value in notably lukewarm terms.

### 3a. Must Be Senior

Colin states the minimum qualification for the on-site person:

> "If you have one person and Srinivas wants someone to, you know, interface with directly day to day -- I think it's worth understanding that person has to be fairly senior in order to keep up with him."

This is consistent with Document Set 02, where Colin argued the on-site role should be an interface layer staffed by a senior person, not a junior developer. The reasoning here is the same: Srinivas (the Cisco-side technical lead) is demanding and non-standard in his workflows. A junior person would not be able to navigate his expectations.

### 3b. The "Wash" Dynamic

Colin then delivers his most revealing assessment of what the on-site person will actually experience:

> "That person... is probably going to be almost like a wash because I know the type with Srinivas. He's going to be pinging the guy all the time, getting distracted. He'll be a high-paid person that's keeping the client happy but not really achieving much because he's always got, you know, three messages to answer from Srinivas, which can be okay."

Colin is describing a specific scenario: the on-site person's day will be consumed by constant interruptions from Srinivas. They will be answering pings, responding to ad-hoc requests, and managing the client relationship rather than doing substantive technical work. The result is a "wash" -- a senior, expensive Bay Area resource whose entire output is client relationship management, not delivery.

Colin acknowledges this can be acceptable ("which can be okay") but frames it as a relationship cost, not a delivery investment. The on-site person keeps Srinivas satisfied but does not meaningfully advance the actual project work. That work will be done by the offshore team.

### 3c. One Person Is Relationship Management; Two Is Investment

Colin draws a clear distinction between the rationale for one versus two on-site resources:

> "If there's two, if it can be justified with additional work, fine. Because that would be, to me, a practical investment. If you have one person, that's not an investment. Two people isn't a good investment because you actually have a, yeah, you know, some rock to stand on."

The logic is as follows:

| On-Site Count | Colin's Characterization | Justification Needed |
|--------------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| **One** | Relationship management cost, not an investment. A senior person keeping Srinivas happy while being constantly distracted by his pings. Acceptable but produces no delivery output. | Srinivas wants access to someone. This is the price of that access. |
| **Two** | Qualifies as a "practical investment" but only if justified by additional work (i.e., a second team, a second engagement, additional scope). | Guaranteed additional business. Not hope -- certainty. |

## 4. The Direction-Taking Problem Revisited

Colin's "wash" assessment of the one on-site person connects directly to a concern he first raised in Document Set 02: who the on-site person takes direction from.

In Set 02, Colin identified the critical dependency:

- If the on-site person takes direction from Srinivas (Cisco), they risk being pigeonholed into junior work and failing the way Sarang did.
- If the on-site person takes direction from BayOne (Colin), they can operate strategically even while on-site.

In this call, Colin effectively updates that analysis. Even under the better scenario (direction from BayOne), the on-site person will be pulled toward Srinivas constantly because Srinivas will ping them directly. The physical proximity that makes on-site presence valuable also creates a gravitational pull: Srinivas has someone physically available, and he will use that access aggressively.

The implication is that the on-site person's day will be divided between:

1. **Actual BayOne-directed work** (coordinating with the offshore team, gathering requirements, facilitating access).
2. **Srinivas-directed interruptions** (responding to pings, answering questions, managing his ad-hoc requests).

Colin's "wash" assessment suggests category 2 will dominate, leaving the on-site person as an expensive client-satisfaction mechanism rather than a contributor to delivery velocity.

## 6. The Bay Area Cost Problem

Colin acknowledges the value of on-site presence but questions whether that presence needs to be in the Bay Area specifically.

### 6a. Colin's Blunt Assessment

> "I do, though, question the importance of onshore specifically in the Bay Area. Because if it was me and I was Rahul, I would say you guys are too damn expensive."

This is Colin stepping outside the immediate engagement and looking at it from BayOne leadership's perspective. Bay Area talent rates are among the highest in the country. An on-site senior developer in the Bay Area commands rates that consume a disproportionate share of a $100-200K quarterly budget.

### 6b. The Dallas Comparison

Colin makes the comparison explicit:

> "I don't think we're going to find people at the price point that Rahul wants that are going to be -- someone that's going to -- put it this way: if you were to do that sourcing in Dallas, you're going to get half the price for, you know, more than the person."

"More than the person" -- Colin is saying that not only would Dallas be cheaper, but the talent quality at the same price point would be higher. Bay Area premiums buy geography, not capability. The same money in a lower-cost market buys a better resource.

### 6c. The Rate-Quality Mismatch

Colin extends this into a broader staffing concern:

> "I'm never opposed to US people. I just question the need for two people, Bay Area, that are getting paid a lot of money."

The problem is not US-based resources in general. The problem is Bay Area-based resources specifically, at Bay Area rates, for a budget that cannot absorb them without gutting the offshore team.

## 8. The Working Hours and Physical Presence Advantage

Colin identifies two structural advantages of on-site presence that he acknowledges as genuine, independent of the cost question.

### 8a. Shared Working Hours

> "There's the advantage of them being sharing working hours with us. That's one limitation right now with India is that the actual crossover time that's wanted and the perception of that is limited."

The India-based offshore team works on Indian Standard Time, which creates a narrow window of crossover with US Pacific Time (roughly 6:00-10:00 AM Pacific, when it is 6:30 PM-10:30 PM IST). This limits real-time collaboration. On-site US resources eliminate this constraint for any work that requires same-timezone interaction with Cisco engineers.

### 8b. The Sarang Evidence: Physical Presence Cannot Be Ignored

Colin directly connects the working hours advantage to the Sarang failure:

> "For instance, poor Sarang, they won't even answer the guy back. But if someone's physically there, you don't really have that option."

This is a restatement of the Document Set 02 finding that Cisco's team ignores remote resources. Colin is acknowledging that physical presence solves a real problem -- it is not just optics. You can ignore a WebEx message from an offshore consultant. You cannot ignore someone standing at your desk.

### 8c. Colin's Concession

Colin summarizes his position as conditionally supportive:

> "So if there's an opportunity to have it and we're willing to make that investment, it's good."

He acknowledges on-site presence is good. His objection is not to the concept but to the cost, the number (two versus one), and the geography (Bay Area specifically versus a lower-cost US location).

## 9. The Compromise: "Ideally Two, Absolutely One"

Zahra and Colin converge on a negotiating position for the meeting with Anand.

### 9a. Zahra's Proposed Language

Zahra proposes keeping the commitment intentionally vague:

> "Do you think it's safe for me to say -- I'll leave it very vague, right? I'll say the plan right now is ideally we want to get two people for you, but we absolutely will get you one person onshore. So with one person onshore and the rest offshore, you're saying that we will be OK. But from a long-term perspective, having two people on site would be a value. Correct?"

Colin confirms:

> "That's right. That's right."

This formulation achieves several things simultaneously:

1. **Sets expectations for one on-site resource as the baseline.** Cisco hears "absolutely one" and builds their planning around that.
2. **Preserves optionality for two.** If the budget increases or expansion materializes, BayOne can add a second on-site person without it being a new request.
3. **Frames two as aspirational.** "Ideally" positions the second on-site person as something BayOne wants to provide (not something Cisco has to ask for), which creates a collaborative dynamic.
4. **Avoids committing to what they cannot justify.** The vague language means Zahra does not have to explain the staffing model in detail or defend the two-person cost.

## 10. Colin's Two-Option Pricing Tactic

Colin immediately follows the "ideally two, absolutely one" compromise with a specific pricing tactic. This connects the on-site staffing decision to the broader pricing strategy discussion.

### 10a. The Car Dealership Analogy

Colin describes a technique that was used on him successfully in the past:

> "My old trick that worked on me -- and like, I'm a hard guy to fuzz a little bit -- but what at least someone had done to me in the past that worked, because I bought into it, is you ask for more money and you propose it in a way that you can justify your own investment by kind of making the numbers similar enough that it doesn't seem like it's -- I think that's what Amit was doing, incorrect."

Colin is describing the two-option pricing strategy: present two packages with prices close enough together that the higher-priced option looks like obvious additional value rather than a significant cost increase.

### 10b. Application to the Staffing Question

Colin proposes presenting Anand with two options:

> "For instance, like if we were to say, here's the price for two onshore resources plus two offshore resources, which is the current path. Here's the price. But here's the price for one onshore resource and four offshore resources."

| Option | On-Site | Offshore | Positioning |
|--------|---------|----------|-------------|
| **A** | 2 | 2 | Current path (Amit's proposal). Higher total cost, smaller offshore team. |
| **B** | 1 | 4 | Alternative. Lower total cost, larger offshore team, more delivery capacity. |

Zahra responds enthusiastically:

> "Yeah, I was thinking that -- you always have like two approaches and put them into, like, just have the numbers up there so they see what that is."

### 10c. The Price Proximity Principle

Colin emphasizes that the two options should not have dramatically different prices:

> "Don't directly make one way cheaper than the other. Because if you -- it's almost like if you were to buy a car -- if you knew the, like, one upgraded model version with all the nice heated steering wheel features is only an extra $1,000 -- yes, you're gonna buy that, because you're gonna go, 'while I'm here, why not?'"

The tactic works because:

1. If the prices are far apart, the client will always choose the cheaper option.
2. If the prices are close together, the client will rationalize the slightly higher price for the perceived additional value.
3. The "additional value" in this case is the second on-site person -- which BayOne has already determined may not add delivery value but adds confidence value for Srinivas.

### 10d. Stay Away from Pros and Cons

Colin and Zahra align on what NOT to present to Anand:

> **Zahra:** "Do I even need to go into the pros and cons of that, or do I just say here's the price difference -- at 160, you will get this, and at 200, you'll get this?"
>
> **Colin:** "I would actually stay away from the pros and cons. For the reason that if you tell him that, that's gonna leave him with, you know, like, actual things to think about. He's more likely to make the off-the-cuff decision if you give him less information."

This is a deliberate choice to minimize the client's decision-making complexity. Presenting pros and cons gives Anand reasons to deliberate and potentially negotiate. Presenting only prices and deliverables invites a faster, more intuitive decision.

## 12. Colin's Broader Objection: Projects at a Loss

The on-site staffing debate triggers a broader philosophical exchange about BayOne's margin discipline.

### 12a. Colin's Position on Loss-Leaders

> "I cannot think through in my mind doing projects at a loss. I mean, that's like giving away free candy on the side of the road."

Colin frames this as a fundamental constraint. He acknowledges he is "not a sales guy" and is "used to a different size and type of organization," but maintains that there must be a floor below which concessions are irrational.

### 12b. Zahra's Context: Venkat's Warning

Zahra provides Venkat's explanation of the competitive reality:

> "Venkat told me from the beginning, he said, 'you are not a projects company, you are not a solutions company, so you're gonna have to work triple the hard to even get those opportunities.' Number two, 'you are gonna have to make -- in some, he's like, me, I'm different, right? I've got a different relationship with you because I have full faith in you, but there are gonna be favors because that's what Zorian and all these guys have had to do. They don't make money in the beginning.'"

This frames BayOne's competitive position: established partners like Zorian and Persistent have the luxury of profitability. BayOne, as a newcomer to Cisco's vendor ecosystem, may need to invest upfront to establish credibility.

### 12c. Colin's Counter: Practical Restraint

Colin does not reject the loss-leader concept entirely. He reframes it as a question of discipline:

> "To be fair, I mean, I totally agree with you. And that's what I was kind of looking for, at least that -- let me call it like this -- like practical restraint. Because yes, you'll have to make concessions, but do them intelligently. Don't just roll over and say, yeah, we're going to do this at, you know, a loss of $50K just to get our foot in the door, whenever we could have, you know, squeezed a little bit harder on the lemon and gotten some more juice out -- it's still gotten the work."

The principle is: concessions are acceptable if they are the minimum required to win the business. Concessions that go beyond what is necessary are negligent, not generous.

## 13. Synthesized Staffing Decision Framework

Based on the full on-site staffing debate in this call, the following decision framework emerges:

### The One On-Site Model (Baseline)

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **Cost** | Significant but manageable within a $150-200K quarterly budget. Leaves meaningful headroom for 3-4 offshore resources. |
| **Client value** | Gives Srinivas the access he wants. Extends the confidence built by Colin. |
| **Delivery value** | Minimal. The on-site person will be a "wash" -- consumed by client relationship management, not contributing to delivery velocity. |
| **Risk** | The on-site person will be pulled by Srinivas constantly and must be senior enough to navigate that without losing effectiveness. |
| **Direction** | Must take direction from BayOne (Colin), not from Srinivas, to avoid the Sarang failure pattern. |

### The Two On-Site Model (Aspirational)

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **Cost** | ~$100K of the quarterly budget for on-site alone. At $180K total, leaves only ~$80K for offshore. |
| **Client value** | Higher confidence signal. Demonstrates commitment. |
| **Delivery value** | Only justified if additional work materializes (second Cisco team, expanded scope, Guhan engagement). |
| **Risk** | Chews up most of the budget. If expansion does not materialize, BayOne has two expensive people in the Bay Area and an underfunded offshore team. |
| **Justification threshold** | Guarantee -- not hope -- of additional business. (Colin's standard.) Venkat/Guhan pipeline makes this plausible but not certain. (Zahra's assessment.) |

### The Negotiating Position (What Zahra Will Say)

"Ideally two, absolutely one" -- vague, preserves optionality, commits to the minimum while signaling willingness to invest more.

### The Pricing Presentation (How It Will Be Shown)

Two options with close price points. No pros and cons -- just prices and deliverables. Designed to nudge Anand toward the higher option by making the incremental cost seem trivial relative to the additional value.
