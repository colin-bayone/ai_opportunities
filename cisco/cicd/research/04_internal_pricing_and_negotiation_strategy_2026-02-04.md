## 3. The Actual Target: $150-200K with a Range Tactic

### What Zahra Says

> "I am going to go and ask for 150 to 200. I always give a range, and I see where they land. It's a tactic, and I never give one number."

### Dissecting the Tactic

This is the clearest statement of Zahra's pricing strategy in any document set:

1. **She will present $150K-$200K as the range.** Not $100K, not $150K, not $200K alone -- a range.
2. **"I always give a range."** This is her standard operating procedure across engagements, not something specific to this deal.
3. **"I never give one number."** This is a deliberate negotiation principle. By presenting a range, Zahra:
   - **Anchors the conversation above $100K.** The lowest figure Anand hears is $150K, which is 50% above the communicated budget.
   - **Gives Anand the perception of choice.** Anand can "choose" where to land within the range, which psychologically converts the interaction from "BayOne is asking for more" to "Anand is deciding how much he wants."
   - **Creates room to negotiate down.** If Anand pushes back, Zahra can move toward the $150K floor of the range without appearing to make a major concession -- she is landing where she always expected to.
   - **Signals flexibility.** Presenting a range communicates that BayOne is not rigid, which is important for a first engagement.
4. **"I see where they land."** Zahra's approach is diagnostic. The range is a probe: Anand's response tells her where his true budget ceiling is, what his comfort zone is, and how much internal justification he would need.

### Reconciling the Targets

Zahra states two slightly different targets in this call:

- **$50-60K above $100K** = $150-160K (stated when describing her pitch to Anand)
- **$150-200K range** (stated when describing her pricing tactic)

These are not contradictory. The $50-60K increment is how she will frame the ask conversationally ("just an extra $50-60K"). The $150-200K range is what she will present formally. The conversational framing makes $150K sound modest (just $50K more than the baseline); the formal range presentation anchors the high end at $200K.

## 4. The Two-Option Presentation: Tactical Price Framing

### What Zahra and Colin Develop Together

The core tactic is presenting Anand with two options that differ in team composition and cost, structured so the more expensive option appears to be the better value:

> **Zahra:** "You always have like two approaches and put them into like just have the numbers up there so they see what that is."
>
> **Colin:** "For instance, like if we were to say, here's the price for two onshore resources plus two offshore resources, which is the current path. Here's the price. But here's the price for one onshore resource and four offshore resources."

### The Two Options

| Option | Team Composition | Price Point | Zahra's Framing |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Option A (premium) | 2 onshore + 2 offshore | ~$180K | "Made to feel like a discount" -- Zahra wants this to sound like a deal |
| Option B (economy) | 1 onshore + more offshore | Lower (unspecified) | The baseline alternative |

### Why $180K for Option A

Zahra selects $180K deliberately:

> "I want to make them sound like they're getting a little bit of a discount. So I want to see maybe like 180. What do you think about that number?"

The logic: if Zahra's stated range is $150-200K, presenting Option A at $180K positions it in the upper-middle of the range but below the ceiling. At $180K (rather than $200K), it feels like BayOne is already discounting. Anand gets the perception of a deal without Zahra actually reducing her target.

Colin validates the number:

> "Yeah, I think that's much much much more palatable."

### The "Heated Steering Wheel" Analogy

Colin provides the conceptual framework for why the two-option approach works:

> "If you knew the like one upgraded model version with all the nice heated steering wheel features is only an extra $1,000. You're gonna buy that because you're gonna go, while I'm here, why not?"

The principle: make the price gap between the two options small enough that the premium option feels like an impulse upgrade rather than a major financial decision. If Option B is, say, $140K and Option A is $180K, the $40K gap on top of an already-large commitment feels incremental. The buyer's psychology shifts from "can I afford this?" to "for just $40K more, I get twice the onshore presence."

This is a classic anchoring and framing tactic. Option B is not the real offer -- it exists to make Option A look attractive by comparison.

## 5. Colin's Tactical Advice: No Pros and Cons

### What Was Discussed

Zahra initially asks Colin to type up both the costs and the "pros and cons" of each option since she is driving and will walk straight into the meeting.

> **Zahra:** "Can you type up... for on-site and like if we do two on-site versus one on-site, the I guess the pros and cons?"

Colin immediately redirects:

> **Colin:** "I would actually stay away from the pros and cons. For the reason that if you tell him that, that's gonna leave him with, you know, like actual things to think about. He's more likely to make the off-the-cuff decision if you give him less information."

### The Rationale

Colin's advice is built on a specific theory of how Anand will make this decision:

1. **Pros and cons create deliberation.** If Anand has a structured comparison document, he will study it, consult colleagues, and possibly bring it to procurement. The decision slows down and moves out of Zahra's control.
2. **Costs alone invite instinct.** If Anand sees only two numbers -- $X for this team, $Y for that team -- the decision becomes simpler. He either has the budget or he does not. He either wants more onshore presence or he does not. No analytical framework is introduced that requires weighing tradeoffs.
3. **Less information = more likely off-the-cuff decision.** Colin states this as a direct causal chain. The goal is to keep the decision in the meeting, in the moment, with Zahra present to steer the conversation. A deliberated decision is one BayOne does not control.

### Zahra's Agreement

> "Yes, agreed. Yeah, okay. Okay. You're thinking like me too."

Zahra validates that this aligns with her own instinct. The final approach agreed upon for the meeting: **costs only, no feature comparison, no pros/cons analysis.** Two numbers. Two team compositions. The question to Anand is simply: "Which do you prefer?"

## 7. The Onshore Staffing Question: Cost vs. Value

### Zahra's Opening Question

Zahra's first substantive question to Colin is about the team structure, specifically whether two on-site people are necessary:

> "Did Amit propose having two on-site people? Are you in agreement with what Amit proposed in terms of the... I know Rahul's gonna tell you something, I know Amit's gonna say something, and I will go with whoever is giving -- I don't trust Amit and his judgment."

This is revealing on multiple levels:

1. **Zahra positions Colin as the authority over Amit on team structure.** She explicitly says she does not trust Amit's judgment and will defer to Colin.
2. **She knows Rahul and Amit may give different answers.** This suggests internal misalignment on the staffing plan.
3. **Her concern is commercial, not technical:** "Is that necessary?" followed by "it's more cost than eating [the margin] and having two people on site."

### Colin's Position on Onshore Staffing

Colin provides a detailed, nuanced assessment that differs from what appears to have been Amit's recommendation:

**On having two onshore:**

> "The only way it makes sense to me to have two people onshore is if there is a guarantee, not just a 'we hope fingers crossed,' but a guarantee that there will be additional business afterwards or at least one other team at Cisco that we have certainty that we will get business from."

**On the cost reality:**

> "Two on-site resources already, if we have them, it's gonna be about a hundred K just having two people on site, right?"

Zahra confirms: "Right, right."

This is a critical number. Two onshore Bay Area resources would consume approximately $100K of budget by themselves -- which is the entire Cisco-communicated budget. Everything offshore would then need to come out of the incremental money Zahra negotiates above $100K, or BayOne absorbs it as a loss.

**On what one onshore person provides:**

> "If you have one person and [Srinivas] wants someone to interface with directly day to day, I think it's worth understanding that person has to be fairly senior in order to keep up with him. And [that person] is probably going to be almost like a wash because I know the type with Srinivas. He's going to be pinging the guy all the time, getting distracted. He'll be a high-paid person that's keeping the client happy but not really achieving much because he's always got, you know, three messages to answer from Srinivas, which can be okay."

Colin is describing a client management cost, not a technical delivery asset. A single onshore person exists to:
- Keep Srinivas happy by being physically available
- Act as a communication interface
- Absorb the constant interruptions so the offshore team can work uninterrupted

This aligns with the "interface layer" role design from Set 02, but Colin is now explicitly pricing the cost of that role: it is expensive (senior Bay Area rate) and produces limited direct delivery output because the person will spend most of their time managing the client relationship.

**On two onshore people:**

> "Two people isn't a good investment... For the work itself, more often than not, people want to go offshore. And this is a good project for offshore provided that, you know, we have the budget for it."

Colin concludes that two onshore people cannot be justified on delivery merit alone. The only justification is strategic -- if having a second onshore person creates a pathway to more business that offsets the cost. Without that certainty, it is a "bad investment."

### Zahra's Synthesis for the Meeting

Based on Colin's input, Zahra formulates her presentation approach:

> "Do you think it's safe for me to say -- I'll leave it very vague, right? I'll say the plan right now is ideally we want to get two people for you, but we absolutely will get you one person onshore. So with one person [onshore] and the rest offshore, you're saying that we will be OK. But from a long-term perspective, having two people on site would be [a value add]. Correct?"

Colin confirms: "That's right. That's right."

This framing is commercially astute:
- **"Ideally we want to get two people for you"** -- positions two onshore as the premium, aspirational option
- **"We absolutely will get you one person onshore"** -- guarantees the minimum with certainty
- **Leaves the second onshore person as an investment decision for Anand** -- if he wants it, he pays for it; if not, BayOne still delivers

### Bay Area Rates: Colin's Cost Challenge

Colin raises a specific concern about the Bay Area labor market:

Colin is flagging that Bay Area rates eat disproportionately into the budget. An onshore resource in the Bay Area might cost double what the same role would cost in a lower cost-of-living market like Dallas, with potentially lower capability. But Cisco's culture (Set 02 and Set 03: physical presence is non-negotiable, remote people get ignored) makes Bay Area placement a requirement, not a preference.

This is a structural cost disadvantage that BayOne cannot negotiate away. It means the onshore component of any team will always consume a large share of the budget regardless of the total dollar amount.

## 8. The Margin Discussion: Engineers vs. Sales

### Colin's Engineer Mindset

Colin articulates a position that he acknowledges is at odds with BayOne's commercial reality:

> "I was like, this isn't my thing. I'm not a sales guy. I'm used to a different size and type of organization. But I cannot think through in my mind doing projects at a loss. I mean, that's like giving away free candy on the side of the road."

And later:

> "There has to be a limit. Like, there has to be... we can't lose $500K on this deal."

The $500K reference is a direct callback to the prior Cisco project failure documented in Set 03, where BayOne lost approximately $500K on a project with undefined scope over six to eight months. Colin is using that historical loss as his anchor for what is unacceptable.

Colin's position is straightforward: margins matter, losses are unacceptable, and there must be a floor below which BayOne will not go even if it means losing the deal.

### Zahra's Response: Venkat's Advice

Zahra counters with a perspective she attributes to Venkat (the Cisco VP):

> "Colin, this is gonna be a challenge. And this is one thing I've learned. And it's taken me so time to finally understand this. Venkat told me from the beginning, he said, 'You are not a projects company, you are not a solutions company, so you're gonna have to work triple the heart to even get those opportunities, number one.'"

And:

> "'Number two, you are gonna have to make in some -- he's like, me, I'm different, right? I've got a different relationship with you because I have full faith in you -- but there are gonna be favors because that's what Zorian and all these guys have had to do. They don't make money in the beginning, which is unfortunate, but he's like, where I can help you, I will, but yes.'"

### Venkat's Framework (as Relayed by Zahra)

Venkat's advice to Zahra establishes several principles:

1. **BayOne is not a projects or solutions company.** BayOne is primarily a staffing/services firm, which means it enters consulting-type engagements from a weaker position than pure consulting firms. It must work harder to prove it can deliver project-based work.
2. **Early engagements will require losses or "favors."** Competitors like Zorian and Persistent also took losses on their first Cisco engagements. Absorbing early costs is the price of entry into Cisco's vendor ecosystem.
3. **Venkat will help where he can.** Venkat frames himself as an ally who can steer opportunities BayOne's way, but he cannot eliminate the economic reality of breaking into a new vendor relationship.
4. **The payoff is long-term, not per-engagement.** The logic is that losses on early engagements are recovered through the subsequent relationship -- larger budgets, more teams, less competition once established.

### Zahra's Synthesis

Zahra positions herself as the mediator between Colin's cost discipline and Venkat's commercial reality:

> "That's why I'm like, Anand, they have somewhat control. Let me see if I can pull in more to compensate on those pockets where we might have to give something up for free."

Her strategy: negotiate for more money from Anand specifically so that any concessions BayOne makes (free work, discounted rates, absorbing onshore costs) are offset by a larger topline. Rather than just accepting the loss, she is trying to create the financial room to absorb it.

### Colin's Practical Restraint

Colin reframes Zahra's point into what he considers an actionable standard:

> "To be fair, I mean, I totally agree with you. And that's what I was kind of looking for -- at least that, let me call it like this, like practical restraint. Because yes, you'll have to make concessions, but do them intelligently. Don't just roll over and say, yeah, we're going to do this at, you know, a loss of 50K just to get our foot in the door. Whenever we could have, you know, squeezed a little bit harder [on] the lemon and gotten some more juice out [and] still gotten the work."

The alignment point between Colin and Zahra:

- **Both agree losses may be necessary.** Colin is not saying zero loss is required.
- **Both agree losses must be intentional, not accidental.** The $500K prior loss was accidental -- scope was undefined, nobody was tracking costs, and the project ran for six to eight months before anyone pulled the plug.
- **Both agree Zahra should negotiate hard.** Whatever the ultimate margin, BayOne should extract as much as possible from the initial negotiation so that the gap between cost and revenue is as small as it can be.
- **The $500K is the outer bound.** Colin's explicit hard limit: "We can't lose $500K on this deal." The historical precedent is the line that must not be crossed.

### Colin's Limit: What It Means in Practice

Colin's $500K limit is a reference to the prior Cisco failure, but in the context of a $100-200K/quarter engagement, the practical risk is much smaller. Even if BayOne lost money every quarter for four quarters, the total exposure at $100K-$200K/quarter would be far less than $500K. Colin's point is not that this specific deal could reach $500K in losses -- it is that BayOne has a pattern of not tracking losses until they are catastrophic, and someone needs to set a floor.
