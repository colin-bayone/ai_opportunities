## 1. Colin's Assessment of Sarang After Direct Contact

Colin spoke with Sarang on or around the day of this call. His assessment is unambiguous and delivered in plain terms across several observations:

### 1a. First Impression: Something Is Wrong

> "I did connect with [Sarang] today. The current person. And I can tell you already they have a very very very just unhealthy way of working."

Colin's first instinct after speaking with Sarang is to describe the entire working environment around him as "unhealthy." He initially frames this not as a personality critique ("not in the sense of like anything dealing with personalities") but as a systemic indictment of development workflow. However, he quickly transitions into observations that are specifically about Sarang's state.

### 1b. Does Not Present as a 10-Year Professional

> "He was strange. I will say, I think like he did not strike me as someone who -- like he said he had 10 years of experience. He did not strike me that way."

Colin's word choice is notable. "Strange" is a subjective first-impression assessment. The disconnect between Sarang's claimed experience (10 years) and his presentation is significant enough for Colin to call it out explicitly. This is not a vague doubt -- it is a direct statement that Sarang's demeanor, knowledge, and bearing did not match the seniority level he claims.

### 1c. Almost Zero Actual Familiarity with the Project

> "He did not have literally almost any actual knowledge. He didn't even know what product line he was working on."

This is the most damning specific observation. Sarang was embedded at Cisco -- nominally working on a project -- and yet could not identify the product line he was assigned to. "Almost zero actual familiarity" is not a gap in deep technical knowledge; it is a gap in basic orientation. A consultant who cannot name the product they are working on has either been completely excluded from meaningful work, has disengaged, or was never properly onboarded. Colin's phrasing ("literally almost any actual knowledge") uses "literally" for emphasis, suggesting this is not exaggeration.

### 1d. Appeared to Be Out of the Game

> "It's kind of like feels like to me he was like out of the game for almost a month from the way he was talking."

Colin assesses that Sarang has been effectively idle or disengaged for approximately a month. "Out of the game" implies not just lack of recent work product, but a drift in engagement -- someone who has lost the thread of what is happening around them. This aligns with the access and communication failures described below: if you have no GitHub access and no one responds to your messages, there is nothing to do, and disengagement is the natural result.

### 1e. Showed Code but It Did Not Change Colin's Assessment

> "I did see his code. He was showing me some things on the screen."

Sarang proactively showed Colin code during their screen share. Colin's response is notable for what it does not say: there is no positive assessment of the code quality, no acknowledgment of progress, no "but at least he's built something." Colin immediately pivots from mentioning the code to reiterating his negative assessment ("I don't know how much utility he would be for this reason that he did not have literally almost any actual knowledge"). Whatever Sarang showed on screen did not alter the overall picture.

### 1f. Eager but Disconnected

> "He seemed very eager to help for sure, but he just seemed very distant to the project."

Colin grants Sarang one positive trait: eagerness. But he frames it as insufficient. Eagerness without connection to the actual project is just energy with no direction. "Very distant" as a descriptor carries weight -- it is not "slightly behind" or "catching up" but fundamentally disconnected from the work.

## 2. Sarang's Specific Failures and Blockers

Beyond subjective impressions, Colin identifies concrete, verifiable failures in Sarang's situation:

### 2a. No GitHub Repository Access

> "He doesn't even have their GitHub repository access."

This is a fundamental infrastructure failure. A developer or consultant assigned to work on a codebase who does not have repository access cannot read, review, modify, or contribute code. This is not a "nice to have" -- it is the absolute minimum prerequisite for any development role. Rahul's response ("Wow") confirms this is understood as shocking even within BayOne leadership.

The fact that Sarang still lacked GitHub access at the time of Colin's conversation with him -- presumably weeks or months into his assignment -- indicates either:

1. **No one at Cisco prioritized granting it.** Access provisioning is a straightforward administrative task. If it was never completed, Sarang was never treated as a real team member.
2. **Sarang could not navigate the process to get it himself.** At Cisco, getting access to internal tools often requires navigating ticketing systems, getting manager approvals, and following up persistently. If Sarang could not or did not do this, it reflects either lack of initiative or lack of a Cisco-side sponsor who cared enough to push it through.
3. **Both.** The most likely scenario: Cisco did not prioritize it, and Sarang lacked the on-the-ground presence or relationships to force the issue.

### 2b. Stuck in a Junior Dev Role Despite Experience

> "They stuck him in a junior dev role. I won't go into that part of it."

Colin references this almost in passing, declining to elaborate further ("I won't go into that part of it"), which suggests the details are either too frustrating to rehash or not relevant to the immediate strategic conversation. The core point: a consultant presented as having 10 years of experience was assigned work appropriate for a junior developer. This is a role mismatch that would demoralize anyone, and it suggests Cisco either:

1. Did not trust Sarang's capabilities and downleveled him.
2. Did not have a meaningful senior-level task defined and defaulted to junior work.
3. Did not differentiate between BayOne consultants and their own junior hires in terms of task assignment.

Colin connects this to a broader strategic lesson later in the call (see Section 5).

### 2c. Zero Responses from US Team on WebEx

> "The part of it that was a little bit jarring to me was the communication. He literally never got -- he showed me his WebEx. He never got one response from anyone in the US that he ever reached out to, not once."

This is the single most revealing data point in the entire call. Colin uses the word "jarring" -- not "disappointing" or "frustrating" but jarring, something that physically jolted him. Key details:

- **Sarang showed Colin his actual WebEx history.** This is not a vague complaint; Colin saw the evidence directly. Sarang proactively demonstrated the communication failure, which suggests it was the thing he most wanted someone at BayOne to understand.
- **"Not once."** This is an absolute. Not "rarely" or "sometimes" -- never. Every message Sarang sent to US-based Cisco team members went unanswered.
- **This was across all US contacts, not just one person.** The framing is "anyone in the US," suggesting Sarang attempted to reach multiple people and received zero responses from any of them.

The implication is severe: Sarang was, from a communication standpoint, completely invisible to the US-based Cisco team. He could not get answers to questions, could not get approvals, could not get feedback, and could not participate in any collaborative workflow that required input from US colleagues.

Despite his critical observations about Sarang's presentation and knowledge gaps, Colin is empathetic about the structural impossibility of Sarang's situation:

> "I don't think there was any chance for the poor guy to be successful."

This is a deliberate and considered judgment. Colin is not blaming Sarang for the failure; he is saying the failure was structurally predetermined. The word "poor guy" conveys genuine sympathy. This empathy matters strategically because it reframes the failure: it is not that BayOne placed a bad consultant; it is that the engagement model (remote, offshore, pure dev role, no on-site sponsor, no access provisioning) guaranteed failure regardless of who was placed. Any replacement operating under the same conditions would fail identically.

## 4. What This Reveals About Cisco's Internal Culture

The Sarang experience, combined with other observations Colin makes during the call, paints a detailed picture of how Cisco's internal culture operates:

### 4a. Physical Presence Is Non-Negotiable

Rahul states this directly:

> "Unless we are sitting on the heads, things may not move."

And more explicitly:

> "You have to be there in person with them. Just walk up to them and get your things done. So that's typically how it would be."

Colin agrees:

> "Yeah, I think there's definitely an advantage to being in person."

And Rahul adds the cultural framing:

> "I'm not surprised knowing these people how they work."

Rahul's phrasing ("knowing these people") indicates this is not news to him -- it is a known characteristic of this particular Cisco group. The norm at this Cisco division is that work gets done through physical proximity and hallway conversations, not through digital communication channels. A person who is not physically present does not exist in the workflow.

This is critical context for understanding why Sarang's WebEx messages went unanswered: it was not personal hostility toward Sarang. It is the default behavior toward anyone not physically in the office. Remote or offshore resources are structurally deprioritized, their messages treated as low-priority or not seen at all.

### 4b. Remote/Offshore People Get Ignored

The WebEx silence Sarang experienced is the proof point, but Colin and Rahul treat it as symptomatic of a broader pattern, not an isolated incident. The implication throughout the conversation is that this is how Cisco operates with all remote or offshore contributors, not something unique to Sarang.

Rahul specifically frames the on-site requirement as necessary "even for discovery" -- not just for implementation, but for the information-gathering phase where you need people to answer your questions. If Cisco engineers will not respond to WebEx messages during discovery, all discovery must happen in person.

### 4c. Access and Tooling Are Not Automatically Provisioned

Sarang's lack of GitHub access after an extended period on the engagement indicates that Cisco does not have (or does not follow) a standard onboarding process for external consultants that ensures tool access. Getting access requires persistent, in-person advocacy. Without an internal sponsor who cares enough to push the access request through, the request simply languishes.

### 4d. External Consultants Must Earn Their Position

Cisco's behavior toward Sarang (junior dev assignment, unanswered communications, no access provisioning) can be read as a passive vetting process: if you cannot navigate these obstacles, you are not someone we need to invest in. This is not explicit but is consistent with the "prove yourself" structure identified in Set 01 (Anand's ground rules, narrow entry point, "startup mindset" requirement).

## 6. Colin's Observations on Cisco's Development Practices

Separate from the Sarang assessment, Colin identifies systemic issues with how this Cisco group does development work:

### 6a. Pasting Code in WebEx Instead of Using GitHub

> "These guys are pasting code to each other in a WebEx channel. They're not even using GitHub properly."

This is a specific, concrete observation about workflow dysfunction. Code is being shared via chat copy-paste rather than through version control. This means:

1. **No code review process.** If code is being exchanged in WebEx, it is not going through pull requests.
2. **No version history.** Code pasted in chat is not tracked, diffed, or attributable.
3. **No CI/CD integration at the point of code sharing.** If code moves via chat before it enters a repository, then automated testing and deployment only apply after an ad-hoc manual step.
4. **This is what BayOne is being asked to fix.** The engagement is literally about improving their CI/CD pipeline, and the team does not use basic version control for day-to-day code sharing. The gap between current state and target state is enormous.

### 6b. Srinivas-Related Work Does Not Follow Standard Workflows

> "Any of the Srinivas-related work in general is not following any kind of a good or standard workflow."

Colin makes this assessment when discussing what skills are needed for the team. Srinivas appears to be a Cisco engineer or manager whose area of responsibility (which overlaps with the CI/CD engagement) operates outside normal development practices. This has two implications:

1. **Prior CI/CD experience may not transfer.** Colin explicitly warns that "most people that have CI/CD background would get there and would get lost almost immediately" because the workflows are non-standard. This means the engagement cannot hire standard CI/CD engineers and expect them to apply textbook knowledge.
2. **The CI/CD engagement is partly about imposing structure on Srinivas's domain.** If the work does not follow standard workflows, then part of what BayOne will be doing is not just building CI/CD pipelines but changing how this group operates. That is a change management challenge layered on top of a technical one.

### 6c. Big Learning Curve Regardless of Background

> "I think there's going to be a big curve no matter what."

Colin frames the learning curve as universal and unavoidable. Even someone with networking experience from a different company will find Cisco's specific environment unfamiliar. The stack is "unique to them" in the sense that while the technologies may be common (GitHub, Airflow, etc.), the way they are used and the workflows around them are idiosyncratic. This reinforces the importance of the discovery phase and the high-touch onboarding.

## 7. Strategic Implications for the New Engagement

The Sarang failure and Cisco cultural observations produce specific, actionable strategic imperatives:

### 7a. On-Site Presence Is Non-Negotiable

This is stated multiple times by both Rahul and Colin. Rahul frames it as an investment decision:

> "We would want to have at least one person here with them who works very closely."

And:

> "If we can get somebody onsite right now, that will be ideal."

Colin already has travel booked for the Bay Area from February 9-20, which gives a two-week window for in-person discovery. But beyond Colin's travel window, a permanent or semi-permanent on-site presence is needed.

### 7b. Pure Dev Role Is a Trap

Colin explicitly warns against placing someone in a pure development role based on Sarang's experience:

> "If they are partially a dev, but more so like an interface layer, that is going to be probably the best bang for the buck. If they're mostly a dev, I feel like they're going to get pigeonholed."

And:

> "That seems to be exactly what happened with Sarang. Basically, he kind of got this situation where he had to prove himself, and they stuck him on something that's not work that you give someone with that much experience."

The pattern is clear: Cisco assigns pure-dev BayOne consultants to low-level tasks, then ignores them when they struggle. The role must be structured so the BayOne on-site person has a legitimate reason to interact across teams and maintain visibility, not silently code in a corner.

### 7c. Interface Layer Role Is Safer

Colin proposes that the on-site person function as "an interface layer" -- a bridge between an India-based development team and the Cisco side:

> "If it's someone that's more of an interface layer that's just kind of being that bridge between an India-based team, that's not a bad thing. That could work."

This role design has multiple advantages:

1. **Prevents pigeonholing.** An interface/bridge role requires cross-team interaction by definition.
2. **Creates justified on-site presence.** The person has a reason to attend meetings, ask questions, and walk up to Cisco engineers -- they are coordinating for a broader team.
3. **Reduces single-point-of-failure risk.** If the on-site person gets stuck, the offshore team can continue working on tasks already defined, rather than everything stopping (as happened with Sarang individually).
4. **Matches Anand's "not staff aug" requirement.** An interface layer person is not a body filling a seat on a Cisco team; they are an active bridge managing a delivery model.

### 7d. Direction Must Come from BayOne, Not Cisco

Colin identifies a critical dependency in how the on-site person's role is structured:

> "It would just depend upon if he's taking directions from Srinivas or if he's still taking full direction from us. If they're still taking direction from us, developer would be okay."

If the on-site resource reports to and takes direction from Cisco, they risk the same fate as Sarang: being assigned inappropriate work with no BayOne-side leverage to correct it. If the on-site resource takes direction from BayOne (from Colin's engagement leadership), then even a developer role could work because the task assignment would be controlled by someone who understands the strategic positioning.

### 7e. Discovery Is Low-Risk and Colin Can Do It Alone If Necessary

Colin explicitly states:

> "If it came down to the wire and I was the only person involved in discovery, that would be no issue. Discovery is fairly easy if you know what you're looking for."

Rahul pushes back gently, wanting additional people involved during discovery both for capacity and for strategic optics ("get their confidence"). The agreed approach is to involve the team even during discovery, but with the understanding that discovery is not the bottleneck -- resource loading for implementation is.

## 8. The Sarang Question: What to Do with Him Going Forward

### 8a. The Problem

Sarang was present in the alignment meetings with Anand (per Zahra's email: "Colin and Sarang will be involved on the Cisco side"). Cisco knows who he is. If he simply disappears from the engagement, Cisco may ask questions.

Rahul raises this concern:

> "I don't know how they're going to take if Sarang is not going to be there at all because he was in all the meetings. So should we use him right now or how do we build?"

### 8b. The Decision

Colin and Rahul agree on a pragmatic approach: start without Sarang and only bring him in if Cisco specifically asks:

> **Rahul:** "Or we just go -- you go right now and see where it goes for the discovery, and they ask for him, then we will figure it out how to get him in the mix."
>
> **Colin:** "I would say so, Rahul. We can start off with that. And if they start getting more and asking for him, then we can see how we can pull him on a consulting side."

Colin's framing of "pull him on a consulting side" is notable -- if Sarang does come back, it would be in a consulting capacity (advice, context-sharing) rather than as an embedded resource. This is a downgrade from his original role.

### 8c. Risk Assessment

The risk of this approach is low. If Cisco does not ask about Sarang, the problem solves itself. If Cisco does ask, BayOne can frame it as a natural transition: Colin is leading discovery, and the team is being structured differently for the new phase of work. Sarang's absence from the new engagement is defensible because the engagement model is explicitly different from whatever Sarang was doing before.
