# Bad Slide Autopsy

Detailed analysis of 18 slides flagged for removal from the McGrath RFP response deck. Each entry documents what the slide shows, what's wrong with it, and the generalizable anti-pattern extracted.

Source material: Extracted PPTX content, screenshots, and Colin/Neha transcript notes from two calls.

---

## Group 1: "All Suck" (Slides 06-08)

Colin flagged these three consecutive slides as a block. They form a "corporate chest-thumping" section that reinforces BayOne's identity as a staffing company rather than a solutions vendor.

---

### Slide 06 -- Location Footprint (Global Map)

**What it shows:** World map with labeled BayOne locations across North America, LATAM, EMEAR, APJC, and India. Three info boxes describe regional capabilities (nearshoring models, BCP strategies, supported languages).

**What's wrong with it:**
- **Generic filler.** Every staffing company has a "look how global we are" map slide. This could be ripped from any IT services vendor's deck without changing a single word.
- **Zero McGrath relevance.** McGrath is a US-based equipment rental company. They don't need Arabic, Mandarin, or Korean language support. The EMEAR and APJC locations are irrelevant to this engagement.
- **Reinforces staffing perception.** The framing (nearshore models, BCP strategies, offshoring) positions BayOne as a body shop that ships labor overseas. This is the exact opposite of what Colin wants: a niche AI-enabled solutions vendor.
- **Visually mediocre.** Generic gray world map with purple callout boxes. Stock template quality.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't include "look how big we are" global presence slides in proposals. They reinforce the staffing company perception and add nothing specific to the client's needs.

---

### Slide 07 -- Awards & Recognition

**What it shows:** Six corporate logos (Cisco, eBay, Expedia, Macy's, Sony, Walmart) with supplier awards beneath each, plus Fast 100 (2018) and Inc. 5000 award logos at the bottom.

**What's wrong with it:**
- **Staffing awards, not solutions awards.** "Diversity Supplier Performance Award," "Top Supplier," "Valuable Supplier" -- these are staffing/procurement awards. Not a single one is for technical delivery, innovation, or managed services excellence.
- **Stale.** The Fast 100 and Inc. 5000 awards are from 2018 -- eight years old. Including dated awards looks worse than including none. It suggests BayOne hasn't achieved anything notable recently.
- **Reinforces the wrong positioning.** Every award on this slide says "we're good at placing people." The McGrath RFP needs to say "we're good at running complex managed services operations."
- **No context or relevance.** What did BayOne actually do for these companies? The slide doesn't say. Just logos and award names.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't include awards that reinforce a positioning you're trying to move away from. Awards older than 2-3 years should be dropped. If you must include recognition, make it relevant to the capabilities the client is evaluating.

---

### Slide 08 -- BayOne Diversity (46% Women, 11% POC, 2% Veterans)

**What it shows:** Three diversity statistics displayed as large purple numbers, with an abstract illustration of diverse figures climbing an upward arrow.

**What's wrong with it:**
- **Razor-thin content.** Three numbers and a stock graphic. This is a slide that exists solely to fill space.
- **Numbers aren't impressive.** 2% veterans is unremarkable. 11% POC in a tech staffing company is not a standout. These numbers invite unfavorable comparisons rather than inspiring confidence.
- **Zero relevance to the RFP.** McGrath isn't evaluating BayOne's workforce demographics. This slide doesn't answer any question the client might have.
- **Part of the culture padding block.** This is the first of several DEI/culture slides (08, 43, 44) that pad the deck without substance. Colin specifically called this pattern out.
- **Connected to internal dysfunction.** The transcripts discuss a "boys' club" dynamic (Ajit, Anuj, Amit, Suva). Trumpeting diversity numbers while internal dynamics contradict them is a credibility risk.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't pad the deck with feel-good corporate culture slides that aren't tailored to the client or required by the RFP. If DEI is an evaluation criterion, address it with substance, not a stats splash page.

## Group 2: "All Awful" (Slides 15-20, 25, 35, 38-44)

Colin flagged these as a block. They range from generic process filler to outright WIP placeholders to embarrassing resume dumps.

---

### Slide 15 -- High Level Support Process Flow (L0-L3)

**What it shows:** Complex flowchart depicting L0/L1 and L2/L3 ticket routing between BayOne Team and McGrath Team. Decision diamonds for "is this supported ticket?" and "can you resolve?" with multiple paths to resolution or 3rd-party coordination.

**What's wrong with it:**
- **Visually cluttered.** Too many shapes, arrows, decision points, and crossing paths. The flowchart is hard to follow even with careful reading. This is the kind of diagram that makes people's eyes glaze over in a presentation.
- **Completely generic.** This is how every managed services vendor handles tickets. There is nothing BayOne-specific or McGrath-specific about this flow. It's a textbook L1/L2/L3 escalation diagram that any consulting firm would show identically.
- **Doesn't demonstrate value.** The one potentially interesting note -- "Potential automation & self-help opportunities will be identified and implemented to improve cycle time & productivity" -- is buried as a tiny key callout in the bottom corner. The actual differentiator is an afterthought.
- **Process theater.** Showing this level of operational detail in an RFP response, before discovery has even happened, is premature. It suggests the process is already decided rather than being shaped by the client's needs.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't include generic process flowcharts that any vendor would present identically. If you must show process, make it specific to the client's environment and highlight what's different about your approach.

---

### Slide 16 -- Communication Model (WIP)

**What it shows:** Communication diagram with McGrath Management, McGrath Team, BayOne SMEs, and BayOne Support Engineers connected by bidirectional arrows. "ADM Practice" and "BayOne CoEs" boxes at the bottom. Marked WIP in red.

**What's wrong with it:**
- **Literally WIP.** The slide is marked as work-in-progress in red text. This should never have been included in a deck shown to anyone outside the team.
- **Boilerplate communication model.** The structure (management talks to management, team talks to team, escalations flow up, status flows down) is universal. There's nothing here that differentiates BayOne.
- **Unexplained elements.** "ADM Practice" and "BayOne CoEs" appear at the bottom with no explanation of what they are or how they add value.
- **No McGrath specificity.** Nothing about this model reflects McGrath's organizational structure, communication preferences, or operational rhythm.

**Anti-pattern:** Never include WIP-marked slides in a client-facing deck. Generic communication models don't warrant their own slide -- they're table stakes, not differentiators.

---

### Slide 17 -- BayOne Escalation Matrix (4 Levels)

**What it shows:** Four-level escalation table mapping Escalation Level to roles (Project Manager through CEO/CTO) and possible triggers (SLA breach through legal/compliance issues).

**What's wrong with it:**
- **Copy-paste boilerplate.** This is the escalation matrix that appears in every managed services proposal ever written. Levels 1-4, escalating from PM to CEO. Triggers progress from minor to major. There is zero original thought here.
- **Premature operational detail.** Escalation matrices get defined during discovery and transition, not in an RFP response. Including one this early signals naivety about the process.
- **Wastes a slide on hygiene.** The client knows you'll have an escalation process. Dedicating an entire slide to proving it makes BayOne look like they're padding content.
- **The summary sentence at the bottom is meaningless.** "A structured, multi-level escalation framework ensures rapid issue resolution, clear accountability and proactive risk mitigation across onsite and offshore teams." This is filler masquerading as insight.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't burn slides on operational tables that are identical across every vendor. Escalation matrices, RACI charts, and governance structures are execution artifacts, not sales material.

### Slide 18 -- Managed Service Governance & Reporting

**What it shows:** Complex three-tier governance model (Strategic/Tactical/Operational) mapping McGrath roles to BayOne roles, communication frequency (Quarterly/Monthly/Daily-Weekly), and reporting topics at each level.

**What's wrong with it:**
- **Overcomplicated diagram hiding generic content.** The visual complexity (three columns, three rows, icons, a communication bar, arrows, topic boxes) is enormous. But the underlying content is: "leadership meets quarterly, managers meet monthly, teams meet daily." Every vendor proposes this.
- **Buzzword nonsense.** "Futuristic Alignment" appears as a strategic reporting topic. This is meaningless jargon that would make a skeptical client cringe.
- **Generic business person icons.** The male/female silhouettes add no information and look like stock template art.
- **Visual density.** The slide is packed so tight that it's hard to parse. In a presentation, the audience would spend the entire time trying to read labels instead of listening.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't create visually complex diagrams to disguise generic content. Complexity is not sophistication. If the underlying message is simple ("we'll have regular meetings at multiple levels"), state it simply.

### Slide 19 -- Proposed Coverage (PST Time-Based Matrix)

**What it shows:** A timeline chart showing India Team and US Team coverage windows, a roles/responsibilities table splitting Onsite team and Offshore team duties, and a key callout about availability percentages.

**What's wrong with it:**
- **Cramped and overloaded.** Two different visualizations (coverage timeline + roles table) plus a key callout all jammed onto one slide. Neither gets adequate space.
- **Incomplete.** The Offshore team row has no roles listed -- only responsibilities. This is clearly unfinished.
- **Technology name-dropping without depth.** Lists "Fusion, Salesforce, Mulesoft, Avalara, RecVue, BI Reporting" as role areas but doesn't say how many people or what level for each. It's a list pretending to be a plan.
- **Standard onshore/offshore split.** The coverage model (India handles nights, US handles days) is the default arrangement for every vendor with offshore capability. Nothing differentiated.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't combine multiple complex visualizations on one cramped slide. Don't present half-finished content. And don't list technology names without tying them to a specific staffing or delivery plan.

### Slide 20 -- Assumptions (WIP)

**What it shows:** Bulleted list of assumptions about ticket volumes, discovery baselines, resource count triggers, and prerequisites. Marked WIP. Contains internal instruction in red: "Neha to pull more assumptions."

**What's wrong with it:**
- **Internal instructions visible.** "Neha to pull more assumptions" in red text at the bottom. This is a catastrophic slip if shown to a client. It reveals the slide isn't finished and names an internal team member.
- **Marked WIP prominently.** The WIP tag is clearly visible in the slide header.
- **Self-contradicting.** The assumptions state ticket volumes "in the absence of ticket volume" -- acknowledging the numbers are made up. Combined with the context from transcripts (Kishore has offered to get actual ticket data), this shows the deck was assembled without waiting for available intel.
- **Incomplete bullets.** "COLA" appears as a standalone bullet with no context or explanation.
- **Mix of useful and useless.** Some assumptions are legitimate (production replica environment, knowledge transfer sessions). But they're mixed with WIP garbage, making the whole slide look unprofessional.

**Anti-pattern:** NEVER leave internal instructions, team member names, or TODO markers visible on slides. If a slide is WIP, either finish it or remove it. Internal working documents and client-facing content must be kept separate.

## Cross-Cutting Observation

The 18 skipped slides share a root cause: **they were assembled by someone who thinks like a staffing company (Amit), not a solutions vendor (Colin).** The deck defaults to proving BayOne is a large, diverse, process-heavy organization with global reach -- exactly the pitch a staffing firm makes. What McGrath actually needs to see is: technical depth in Oracle Fusion, credible AI capabilities, realistic staffing for their specific systems, and evidence of past managed services delivery.

The single most damaging pattern across all 18 slides is the conflation of "about BayOne as a company" with "about BayOne's ability to serve McGrath." Every slide that talks about BayOne's global offices, diversity stats, employee benefits, L&D platforms, or mom relaunch programs is a slide that doesn't talk about Oracle Fusion, Mulesoft integrations, ticket resolution, or AI-enabled operations -- the things McGrath is actually buying.
