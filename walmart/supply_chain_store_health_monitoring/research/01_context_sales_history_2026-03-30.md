## Overview

This document reconstructs the complete chronology of two rounds of sales engagement with Walmart, the recurring patterns Colin identified in each, his approach to addressing them, and the open questions that remain. The central theme is not a single incident but a systemic problem: the sales team's repeated failure to do basic preparation, communicate context, and accurately represent BayOne's AI capabilities before pulling leadership onto client calls.

## Round 1: The Unauthorized Deck

### Chronology

1. **Amit sends a deck to a Walmart contact.** The deck was not reviewed, approved, or even seen by Colin. It positioned BayOne as having pre-built AI accelerators, chatbot demos, agent-based solutions, and a potential standalone product. None of this exists at BayOne.

2. **Kanchan reaches out to Colin after the fact.** She relays the Walmart lead's questions, which include requests for a demo of the AI capabilities, exploration of whether this can be a standalone product, and a feature and functionality list. These questions are entirely the product of expectations Amit set.

3. **Colin responds to Kanchan.** This is where the dynamics become important. Colin's response was warm, transparent, and firm. He:
   - Made it explicitly clear that none of this was Kanchan's fault.
   - Named Amit as the source of the problem. He did not allow this to float as a vague "miscommunication." He said directly that Amit had been told before that these capabilities do not exist, and that Amit likely confused case study work from Colin's prior organization (Coherent Corp) with demo-able products BayOne can claim.
   - Refused to own unknown commitments. His exact framing: "I can't speak to what was promised, and I won't be able to own any commitments that were made without my input."
   - Said directly that if something was communicated to Walmart that BayOne cannot back up, it needs to be walked back by whoever sent it.
   - Redirected the conversation to what actually works: understanding Walmart's real problem rather than leading with a product catalog that does not exist.
   - Offered to help Kanchan take a different angle. He did not just shut things down. He offered to walk through real capabilities and find a better approach.

4. **Kanchan thanks Colin and asks for a sync with Amit.** She is receptive, appreciative, and appears to genuinely understand.

5. **Kanchan follows up separately** asking for "a script for our AI work" to send to Walmart. This is a softer version of the same problem: asking Colin to produce sales content for capabilities that should be framed by the sales team.

6. **Colin declines the script request.** He frames it as a sales responsibility, not a limitation on his end. He does not diminish himself or overexplain. He offers to connect and help from a better direction, but makes clear that writing sales pitches is not his job.

### What Makes Colin's Round 1 Approach Notable

The strategy behind Colin's messaging in Round 1 was deliberate. Each element served a purpose:

- **Warmth to Kanchan** preserved the relationship and acknowledged her position as a messenger. She was not the source of the problem and Colin made sure she knew that.
- **Naming Amit directly** prevented the situation from being diffused into vague organizational confusion. It established a clear chain of accountability: Amit was told before, Amit sent the deck, Amit is the repeat offender.
- **Refusing to own unknown commitments** established a boundary that prevents Colin from being retroactively made responsible for promises he had no part in making.
- **Redirecting to problem-first** reframed the conversation from "what can we demo" to "what is their actual need," which is both the honest approach and the approach more likely to produce real business.
- **Offering to help differently** ensured that the boundary did not read as refusal to engage. Colin was clear about what he would not do and equally clear about what he would do.

### Round 1 Resolution Status

Round 1 was resolved between Colin and Kanchan. It was **not** resolved with Walmart. As of the date of this document:

- It is unknown whether the sync with Amit ever happened.
- Whatever was promised to Walmart via the deck has not been walked back.
- It is unknown whether Kanchan connected with Colin for the "different angle" conversation before Round 2 began.
- The Walmart contact from Round 1 has not been identified by name. It is unknown whether this person is Troy Ward (the Round 2 contact) or someone else entirely.

The unresolved Walmart expectations from Round 1 remain a ticking clock. If Troy Ward is the same person or is connected to the person who received the deck, he may arrive at the Round 2 call with misaligned expectations that no one on BayOne's side has corrected.

## Round 2: Communication Gaps and Zero Preparation

### Chronology

1. **Surej asks Colin in person on Friday to join a Walmart call.** The call is scheduled for Monday at noon ET. The only context Surej provides is a LinkedIn profile for Troy Ward. Surej describes Troy as being "responsible for robotics and automation within Walmart." The referral came through someone Surej knows personally, giving the opportunity real social capital.

2. **The meeting title reads "Showcasing BayOne's AI Capabilities."** This was Kanchan's doing. It does not match Surej's intent, and Surej does not know why she titled it that way.

3. **Monday morning: Colin messages Surej.** Colin asks whether Kanchan or the sales team has shared any details or background. He flags the meeting title as a concern. He asks what he should have prepped. He notes that he is going in blind. The tone is direct but appropriate for a CEO conversation.

4. **Surej responds.** His response is encouraging. He sees this as discovery, not a showcase. He explicitly says "we could use this time to understand more about his requirements." He acknowledges that the sales team does not have enough details on first calls. He offers to handle the call solo if Colin is tied up. His framing is actually well-aligned with Colin's philosophy.

5. **Monday morning: Colin messages Kanchan.** He asks for five specific pieces of context:
   1. Details on who they are meeting with and their role at Walmart
   2. What conversations have already happened and what expectations have been set
   3. What business BayOne is doing for this person or their team already
   4. Any specific articulated AI interest
   5. What the objective is for this meeting

   He then draws a hard boundary: if there is an expectation that he has prepared slides or will be presenting on specific topics, the meeting should be rescheduled. He would not have time to prepare anything. If it is a casual meet and greet, that is fine, but he will decline if there is a presentation expectation. He requests a pre-call sync. The tone is firm but fair. No hostility.

6. **Kanchan responds: "I am driving to Office right now. I will send it to you shortly."** It is approximately 9:30 AM Pacific. The call is at noon ET (9:00 AM Pacific). The sales team member responsible for this meeting is commuting to the office 2.5 hours before the call and has shared nothing.

7. **Colin crafts his message to Surej.** This is the calibrated escalation. Colin's final message to Surej:
   - Opens with warmth and loyalty: "You should definitely not be on the hook for this alone. Even if it is not the right flow for me, it should still be my problem before it is our CEO's problem."
   - Confirms alignment on discovery.
   - Explains he wanted to make sure nobody on the other end is expecting a presentation or demo, because he would not have had anything to go on even if he wanted to prepare one.
   - Names the pattern explicitly: "I know I keep raising the same issue, but this is the exact recurring pattern I keep flagging with sales."
   - Provides the concrete evidence: "It's 2.5 hrs before the call and I still have zero context from Kanchan/ Richa; she just now messaged me to tell me she's driving into the office."
   - Delivers the signature observation: "The fact that you're the one who sent me his LinkedIn profile and not one word from the sales team really highlights the pattern."
   - Closes with the assessment Colin crafted carefully: "In this case it is less a technical knowledge gap than a basic communication and preparation problem."
   - Ends optimistic: "We will get through and hopefully make a deal out of this. Looking forward to it!"

8. **Kanchan eventually provides answers to Colin's five questions.** The responses arrive piecemeal over the next hour or so. Key details:
   - Troy Ward is Director of IT Operations in Supply Chain at Walmart. Not "robotics and automation." Colin suspects Kanchan heard "robotic process automation" and interpreted it as physical robotics and automation. Unconfirmed but plausible given the actual role description.
   - No existing business with Troy's team.
   - AI interest: Predict outages before they happen. Real-time monitoring of all store tech systems.
   - Meeting objective: How BayOne can embed AI across POS, self-checkout, payment systems, store networks, associate devices, IoT infrastructure.
   - Kanchan also mentioned that she told Troy BayOne has been working on AI solutions focused on improving IT support and operations, especially around automating issue resolution, improving ticket quality, and enabling faster troubleshooting.

9. **Red flag on Question 2.** Colin asked "What conversations have already happened and what expectations have been set?" Kanchan's answer was the Multimodal SFT case study that Colin himself had just mentioned to her moments earlier. She echoed his words back as her answer. This is not an answer to the question. It is unclear whether Kanchan relayed this to Troy in real time or whether she had any prior context about the case study before Colin brought it up.

10. **Kanchan clarifies what was shared with Troy.** She did NOT send any materials, decks, or written descriptions. Everything was verbal. She told him BayOne has experience with multimodal AI and supervised fine-tuned models for predictive diagnostics, and said "we can talk more deeply about how we did all of this." She did not mention providing a demo.

11. **Colin's response to Kanchan after the clarification** is deliberately warm and encouraging:
    - Thanks her for getting information back and notes she is the only one who responded. He also messaged Richa, who did not respond.
    - Tells her that her instincts were right: connecting Troy's interest to the multimodal SFT case study was the correct move, and framing it as experience rather than a product or demo was exactly how it should be positioned.
    - Is transparent about the capacity challenge: if this opportunity is real, BayOne does not have bandwidth for immediate new work until the team grows. He frames this explicitly as not her problem and something he has been pushing leadership on for months.
    - Closes with encouragement for a good call.

12. **Kanchan provides a refined problem statement.** Troy's primary challenge: establishing effective health checks and early diagnostic capabilities for in-store machines, allowing issues to be identified and resolved before they impact store performance. This is more focused than the earlier laundry list and gives the call a real anchor.

## Patterns Identified

Colin identified three patterns across Rounds 1 and 2. These are not isolated incidents. They are recurring behaviors that Colin has been flagging with leadership.

### Pattern 3: Promising Things That Do Not Exist (Primary, Round 1)

Amit sent a deck to Walmart, without Colin's review or input, that positioned BayOne as having pre-built AI accelerators, chatbot demos, agent-based solutions, and a potential standalone product. None of this exists. Amit had been told this before. He likely confused case study work from Colin's prior organization (Coherent Corp) with capabilities BayOne can claim or demo. This is the most severe pattern because it creates client expectations that cannot be met and puts whoever follows (in this case, Colin) in the position of either lying or walking it back.

### Pattern 5: Committing Colin's Time Without Asking (Secondary, Round 1; Echoed in Round 2)

In Round 1, Colin was brought in after the fact to deliver on promises he never made and did not know about. The deck was sent without him ever seeing it. In Round 2, the echo is softer: Colin was told Friday, given a LinkedIn profile, and the call is Monday. That is not enough lead time for anything beyond showing up and listening. The Round 2 version is less egregious because Surej framed it as discovery and offered to handle it alone, but the underlying dynamic is the same: Colin's participation is assumed without adequate context or lead time.

### Pattern 1: "Come Present Our AI Capabilities" (Tertiary, Both Rounds)

In Round 1, Kanchan's second ask (a "script" for AI work to send to Walmart) is a softer version of this pattern: asking Colin to produce sales content for capabilities that should be framed by the sales team. In Round 2, the meeting title itself ("Showcasing BayOne's AI Capabilities") embodies this pattern. The title does not match Surej's intent (discovery), does not match what was communicated to Troy (experience discussion), and creates a risk of expectation mismatch if Troy saw it and is expecting a product demo or capability walkthrough.

### The Overarching Pattern: Zero Preparation and Zero Communication

Across both rounds, the sales team failed to do basic sales work before involving leadership. Specific failures in Round 2:

- No context shared with Colin before Monday morning of the call.
- No context shared with Surej beyond his own personal referral knowledge.
- The CEO himself sent Colin the LinkedIn profile. The sales team did not.
- The sales team member (Kanchan) was driving to the office 2.5 hours before the call and had shared nothing.
- Colin had to proactively message both Surej and Kanchan to get any context at all.
- Richa (another sales team member) was also messaged by Colin and did not respond.
- The meeting title was set by Kanchan as "Showcasing BayOne's AI Capabilities" without coordination with Surej, Colin, or any understanding of what the call should actually be.
- The description of Troy's role ("robotics and automation") was likely a misinterpretation of "robotic process automation." No one on the sales team verified this against his LinkedIn profile or asked a clarifying question.

Colin's assessment of this pattern, articulated to Surej: **"In this case it is less a technical knowledge gap than a basic communication and preparation problem."** The point Colin was making: do not give the sales team an excuse because they come from staffing and do not understand AI. The failures here have nothing to do with technical understanding. Looking up a LinkedIn profile is not technical. Articulating who a referral is, what their role is, what they care about, and what the meeting objective is requires no AI knowledge. Those are fundamental sales functions that were simply not performed.

## Colin's Approach: Strategy and Calibration

### With Kanchan: Warm, Direct, Constructive

Across both rounds, Colin's approach to Kanchan was consistent:

- **Round 1:** Made it clear none of the problems were her fault. Named Amit as the source. Offered constructive paths forward. Declined requests that were not his responsibility (the script) without being dismissive. Positioned the redirect as an opportunity, not a rejection.
- **Round 2 (outbound):** Sent a firm but fair list of five specific questions. Drew a hard boundary on presentations. Requested a pre-call sync. No hostility, but no ambiguity about expectations.
- **Round 2 (after Kanchan responded):** Shifted to warmth and encouragement. Recognized that her instincts on the case study positioning were correct. Thanked her for being the only one who responded. Gave her credit. Was transparent about the capacity issue without making it her burden.

The strategy: Kanchan is a potential ally who is junior and following patterns set by others (primarily Amit). If she is supported and coached rather than criticized, she could become a salesperson who actually learns the AI side. Colin is investing in that possibility while maintaining clear boundaries.

### With Surej: Aligned, Transparent, Loyal

Colin's messages to Surej were carefully calibrated:

- **Opened with loyalty:** "You should definitely not be on the hook for this alone. Even if it is not the right flow for me, it should still be my problem before it is our CEO's problem." This ensured Surej did not feel criticized or abandoned.
- **Confirmed alignment:** Discovery is exactly the right approach. Colin and Surej are on the same page.
- **Named the risk without being alarmist:** Flagged the meeting title and the lack of context. Did not catastrophize.
- **Escalated the pattern clearly:** Named it as the same recurring pattern he has been raising. Provided concrete evidence (2.5 hours before the call, driving to the office, CEO is the one who sent the LinkedIn profile).
- **Closed the escape hatch:** "In this case it is less a technical knowledge gap than a basic communication and preparation problem." This line was deliberately crafted to prevent Surej from dismissing the issue as "they're staffing people, they don't understand AI." Colin's point: the failures here are not about AI knowledge. They are about not doing the homework, not communicating, not sharing what little they had.
- **Ended forward-looking:** "We will get through and hopefully make a deal out of this."

The strategy: Surej is aware of the sales team patterns. He does not need the full case built out. Colin gave him enough to see the problem clearly, framed it as an ongoing systemic issue (not a one-time complaint), and closed the door on the most likely excuse, all without making Surej feel lectured or blamed.

### What Colin Did Not Do

Equally important:

- **Did not overreact in Round 2.** Despite the pattern history, Colin read the room. Surej's framing was reasonable. This was not a pure Amit-driven mess like Round 1. Colin calibrated his response accordingly.
- **Did not keep score publicly.** An early draft of his message to Surej included a parenthetical about sitting next to Richa and Kanchan for five hours without hearing a word. Colin cut it. The observation was true but made it look like he was keeping a scoreboard, which Surej did not need to see.
- **Did not burn Kanchan.** Even when flagging the pattern to Surej, Colin kept the focus on the systemic issue, not on Kanchan specifically. He mentioned Kanchan and Richa by name as evidence (zero context from either), but the framing was about the sales team as a function, not about individual people failing.
- **Did not pretend the situation was fine.** Colin did not downplay the problem to avoid discomfort. He raised it, documented it, and moved forward.

## The Distinction: Intent vs. Execution

A recurring theme in Colin's assessment: the sales team's intent is not malicious. Kanchan is not trying to sabotage anything. She is following patterns set by leadership (Amit), operating without adequate training or process, and doing her best with what she has. When given direct guidance, she responds well. Her instinct on connecting the case study to Troy's interest was correct. She was the only person who responded to Colin's requests for information.

The problem is execution:

- **No homework is done before calls.** LinkedIn profiles are not reviewed. Roles are misidentified. Basic context about who someone is and what they care about is not gathered.
- **No information is shared proactively.** Colin has to ask for everything. Nothing arrives without a request.
- **Meeting titles and framing are set without coordination.** "Showcasing BayOne's AI Capabilities" was set by Kanchan without input from Surej or Colin, and it does not match anyone else's understanding of the call's purpose.
- **Capabilities are misrepresented.** In Round 1 (Amit), this was egregious: an entire deck of nonexistent products. In Round 2, it is more subtle: Kanchan titled the meeting as a showcase and described Troy's role inaccurately. But the direction is the same.
- **Timing is inadequate.** Colin learned about the Round 2 call on Friday for a Monday meeting. Context arrived in fragments 2.5 hours before the call. The sales team member responsible was commuting to the office when she should have been sending prep materials.

Colin's framing: this is not a technical knowledge gap. It is a communication and preparation problem. The sales team does not need to understand AI to do the baseline work of identifying who they are meeting with, what that person cares about, and what the meeting is for.
