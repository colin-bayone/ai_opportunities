## 4. Proposal Strategy

### Phase-Based Architecture

Anuj's direction: structure the proposal in phases with hooks between them.

- **Phase 1:** Start with the smallest defensible piece. Solve the immediate problem.
- **Phase 2:** Put a hook at the end of Phase 1 that leads to a bigger piece.
- **Phase 3+:** Scale out from there.

The reasoning: Lam doesn't have the vision to absorb a big-picture proposal right now. They need to see the small thing work first, then BayOne creates hooks to expand scope. This is how Deloitte and Capgemini have been embedded at Lam for years -- they've been "booting off them" long-term.

### Don't Dump Everything on Their Plate

Both Colin and Anuj agree: Lam will not understand the full scope of what's possible. The proposal needs to be digestible. Colin's temptation is to "boil the ocean" (because the work is easy enough to justify it), but Anuj's counsel is piecemeal -- start slow, embed, expand.

Colin's internal position is actually that boiling the ocean is justified here ("this is one where I would actually say boil the ocean... it's so easy frankly"), but he defers to Anuj's sequencing instinct for how to present it.

## 7. Key Tactical Observations from the Debrief

### Lam's Technical Level (Colin's Unfiltered Assessment)

- What they're asking for is "probably the easiest thing we've ever had dumped in our lives"
- The entire use case is "pretty much a RAG chatbot. That's it."
- "All this complexity they're talking about doesn't exist. It's in their own mind. They're creating it."
- The customer name redaction thing is "essentially a synonyms lookup table" -- not even an AI project
- Colin prepared to talk about Azure Purview, Azure Sentinel, sophisticated tooling -- none of it was relevant
- Their approach was described as "someone literally bought an AI 101 book for a teenager, flipped to the first chapter, read it, and said okay, let's do this for a $60 billion enterprise"

### Prior Partners' Work

- Accenture and Capgemini are the two major partners that have been embedded at Lam
- They tried a "computer science 101 approach to classifying text and got stuck"
- This is what prior partners delivered, and Lam has been paying for it
- Deloitte and Capgemini have been "booting off them for years" -- the sticky, long-term partner model is proven at Lam

### Specific Technical Misconceptions Colin Noted

- **Redaction vs. identification:** One participant talked about redaction as separate from identification. Colin's reaction: "How do you do redaction without identifying what needs to be redacted? That doesn't make any sense."
- **"The tools aren't structured":** Pradeep said something to this effect on the call. Colin's response: "That is completely false. They've been very mature for years. Defense companies use them." Colin sees this as an enablement opportunity -- correct the misconception and build trust.
- **IAM terminology:** Lam was talking about IAM. Colin's note: "That's called RBAC. No one calls it IAM. IAM is the Microsoft term for it. It's role-based access control."

### What Surprised Colin

- A Fortune 10 company with no confidence in this space and no in-house expertise
- They work with federal clients and still don't have their document handling sorted
- They have not had an actual security incident despite their current approach (Colin is surprised)
- Engineers will "find a way to get through the door if it is locked" -- Lam doesn't seem to understand this about their own people

### Business Justification (Already Done for BayOne)

RAG chatbots typically have a bad business justification because it's hard to prove adoption. But Lam has already done the homework: they've articulated the value, listed what they've tried, and they're already sold on the concept. BayOne doesn't have to make the business case -- Lam made it for them.
