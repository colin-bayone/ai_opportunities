When AI Gets It Wrong

Not every mistake is a "hallucination." There are five distinct ways AI output can go wrong, and understanding which one happened changes how you respond to it.

True Hallucination

The model fabricates something with no basis in any input or training data. It invents a legal citation, a statistic, or a person who does not exist. This happens because language models generate plausible-sounding sequences, not verified facts.

AI made it up

Inaccuracy from Source Data

The model faithfully reproduces information that was wrong in what it was given. The training data had an error, or you uploaded a document with a mistake, and the model reflected it back. Garbage in, garbage out.

The data was wrong

Incoherent Prompt

The user asked a bad question, gave contradictory instructions, or framed something in a way that led the model astray. AI gave back exactly what was asked for. The question itself was the problem.

The question was flawed

Outdated Information

The model's training data has a cutoff date. It gives a confident answer based on information that was accurate when trained but has since changed. Tax law, case law, insurance regulations, and financial products all move faster than models get retrained.

The data aged out

Personal Bias

The user disagrees with the output, but the output is not actually wrong. It conflicts with a belief, preference, or assumption, and the instinct is to call it an error. Model bias and human bias are both real, but they are different problems with different solutions.

The reader disagreed

Three of these five failure modes are about the human side, not AI itself. One is about the training data's age. Only one is AI genuinely making things up. This gives you a vocabulary for diagnosing what actually went wrong instead of just saying "AI hallucinated."
