## Core Principle

**RFP Q&A submissions are PUBLIC.**

Every question you ask, every competitor sees. The Q&A document becomes part of the RFP record, distributed to all bidders.

This creates a paradox:
- You NEED information to price accurately
- Asking reveals what you don't know
- Large incumbents already have information
- Small bidders are at a disadvantage

---

## The Information Asymmetry

### Large MSPs / Incumbents

- Have 100-200 embedded resources
- Get answers through informal channels
- Know operational reality firsthand
- Use Q&A to clarify minor ambiguities only
- Sometimes ask "softballs" that make them look good

### Smaller Bidders

- Depend heavily on Q&A for information
- Every question reveals a gap in knowledge
- Must balance information gathering vs. risk exposure
- Need to be strategic about what and how they ask

### The Implication

A small bidder asking "What's the current FTE count?" signals they don't know.
An incumbent asking the same question might be validating or creating a paper trail.

The context matters, but you can't control how evaluators interpret intent.

## What Questions Reveal

### Capability Gaps

Questions that suggest you lack experience:

| Signal | What Evaluators Think |
|--------|----------------------|
| "What tools does MGRC currently use?" | "They don't know our stack" |
| "How complex is the Oracle implementation?" | "They haven't done this scale" |
| "What's the typical error rate?" | "They can't estimate without this" |

### Risk Awareness

Questions that show what you're worried about:

| Signal | What Evaluators Think |
|--------|----------------------|
| Multiple timeline questions | "They're worried about delivery" |
| Scope boundary clarifications | "They're trying to limit liability" |
| Escalation path questions | "They expect problems" |

### Strategic Approach

Questions that reveal your plan:

| Signal | What Evaluators Think |
|--------|----------------------|
| "Is multi-vendor acceptable?" | "They're planning a consortium" |
| "Is phased go-live acceptable?" | "They want to reduce risk" |
| "Is variable pricing acceptable?" | "They want flexibility to adjust" |

### Commercial Positioning

Questions that telegraph pricing strategy:

| Signal | What Evaluators Think |
|--------|----------------------|
| Heavy focus on variable hours | "They want scope creep revenue" |
| Questions about multi-year escalation | "They're planning low Year 1, higher later" |
| Questions about rebadging | "They want to inherit cheap staff" |

## Question Types by Risk

### Low Risk (Standard Questions)

Every bidder asks these. Safe to include:

- Ticket/request volume by priority
- Current FTE counts by role
- Technology stack details
- Contract term and renewal process
- Evaluation criteria weighting

### Medium Risk (Context Dependent)

May reveal concern or approach. Evaluate carefully:

- Timeline flexibility questions
- Scope boundary clarifications
- Staffing model preferences
- Pricing structure flexibility

### High Risk (Strategy Revealing)

Often better saved for orals or removed:

- Specific approach suggestions embedded in questions
- "Would MGRC be open to..." with detailed approach
- Questions that assume a particular delivery model
- Questions about incumbent advantage

### Very High Risk (Avoid)

Should almost never be submitted:

- Questions that highlight competitor advantages
- Questions that reveal specific weaknesses
- Questions that assume you'll lose certain areas
- Aggressive questions about evaluation fairness

## Revision Strategies

### Strategy 1: Generalize Specifics

**Before:** "For vendors with existing embedded resources at MGRC, how will institutional knowledge be weighted?"

**After:** "How will the evaluation weight institutional knowledge and transition planning?"

**Why:** Removes the self-reference while getting similar information.

### Strategy 2: Frame as Best Practice

**Before:** "Is MGRC open to a phased go-live to reduce risk?"

**After:** "Does MGRC have preferences on go-live approach (big bang vs. phased)?"

**Why:** Positions as information gathering, not risk mitigation.

### Strategy 3: Neutralize Commercial Preference

**Before:** "Is MGRC open to a hybrid model combining fixed retainer with T&M for projects?"

**After:** "Is MGRC open to pricing models that combine fixed support with variable capacity?"

**Why:** Gets flexibility information without revealing exact preference.

### Strategy 4: Convert to Process Question

**Before:** "Will we have access to existing documentation and SMEs during transition?"

**After:** "What is the expected KT approach, including documentation and SME availability?"

**Why:** Standard process question rather than concern about information access.

### Strategy 5: Remove Entirely

Some questions are better asked in orals or not at all:

- Questions that only matter if you win
- Questions where the answer is obvious but embarrassing
- Questions that reveal specific proposal weaknesses

## Patterns to Avoid

### Pattern 1: Clustering Sensitive Questions

**Problem:** 3-4 questions in a row about timeline flexibility
**Inference:** "This bidder is worried about timeline"
**Solution:** Spread sensitive questions across categories

### Pattern 2: Leading Questions

**Problem:** "Given the complexity of Oracle Fusion, would MGRC consider extended timelines?"
**Inference:** "They think this is too complex for them"
**Solution:** Ask neutral questions about scope, not loaded questions about your capabilities

### Pattern 3: Obvious Self-Interest

**Problem:** "For incumbent vendors who know the environment, will there be evaluation credit?"
**Inference:** "They're trying to tilt the playing field"
**Solution:** Either ask neutrally or don't ask at all

### Pattern 4: Over-Engineering

**Problem:** Asking for detailed runbooks, specific procedures, granular metrics
**Inference:** "They need this level of detail to operate - they don't know what they're doing"
**Solution:** Ask for operational indicators, not implementation procedures

### Pattern 5: Contradiction Patterns

**Problem:** Asking about multi-vendor split while also asking about single-vendor benefits
**Inference:** "They're confused about their own approach"
**Solution:** Pick one angle; don't hedge in Q&A

## Patterns That Strengthen Position

### Pattern 1: Due Diligence Questions

Questions that show thorough analysis:

- "For NexSTAR, can MGRC provide architecture documentation including deployment pipeline?"
- "What is the PCI scope for Customer Hub - does MSP maintain PCI certification?"

These show you understand the complexity and are asking the right questions.

### Pattern 2: Operational Maturity Questions

Questions that show you understand operations:

- "For period-close activities, are there seasonal peaks requiring surge staffing?"
- "What is the typical intervention frequency for overnight data pipelines?"

These show you've done similar work and know what to ask.

### Pattern 3: Governance Questions

Questions that show you think about process:

- "Does MGRC have existing change management policies for the MSP to adopt?"
- "What is the process for scope changes identified post-contract?"

These show you're thinking about sustainable operations, not just winning.

### Pattern 4: Strategic Alignment Questions

Questions that show you understand their goals:

- "What role does MGRC envision AI and automation playing in managed services?"
- "As MGRC consolidates vendor relationships, what qualities are most valued?"

These show you're thinking about partnership, not just transaction.

## Summary: The Litmus Test

For every question, ask:

1. **Does this reveal a gap?** If yes, is the information worth it?
2. **Does this reveal a concern?** If yes, can we reframe?
3. **Does this reveal strategy?** If yes, should we neutralize?
4. **Does this strengthen position?** If yes, keep it.
5. **Would an incumbent ask this?** If no, reconsider.

When in doubt, save for orals. You can always ask verbally what you can't ask in writing.
