## 1. Contrastive Rhetorical Framing

The most common AI tell. States what something ISN'T before saying what it IS.

### Patterns to Catch

- "This isn't X, it's Y"
- "It's not just X, it's Y"
- "This isn't about X. It's about Y."
- "We're not just building X. We're building Y."
- "The challenge isn't X. The challenge is Y."
- "This is more than X. It's Y."

### Examples

| AI Pattern | Professional Fix |
|------------|------------------|
| "This isn't just about efficiency. It's about transformation." | "This improves efficiency and transforms operations." |
| "We're not just building software. We're building the future." | "We're building software that shapes how teams work." |
| "This isn't a skinning exercise." | "This requires deep architectural work." |
| "The POC isn't just a demo. It's heavy lifting." | "The POC delivers working infrastructure, not just demonstration." |

### Fix

State what something IS directly. Remove the negative framing entirely.

## 2. Em-Dash Overuse

AI uses em-dashes constantly, even when commas or periods work better.

### Patterns to Catch

- Multiple em-dashes in a single sentence
- Em-dashes where commas suffice
- More than 5 em-dashes in an entire document
- Triplet em-dash patterns: "X— Y— Z"

### Examples

| AI Pattern | Professional Fix |
|------------|------------------|
| "Our solution—powered by cutting-edge technology—delivers results." | "Our solution, powered by cutting-edge technology, delivers results." |
| "end-to-end— UI and backend logic— using a methodology" | "end-to-end (UI and backend logic) using a methodology" |

### Fix

Replace 90% of em-dashes with commas, parentheses, or periods. Reserve dashes for true interruptions.

## 3. Rhetorical Questions with Fragment Answers

AI asks questions and immediately answers them with fragments.

### Patterns to Catch

- "The result? [Fragment]."
- "Our secret? [Fragment]."
- "Why does this matter? Simple."
- "What's the answer? [One word]."
- Question followed by "No." or "Yes." as a paragraph

### Examples

| AI Pattern | Professional Fix |
|------------|------------------|
| "The result? Unprecedented growth." | "The result was unprecedented growth." |
| "Does 100 screens take 150 weeks? No." | "The timeline does not scale linearly." |

### Fix

Make statements. Do not interview yourself.

## 4. Triple Patterns

AI obsessively groups things in threes.

### Patterns to Catch

- "Fast. Reliable. Secure."
- "We innovate, integrate, and iterate."
- Three-item bullet lists that feel forced
- Triplet adjectives before nouns

### Examples

| AI Pattern | Professional Fix |
|------------|------------------|
| "Fast. Reliable. Secure." | "Fast and secure" or "Reliable performance" |
| "We innovate, integrate, and iterate." | "We continuously improve through integration." |

### Fix

Vary groupings. Use pairs, singles, or longer lists.

## 5. First Person in Business Documents

Business proposals use organizational voice, not personal pronouns.

### Patterns to Catch

- "I will..." in proposals
- "I'm working..."
- Referring to oneself by name within the document
- "My approach..."

### Examples

| AI Pattern | Professional Fix |
|------------|------------------|
| "I'm working solo and sequentially." | "BayOne will staff this engagement with a single senior resource." |
| "I'll confirm the date." | "BayOne will confirm the date." |
| "Colin solo" | "A senior BayOne resource" |

### Fix

Use organizational voice: "BayOne will...", "The team will...", "This engagement..."

## 6. Blog-Style Headers

Professional documents use descriptive headers, not catchy ones.

### Patterns to Catch

- Question headers: "Why This Matters"
- Cute/clever headers: "The Flywheel Effect"
- Casual headers: "The Math"
- Headers with parenthetical asides: "One-Time Investment (Happens Once)"

### Examples

| AI Pattern | Professional Fix |
|------------|------------------|
| "The Flywheel Effect" | "Acceleration Mechanism" or "Incremental Efficiency Gains" |
| "The Math" | "Staffing and Velocity Model" |
| "Why This Is Vertical Work" | "Vertical Integration Requirements" |

### Fix

Use formal, descriptive headers that explain content without being catchy.

## 7. Colloquial Language

Professional documents avoid slang and casual idioms.

### Patterns to Catch

- "skinning exercise"
- "baked into"
- "heavy lifting"
- "blind guesses"
- "trivial wins"
- "Happy to discuss"
- "happen to work"

### Examples

| AI Pattern | Professional Fix |
|------------|------------------|
| "skinning exercise" | "surface-level UI modification" |
| "baked into the code" | "embedded within the codebase" |
| "heavy lifting" | "foundational work" or "infrastructure development" |
| "Happy to discuss" | "BayOne welcomes the opportunity to discuss" |

### Fix

Replace casual language with professional equivalents.

## 8. Inspirational Pivots

AI shifts from specific to abstract to create fake profundity.

### Patterns to Catch

- "This isn't just about data. It's about human potential."
- "It's about creating a legacy that transcends..."
- "Together, we'll turn your challenge into an opportunity for growth!"
- Sudden shifts to grand abstractions

### Examples

| AI Pattern | Professional Fix |
|------------|------------------|
| "This isn't just about data. It's about human potential." | "Better data helps teams make confident decisions." |
| "It's about YOU becoming the leader our world desperately needs!" | (Delete entirely) |

### Fix

Stay concrete. Avoid grand abstractions. Delete inspirational fluff.

---

## 9. Universal Claims Without Evidence

AI makes sweeping statements without sources.

### Patterns to Catch

- "Studies show that..."
- "Everyone knows that..."
- "It's widely recognized that..."
- Unattributed statistics

### Examples

| AI Pattern | Professional Fix |
|------------|------------------|
| "Studies show that 87% of executives prefer our approach." | "In our 2024 client survey, 87% of executives preferred our approach." |
| "Everyone knows that digital transformation is essential." | "Digital transformation has become essential for competitive advantage." |

### Fix

Cite real sources or avoid universal claims.

## 10. Filler Words

Certain words signal AI padding.

### Patterns to Catch

- "just" as dismissive filler ("This isn't just...")
- "actually" as emphasis filler ("how teams actually work")
- "really" without purpose
- "very" without necessity
- "truly" for false emphasis

### Examples

| AI Pattern | Professional Fix |
|------------|------------------|
| "how experienced teams actually work" | "how experienced teams work" |
| "This is really important" | "This is important" |

### Fix

Delete filler words. They add nothing.

---

## 11. Emoji Usage

Professional documents never use emojis.

### Patterns to Catch

- Any emoji in formal documents
- Emoji in headers or bullet points
- Multiple emojis in casual contexts

### Fix

Zero emojis in formal communication. Maximum one in casual contexts (rare).

---

## 12. Contractions in Formal Documents

Heavy contraction use contributes to casual tone.

### Patterns to Catch

- "isn't", "aren't", "don't", "doesn't", "won't"
- "we're", "they're", "you're", "it's", "that's"
- "we'll", "I'll", "I'm"

### Fix

Eliminate contractions in formal sections. "We are" not "We're". "Does not" not "Doesn't".

---

## The Ultimate Test

Before approving any document, verify:

1. Would this sound natural if read aloud to the client?
2. Are there specific details that demonstrate real understanding?
3. Does it sound like a professional on Tuesday, not someone giving a TED talk?
4. Have all em-dashes, rhetorical questions, and "it's not just" phrases been removed?
5. Is every claim supported by specific evidence?
6. Would a Big Four consultant be embarrassed to send this?
