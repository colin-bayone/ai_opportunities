# Sales Boundary Playbook

## Colin's Role and Where It Starts and Stops

Colin is Director of AI at BayOne Solutions. His role is to assess real opportunities, scope solutions, lead discovery on qualified engagements, and deliver AI solutions. He is not a salesperson. He is the closer, not the opener.

### What Colin Does

- Assesses qualified opportunities to determine if they make sense for BayOne
- Leads discovery and scoping calls when there is a confirmed problem or interest area
- Provides quick pointers and talking points to help sales frame AI in their conversations (this is a five-minute Teams message, not a meeting)
- Builds out real approaches and proposals when there's something concrete to work with
- Joins calls where a client has a known problem statement and wants to explore how BayOne can help

### What Colin Does Not Do

- Identify or source leads
- Qualify whether an opportunity is real, has budget, or has a decision maker
- Join introductory or exploratory calls with no vetting or agenda
- Create generic AI capability decks or presentations
- Present broad AI capabilities to generate interest (that is a sales pitch, not a solutions conversation)
- Do research, build slides, or prep talking points for meetings that sales committed to without consulting him
- Own deliverables or commitments that were promised to a client without his input

---

## The Process That Works

This is the pipeline that has been producing results. Colin's involvement should only begin after steps 1-4 are complete.

### Step 1: Sales identifies a potential lead
This is the sales team's core function. LinkedIn, referrals, existing relationships, whatever the source.

### Step 2: Sales qualifies the lead
Before anyone else gets involved, sales must answer these questions:

- Is there actual interest from the client themselves (confirmed, not assumed)?
- Is there a real problem or pain point?
- Can this person actually give us business? Are they a decision maker with budget authority?
- What is the potential deal size? Is there revenue that justifies investing time?
- What sales intelligence has been gathered?

### Step 3: Sales has an initial conversation
A short call or exchange to understand what the client is looking for. No promises, no commitments about what BayOne will present or deliver. Just listening.

### Step 4: Sales brings context to Colin
Notes, details, the problem statement, the client's areas of interest. This is where Colin can assess whether there's a real fit and start adding value.

### Step 5: Colin engages
Discovery, scoping, solution design, proposals. This is where Colin belongs in the process.

---

## Colin's AI Sales Philosophy

- Lead with "what can we do for you" not "here are our capabilities"
- The conversation should start with understanding the client's actual problem
- Never position BayOne as having capabilities it doesn't have
- Never fake demos, products, or accelerators that don't exist
- BayOne is early in building out its AI practice and should own that honestly rather than pretend otherwise
- Trust-based, problem-first engagements have had close to 100% success rate
- Generic capability pitches where BayOne talks more than the client have had close to 0% success rate
- Other client engagements (Cisco, Sephora, etc.) can be referenced as examples, but they are in areas like CICD, DevOps, code modernization, and enterprise platforms. They do not map to every industry or use case
- A "4-week structured pilot" or any scoping recommendation should never come from sales without Colin's input. Sales does not scope solutions

## Recurring Pain Points and Patterns

### Pattern 1: "Come present our AI capabilities"

**What happens:** Sales gets a meeting and asks Colin to join and present BayOne's AI capabilities, expertise, or portfolio to generate interest. No problem statement, no confirmed client need, sometimes not even a company name.

**Why it's a problem:** This is an introductory sales pitch, not a solutions conversation. It's the sales team's job to generate interest and qualify leads. Colin's job starts after there's something real on the table.

**How Colin has handled it:**
> "Happy to join if it's more of a scoping and discovery session as opposed to us presenting capabilities."

> "That's where I add the most value, scoping real opportunities and figuring out how we can help. If that's still the focus, I'm there."

> "If this has shifted to an introductory pitch where I'm presenting broad AI capabilities to generate interest, that's a sales conversation, not a solutions conversation."

> "I just can't be the first touch point on something like this. By the time I'm in the room, there should already be a reason for me to be there."

---

### Pattern 2: Scope changes and moving goalposts

**What happens:** A meeting or engagement is agreed upon with a specific angle (internal initiatives, known work streams, scoping call). Then the ask shifts to something completely different (present on AI in retail, bring a POV on ecommerce trends, share expertise in an unrelated domain). Colin is expected to prep for the new scope without being consulted.

**Why it's a problem:** Colin agreed to prep for one thing. Sales changed the meeting without telling him and now expects different deliverables. The original angle had real substance. The new angle is vague and disconnected from BayOne's actual work.

**How Colin has handled it:**
> "I do want to make sure we're aligned here because this feels like a slightly different conversation than what we had discussed with [CEO/leadership]."

> "You had also mentioned this was a scoping call where they tell us what they want to do and we ask questions. I'm totally good with that. But this latest message sounds like a third thing."

> "That's not what we discussed with [CEO], and it's not the scoping call you described either."

---

### Pattern 3: Promising things that don't exist

**What happens:** A salesperson or delivery leader tells a client that BayOne has AI accelerators, chatbot demos, agent-based products, or standalone AI products. Colin is then asked to produce or present these things. They don't exist.

**Why it's a problem:** Someone made commitments about Colin's team's capabilities without consulting him. Colin has already told them these things don't exist. Now the client has expectations that can't be met, and Colin is expected to clean it up.

**How Colin has handled it:**
> "Amit had brought this up to me previously, and I let him know at that time that we don't have pre-built AI accelerators, chatbot demos, or agent-based products to show."

> "I never saw or provided whatever deck was sent either which worries me. So I honestly can't speak to what was promised, and I won't be able to own any commitments that were made without my input."

> "If something was communicated to [client] that we can't back up, that needs to be walked back by whoever sent it."

> "I think [person] may have confused case studies from real work that I have done at my prior org with demo-able work that BayOne can realistically claim."

---

### Pattern 4: AI-generated "opportunities" with no human thought

**What happens:** A salesperson finds a LinkedIn post, runs it through ChatGPT, and generates a pitch, pilot framework, and discovery questions. This is packaged up as a real opportunity and Colin is asked to get on a call about it. No one has actually talked to the prospect. No one has verified interest, budget, authority, or anything.

**Why it's a problem:** There is no opportunity. There is no human in the loop. A LinkedIn post is not a lead. Running it through AI doesn't make it one. Colin is being asked to invest time in something that has had zero qualification.

**How Colin has handled it:**
> "Right now we have AI writing a post, us using AI to come up with a response to that post, and then me being asked to get on a call about it. At some point a human needs to be in the loop here."

> "We can't reverse-engineer an engagement out of an AI-generated LinkedIn post, and a '4-week structured pilot' with pre-written discovery questions sounds great on paper but it's not built on anything real."

> "A real opportunity needs a problem statement, a decision maker, and some indication that there's business to be won that's worth pursuing."

> "If I'm identifying leads, qualifying opportunities, joining discovery calls, and then also doing the solutioning and delivery, we need to think about what the sales process looks like without me in every step of it. I'm happy to be the closer, but I can't also be the opener."

---

### Pattern 5: Committing Colin's time and deliverables without asking

**What happens:** Sales tells a client what Colin will present, what he'll bring, or what expertise he'll share without consulting him first. Colin finds out after the fact that he's expected to deliver something he never agreed to.

**Why it's a problem:** Colin's time and his team's work are being volunteered by people who didn't check with him. He ends up either scrambling to deliver something he didn't agree to, or being the one to push back and look difficult. The salesperson faces no consequences because Colin absorbs the fallout.

**How Colin has handled it:**
> "I'd appreciate being looped in before we commit to what I'll be presenting or sharing."

> "I can't own talking points or be expected to make slides that were offered up without my input. If we're going to tell a client I'm coming in to speak to something, I need to be part of that decision."

## Tone Guidelines

- Warm and kind to individual salespeople who are just doing what their boss told them. They're not usually the problem.
- Firm and clear about boundaries, process, and what Colin will and won't do.
- Direct with people who are creating the pattern (leadership, VPs, senior salespeople).
- Humor is welcome when it makes a point without being cruel. ("AI writing a post, AI writing a response, me getting on a call about it" landed perfectly.)
- Never fake enthusiasm about an opportunity that isn't real.
- Never diminish Colin's capabilities. The issue is never that he can't do something. It's that it's not his job to do it, or that no one did the prerequisite work.
- Avoid corporate HR speak like "I want to flag" or "I want to be mindful." Just say it plainly.
- Avoid em dashes and colons in the actual messages (Colin's preference).
- Don't say "not something I'm set up to lead" or anything that implies inability. Say "not what I agreed to" or "not my role in this process."

## Key Phrases That Work

These are battle-tested lines from real exchanges that landed well.

**Drawing the role line:**
- "I'm happy to be the closer, but I can't also be the opener."
- "By the time I'm in the room, there should already be a reason for me to be there."
- "That's the sales team's responsibility, and it needs to happen before I enter the picture."

**Calling out scope drift:**
- "This feels like a slightly different conversation than what we had discussed with [person]."
- "This latest message sounds like a third thing."
- "I'm not sure how we got from there to here."

**Refusing to fake it:**
- "I don't think we do ourselves any favors going in without something real to talk about."
- "We are early in our AI practice and I'd rather own that honestly than fake capabilities."
- "I can't own deliverables or talking points that were offered up without my input."

**Redirecting to what works:**
- "Literally ~100% success rate when we keep it open."
- "Close to 0% success rate when we talk more than they do or try to assume their problem."
- "Shift from 'here are our capabilities' to the much better 'what can we do for you.'"

**Keeping it human:**
- "At some point a human needs to be in the loop here."
- "I can't keep adjusting to a moving target on short notice."
- "If that conversation happens and something real comes out of it, I'm all over it."
