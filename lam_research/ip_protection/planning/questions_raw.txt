● Recorded. Item 3: The Parallel Reconciliation Algorithm.

  This is one of the more interesting architectural decisions they made. Instead of running the three models sequentially (where
  each one filters further), they ran all three in parallel against the same input and then used a reconciliation algorithm to
  compare results and make the final determination. The false positive rate went from 21% with individual models to 17% with the
  reconciliation.

  On the call, Colin identified this as a "modified take on mixture of experts," which was the hot architecture about 18 months ago
  when Mistral MOE was getting attention. The fundamental problem Colin called out: this approach treats all three models as having
  an equal chance of being correct. If they are not equally capable or equally suited to the task (which they are not, since they
  are three completely different types of models), then the voting is biased. Two models agreeing does not mean the answer is right.
   It means two models that do not understand the problem happened to agree.

  The 21% to 17% reduction is also telling. That is a marginal improvement (4 percentage points) for the complexity of running three
   models and building a reconciliation layer. If the parallel approach was working, you would expect a much larger improvement from
   combining three independent signals. The fact that it barely moved suggests the models were making similar errors because they
  share the same fundamental limitation: none of them know what Lam's sensitive entities actually are.

  Compare this to the linear funnel Colin proposed, where each layer has a specific job. The deterministic layer handles what it can
   handle (known patterns, zero false positives). Only the things it cannot resolve get passed to the next layer. Each subsequent
  layer deals with a smaller, harder set of problems. The cost and complexity scale with the actual difficulty, not with the total
  volume.

  The parallel approach is "ask three people who do not know the answer and hope two of them agree." The linear approach is "ask the
   cheapest expert first, and only escalate to the expensive expert when the cheap one cannot answer."

  What is your take on how they designed the reconciliation and why it did not move the needle?

  three-tier labeling framework presented in the meeting (Tier 1: word level, Tier 2: word + document, Tier 3: word + document +

      217  ## Items from the Architecture Discussion

● Recorded. Moving to the architecture discussion items. Item 15: Linear Funnel vs. Parallel.

  This was the core architectural contrast presented in the meeting. Lam's prior approach ran three models in parallel and
  reconciled results. The proposed approach runs detection through a sequential funnel: deterministic first, then ML/NLP, then Gen
  AI. Each layer handles what it can and passes unresolved items to the next.

  The conceptual difference is fundamental. The parallel approach treats all models as peers and asks them to vote. The linear
  approach treats each layer as a specialist with a defined role, and each subsequent layer only sees the problems that the previous
   layer could not resolve.

  The practical implications are significant:

  - Cost: In the parallel approach, every piece of content runs through every model. In the linear approach, the deterministic layer
   (essentially free in compute terms) resolves the majority of cases. Only a fraction reaches the ML layer, and an even smaller
  fraction reaches the Gen AI layer (which is the most expensive per call). Total compute cost drops dramatically.
  - Accuracy: Each layer operates on a different class of problem. The deterministic layer handles known patterns with zero false
  positives. The ML layer handles pattern-adjacent cases. The Gen AI layer handles truly contextual ambiguity. Each layer is
  optimized for its specific problem class rather than being a generalist asked to handle everything.
  - Debuggability: When something goes wrong in a parallel system, it is unclear which model caused the error and why the
  reconciliation chose that result. In a linear system, you can trace exactly which layer made the determination and why, which
  makes error correction targeted rather than systemic.
  - Scalability: Adding new entity types or new applications in the linear approach means updating the deterministic layer's
  dictionaries first, which is a configuration change, not a model retraining exercise. New ML or Gen AI capability is only needed
  if the new entities introduce genuinely new contextual ambiguity.

  Colin explicitly called out the parallel approach as a modified mixture of experts on the call, noting that MOE was the hot
  architecture 18 months ago (Mistral MOE). The framing was diplomatic but the point was clear: the approach was trendy, not
  appropriate.

  demonstrate that creating ground truth does not require the 1,000-hour all-or-nothing exercise the team had been told was
  necessary:

  - Tier 1: The flagged word or entity on its own. Binary: is this a sensitive entity or not? No document context, no human
  explanation. The existing thumbs up/thumbs down data is Tier 1.
  - Tier 2: The flagged word plus the document or text field it appeared in. The model gets context (this word appeared in this
  document) but no human reasoning about why it was flagged. Significantly more useful than Tier 1 because context enables the model
   to learn patterns about when and where sensitive entities appear.
  - Tier 3: The flagged word, plus the document, plus a human explanation of why it is or is not sensitive. The richest training
  signal but the most expensive to produce. This is what the team was told they needed for the entire data set, and it is what
  caused the 1,000-hour estimate.

  Mikhail's immediate response was revealing: "We'd be definitely Tier 1 first." He understood where the team currently sits (they
  have Tier 1 data from the thumbs up/down UI) and implicitly accepted that the path forward is incremental rather than requiring a
  massive upfront investment. Colin then clarified that you start at Tier 2 and subset from there to get Tier 3 for the hardest
  cases, not the other way around.

  The framework accomplished two things simultaneously. It broke the mental barrier of the 1,000-hour estimate by showing that
  useful training data can be produced at a fraction of that cost. And it gave the team a vocabulary for discussing data quality
  that is more nuanced than "we need labeling" versus "labeling is too expensive."

      287  ### 20. EDA to Determine Sample Sufficiency

● Recorded. Last item. Item 20: EDA to Determine Sample Sufficiency.

  When Brad asked the direct question of how many labeled examples they would need ("a thousand five fields, so like five thousand
  total fields, or we talk in tens of thousands?"), Colin introduced Exploratory Data Analysis as the method to answer that question
   with precision rather than guesswork.

  The core point was that sample sufficiency is not a fixed number. It depends on data separability, the degree of similarity
  between the classes. Colin used the giraffe/car analogy: if you are distinguishing two things that look nothing alike, you need
  very few examples. If you are distinguishing two things that look almost identical (like the front and back of a quarter), you
  need many more. For Lam's five text fields, which are all problem descriptions written in similar style and language, the content
  within classes is likely highly similar. Colin cited 20,000 samples as a general benchmark for highly similar data but immediately
   qualified it as not a meaningful number without understanding the actual data distribution.

  EDA answers this definitively before any labeling commitment is made. By examining the existing data (including the ~1,000 thumbs
  up/down examples), EDA measures how similar the data is within and between classes, whether the existing labels are consistent,
  and what the minimum sample size is for statistically meaningful model training. This means the team does not have to guess at
  labeling effort or accept hand-waved estimates. They get a data-driven answer.

  This was well received in the room. Brad's question was specific and practical (how many?), and the answer was honest (it depends,
   but here is exactly how we find out). It avoided the trap of either committing to a number that might be wrong or deflecting the
  question entirely.
