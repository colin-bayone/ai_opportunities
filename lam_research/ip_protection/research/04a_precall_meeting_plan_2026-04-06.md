## Meeting Structure: Agreed Format

The team converged on a format for the 1.5-hour Lam session during this brief day-of sync. The agreed structure is:

### Colin Leads Questions, Pratik Backs Up In-Person

Colin will drive the meeting remotely, leading the questioning. Pratik (who is in person at Lam's office along with Anuj and Amit) will serve as the in-room enforcer -- catching dropped threads and redirecting when Lam's attendees go vague.

Pratik committed explicitly: **"I'll let you lead it largely and then I'll continue to be like backing you in whenever I see if it's going anywhere."**

Colin described what worked well last time and requested a repeat: "Whenever I asked a question and didn't really get a good answer, maybe it got forgotten or you know, maybe it wasn't phrased right on my end. You you jumped in and you know, were kind of the second person there and that was perfect. If we do that again, I will be totally happy."

The BayOne in-person team (Anuj, Amit, Pratik) have a shared directive to keep Lam attendees specific and prevent the meeting from drifting into generalities. Anuj stated: **"The biggest help that we can provide Colin is to keep them on track one and not let them be very generic and all over the place. So you know, 'why don't you show us? Why don't you tell us?' Those kind of things. We have to catch hold of them and say, this is what it is that Colin needs. Otherwise, how do we come back and help you?"**

### Icebreaker Slides, Then Questions -- Not a Presentation

The team explicitly agreed this is not a presentation. It is a discovery conversation preceded by 1-2 slides to set context.

Colin: **"These are like icebreaker slides. It's not like we're presenting to you for an hour and a half. We're using these to kind of set the stage and then you guys are really the talkers today. We're the askers and listeners."**

Pratik aligned on the same structure: "We'll start with two [slides] so they know what the goal post is today and we'll steer it in that."

The icebreaker content would cover:
- Recap of what was discussed in the prior meeting
- What BayOne is looking for today (the outcomes sought from this session)

After the slides, the meeting shifts to pure questions and extraction.

## Colin's Worst-Case Scenario

Colin identified one outcome he considers a non-starter: **"The worst case for me is the POC. They come back and say 'oh just do a POC where you can detect stuff and we'll feed it documents and see if it works.' That's a non-starter, non-project."**

He described this as the "intern project" outcome -- a generic pass/fail test with no business context, no specific application, and no defined success criteria. This is what he called "the worst possible outcome for today."

The antidote is getting Lam to identify a specific business case: "They need to give us kind of a specific business case here from the business side, you know, that's going to matter to them and it can be the same one that they tried before too."

## What They Need From Lam

The team agreed on what must be extracted from Lam during the session. Colin articulated the core requirement across multiple exchanges:

**A specific application:** "What application are you doing? What does it do? Tell me about it. Especially if you're an engineering lead, tell me what your application does. Tell me what audience it has. Tell me what data it uses. It doesn't have to be super in the weeds like code level lines, but give me the picture of what this does so that I can understand enough that we can take some action."

Colin has asked for this in prior meetings and been deflected. Mikhail previously said "that's too technical" when asked to name an application. Colin's frustration: "Even the diagram they drew on the board. Oh, knowledge enablement. Yeah, but like, what is it doing? What knowledge are you enabling? What is the purpose of that app? What apps do you have? They just said we have a bunch. I can't do anything with that."

The two paths forward, as Colin framed them:
1. **The catalog approach:** Lam gives BayOne an inventory of what they are doing so BayOne can assess and recommend an entry point. This is the "consulting partner" path they have asked for, but it requires Lam to open the gates.
2. **The single application approach:** Lam picks one high-impact application where they want to start -- even the one Accenture previously attempted -- and that becomes the POC scope.

Colin: "It has to be one of the two. It has to be either give us the catalog of what you guys are doing and we can take a look and help you make sense of it, or give us a specific case where you're going to decide that this is impactful on your end that we can use if that catalog is too overwhelming."

## Pricing Approach: Agreed Strategy for Today

### No Numbers Today

The team agreed unanimously: no pricing figures during this meeting. The rationale is that scope has not been defined, and quoting without scope is a known mistake.

Colin confirmed: "Avoid though saying like a flat number for a POC until the POC is well defined."

Amit agreed: "Until we have the scope carved out, we can talk about that. This will be a paid POC, but in terms of numbers, we don't have to give it today."

Anuj: "We don't have the clarity on what they really want and like the business objective is not defined. So it's gonna be difficult to sort of lay out a pricing right there and then. We will have to come back and do resource planning and scoping it out and all of that."

### Paid POC Confirmed

The team reaffirmed that whatever POC emerges will be paid, not free. Amit stated this explicitly: "This will be a paid POC." This is consistent with the decision documented in Set 04 (04_internal_prep_pricing_strategy_2026-04-01.md).

### "What Is This Worth to You?"

Colin proposed the one pricing-adjacent question to ask during the meeting -- not to get a number, but to start Lam thinking about value: **"What is this worth to you? And it doesn't have to be the POC itself because POC will happen at some cost. But what is the actual business value to you?"**

He predicted the response and explained why even an empty answer is useful: "Guarantee they're going to come back with, 'I don't know' or 'you guys give us a number and we'll let you know.' If they come back with that, that's good because that means that they're not looking at other vendors in a serious way. They haven't already started thinking about this. They don't have a price point."

Related value-probing questions Colin suggested: "What's the budgeted target for a project like this?"

### Sound Responsive, But Hold Numbers

Colin described the right posture: be responsive on pricing without committing to specifics. He modeled the language: "The complexity of this is pending the amount of information and the speed at which we get that from your team. Any scoping that we do is going to be based upon that information. So the more information you share with us, the quicker we can get you a quote, the quicker we can get started."

He framed this as a motivator for Lam to share more: "It's a good motivator for them."

### The Cisco Mistake Reference

Colin explicitly invoked the Cisco CICD engagement as a cautionary example of what not to do: **"Even with Cisco, I will say the biggest mistake that we made... we had no idea. We didn't even do proper discovery. And yet we threw out a number for a budget without even really understanding what the problem was. Which is why now we have to kind of go and do more homework because it wasn't done the right way, just frankly."**

This reinforced the team's discipline: scope first, then quote.

## Pat's Reality Check Idea: The Scale of Data Labeling

Pratik proposed a specific tactic to recalibrate Lam's expectations about the effort involved in data tagging and labeling. In a prior call, Lam had estimated that approximately 1,000 hours of manual tagging would suffice.

Pratik's proposed counter: **"Give them Alphabet and these four examples -- the kind of money they're putting in. We're talking billions here and they're thinking 1,000 hours for tagging. To give a reality check at what you're thinking of. They have a lot of data. They were talking about terabytes. To have the right tagging, they're thinking, 'OK, maybe 1%, six months, it's gonna happen.' They need to realize that's not at all close to what the reality is. They have no idea what the budgets of Anthropic or Open AI or Alphabet or even better -- Scale AI. They have billions flowing just on tagging itself."**

Colin agreed to the approach but described his tactic for delivery as more subtle: "The tactic I try to go with is 'it's easy for me, it's not easy for you.' I'll give them just enough information that they feel like, 'oh, I could try this myself,' and then they're going to try and it's not gonna work."
