## 6. Environment Options: Option A (Lam's) vs Option B (BayOne's)

### 6a. The Core Debate

The environment question arose from Colin's proposal assumption that stated "B1 handles all data in accordance with confidentiality requirements." Amit challenged this:

> "Should it not be that we will be working on in their environment and on their infrastructure? ... Nothing comes out of that environment, right? ... They would want whatever we are doing has to be done in their infrastructure." [00:29:57]

**Amit's position was strong** -- based on his experience with Lam Research, he was nearly certain they would require work on their own environment. He cited the existing relationship dynamics:

> "Knowing them is where I'm coming from. ... We know how their AVD sucks, right. So we've seen that." [00:32:35]

(AVD = Azure Virtual Desktop -- this is the remote access mechanism Lam uses for external contractors.)

**Anuj offered a counterpoint:** "Unless Amit they give dummy data, you know they are absolutely capable to give dummy data." [00:31:18]

**Amit dismissed this:** "No, but then again then the whole purpose is lost, right? For this POC if it's a dummy data, I mean dummy data, we can create our data and then show it. I mean that's not the purpose for this POC, right? They have to give the actual data." [00:31:26]

Amit went further: "For the option one, they would want us to do it for free" [00:37:05] -- meaning if the POC used dummy data and was just a capability demonstration, Lam would not want to pay for it.

### 6b. Anuj's Option A/B Framework

**Anuj proposed structuring it as two options** to keep the proposal moving:

> "My suggestion is if we want to keep the ball moving faster, we can do a Option A & B. Option A is this. Option B is if the data sit with you." [00:33:36]

For Option B (work on BayOne's infrastructure), Anuj suggested including a Lam engineer as a data liaison:

> "If that's the case, then maybe we can tap into one of their engineers sort of coming as part of this for let's say, you know, whatever X amount of hours, you know, every week and them giving us the data and them, you know, because they are already established. So we are not now waiting on our accesses to come in." [00:33:51]

The Lam engineer would "pull up this data, pull up this report, whatever it is" -- serving as a bridge so BayOne does not get blocked waiting for access.

Anuj framed this as also "having them put some skin in the game as well." [00:34:11]

### 6c. Colin's Resolution on Option Ordering

Colin agreed to present both options but **switched the recommended order** based on Amit's input:

> "I'll switch the order of them. So Option A recommended -- I even did that for Cisco page the same -- Option A recommended: we'll do with your stuff provided that we have access. ... And then for Option B, I'll say that this is fine." [00:37:09]

- **Option A (recommended):** Work is done on Lam's environment, with their data, on their infrastructure. The critical protection: "our timer does not start until we have what we need."
- **Option B:** Work is done on BayOne's infrastructure. Colin said he would not explicitly mention dummy data: "I won't say like with dummy data or anything else like that, I'll just leave it be and kind of leave it to the imagination and that way we don't have to walk anything back." [00:37:34]

Colin noted he used the same Option A/B structure for the Cisco engagement.

### 6d. Differential Pricing for the Two Options

**Anuj suggested different pricing** for the two options:

> "You can actually have two different pricings for both because again, you know, if there's anticipated more time to be spent to, you know, navigate their own ecosystem, then so be it and you know we'll have to charge you for that to a certain extent." [00:37:48]

**Amit disagreed** with differential pricing:

> "I have a little different point of view on that, Anuj. ... In terms of different pricing, right, for Option A and Option B. Option A being we do it on their environment, you know, which will maybe charge more, but see our clock doesn't start until the time everything is ready. ... So we are not starting our efforts, you know, I mean the real efforts until the time everything is in place, right? It is the -- so I mean -- and after that the effort should be the same, right?" [00:38:03]

Amit's logic: since the three-week clock does not start until access is granted, the actual work effort is the same regardless of environment. Different pricing is not justifiable.

**Anuj countered** with a resource retention argument:

> "In a real POC scenario if I'm doing it and I am technically going to be hiring a resource for this particular project or allocating some time on this project, right? I need some cost to retain to manage that resource, whether it's in India or whatever it is. So there is going to be some difference. It might not be too much, it might only be, let's say 1000, 1500, but there's going to be a difference. Because of the slowness of that process that now I have to hold on to these resources." [00:39:10]

**Amit's rebuttal:** "I think we should cover that as part of the, you know, once it starts, right, because COEs are for that only, right, that you have resources who you are able to leverage, you know, on different aspects and products." [00:39:38]

(Note: The transcript renders "Anil" at [00:39:38] but this is Amit addressing Anuj -- a speech-to-text misattribution.)

### 6e. Colin's Resolution: No Differential Pricing, Protected by Existing Mechanisms

**Colin resolved the disagreement** by pointing to two existing protections already in the proposal:

1. **The 25% risk reserve:** "So that protects us 25% timing. If time exceeds, we're safe. If it's less, hey, we'd make more money. It's fine." [00:40:38]

2. **The change request clause:** "This is for the step up from things that are outside of that 25%. If it's an over 25% scope change, there has to be a change request." [00:40:53]

Colin framed this as both Anuj and Amit being right: "Good news is you're both right." The risk reserve absorbs minor overruns from navigating Lam's environment, and the change request clause protects against major scope changes.

**Resolution:** Same pricing for both options. The 25% risk reserve and change request clause provide sufficient protection without needing to charge more for Option A.

## 7. Three-Week Timeline Justification (Pricing Context)

Colin explained the three-week POC duration, which is a change from earlier discussions:

- **Week 1:** Discovery and data assessment. Colin described this as "our way to get out of jail free. We haven't done any real work in that first week." The purpose is to validate that Lam's data is usable: "We have no clue. Even if they say that they have that, as you said, like the gold samples, we have no idea if it's actually accurate. If that's junk, then everything downstream is junk." [00:15:10]
- **Weeks 2 and 3:** Actual build -- layered detection implementation and accuracy benchmarking.

Colin noted: "Probably is not going to take us this long. That's why I'm giving us this as a buffer just to be safe." [00:22:25]

This structure supports the pricing because the 40/60 split in the client-facing breakdown ($4,000 discovery / $6,000 build at the $10K level) maps to week 1 vs weeks 2-3.

## 8. Protective Pricing Mechanisms in the Proposal

Colin highlighted several mechanisms already built into the proposal that protect BayOne's pricing position:

1. **Timeline does not start until access is provided:** "Timeline begins upon receipt of the required data and access to everything." [00:23:55] Reiterated multiple times: "Basically we don't lift a finger to do actual work until that access is provided." [00:42:25]

2. **25% risk reserve:** Built into the costing as a buffer for timing and scope variance.

3. **Change request clause:** For scope changes exceeding 25%, a formal change request is required. Colin: "Calling that out early is a good way to stay safe. Because that happens every single AI project, it's like while you're there, why don't you go do this too?" [00:40:53]

4. **Feedback timeline protection:** "Lam Research provides timely feedback and validation. Timeline extends if response time exceeded agreed window." Colin noted he would strengthen this to require a change request for extreme timeline shifts. [00:41:52]

5. **Strategic document length:** Colin deliberately made the detailed proposal long: "I am fully aware that this is too long and that's part of the strategy. ... It doesn't matter if it's too long and no one reads it. It's still sent. It was still agreed upon. So it's like terms and conditions that you didn't scroll to the bottom of." [00:42:43]

Anuj validated this approach: "They're used to working with larger SIs who just dump a ton of data on them. They love it. So yeah, I don't think this is gonna be a problem." [00:43:02]
