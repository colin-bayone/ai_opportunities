## What We Got Wrong

### 1. We priced Option A and Option B the same, signaling equivalence
In the engagement pricing one-pager, both options are $15,000 and Option A is flagged "Recommended." The pricing signal says "equal from our side, pick either." Mikhail's email reveals Lam does not see them as equal — Option A is preferred, not merely acceptable. This is fine commercially (we get $15K either way), but the Set 08 negotiation between Anuj and Amit was oriented around whether to charge more for Option A; Amit's position (same price, risk reserve + change request protects us) won. In retrospect, Anuj's instinct that Lam would self-select to A is vindicated, and the price sensitivity debate was somewhat academic — Lam was always going to choose A.

### 2. We under-emphasized "data stays in Lam systems" as a selling point
The engagement pricing one-pager describes Option A data handling as "Data remains within Lam systems at all times" — accurate, but as one row in a comparison table, not as a highlighted positioning. Given how strong Lam's preference is, leading with this theme might have shortened the review cycle further. Future deliverables for this client should frame data-residency as the default, not as an option.

### 3. Implicit assumption that the SOW would be BayOne's form, not Lam's
The session handoff line "SOW/legal ~one week" did not specify whose form. The SOW arrived on the BayOne MSA template — which is Lam's contractor standard for engagements under the 2018 agreement. This is a BayOne-letterhead SOW, filled out under Lam's MSA. BayOne is the Contractor; Lam is the Company. This was a reasonable guess on a coin flip; it landed on Lam's side (their procurement-standard form, not a BayOne-drafted bespoke SOW).

## What We Got Right

### 1. Pricing both execution options at the same dollar amount
This removed Lam's procurement-easy reason to push for Option B. With equal pricing, Lam chose Option A on merits (data residency, confidentiality practice, Amit's expected behavior). BayOne still books $15K.

Set 08a's analysis showed $15K was the only price point that gave any negotiation room while clearing the loaded-margin floor. Lam did not negotiate. Had we anchored at $10K (Set 07 number), we would have priced in 30 days of work for a below-floor margin.

The SOW Term field on page 2 of the template is an empty date range for BayOne to fill. Because our proposal language does not commit to a start date, we can populate the SOW Term with a range that starts after tech access is confirmed — preserving the Set 04 access-gating protection.

Set 08 decision: Brad and Mikhail only. Mikhail was the one who proactively responded and is coordinating the tech access. Daniel is now looped in at the right point (tech access, his area) rather than the wrong point (commercial review, where he would have churned).

### 5. The decision NOT to over-commit on a fixed calendar start date
Session handoff flagged this as a protection. The SOW arriving without a hard start date expected from BayOne validates it. We can populate SOW Term as "Execution: upon confirmation of environment and data access" or as a forward-dated range.

No pushback from any Lam stakeholder. Mikhail's go-signal email treats the technical approach as settled and moves to logistics.
