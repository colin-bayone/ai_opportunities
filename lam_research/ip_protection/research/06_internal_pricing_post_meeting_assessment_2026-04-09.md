Colin's evaluation of Daniel Harrison was blunt and unsparing. When discussing what the call revealed, Colin said: "That engineering guy on the call, wow, that was bad." He continued: "I've worked with engineers my entire career and that is, you know, C grade person at best. I don't care how many years of experience someone has, that's just bad."

He elaborated on the quality of the prior work: "They were doing it in a very, you know, infantile way. It's not even like tutorial level stuff. It was kind of just like summer intern type work. And the fact that it's been 18 months is also pretty telling."

### Specific Behavioral Issues on the Call

Colin identified two specific problems with Daniel's conduct during the April 6 meeting:

1. **Passivity in a technical role.** Colin: "Even on the call, he led with, you know, I'm just here to observe, and I'm like, what? What engineering lead gets on a technical call and says I'm just here to observe?"

2. **The SLM / air-gapped tangent.** Colin: "He was going off on this weird tangent about on-prem. Didn't understand the concept of air-gapped. So that tells you all you need to know right there. He was going down this rabbit hole of small language models." Colin's assessment is that Daniel was using terminology (air-gapped, small language models) without understanding the underlying concepts, specifically the distinction between deployment topology and model architecture.

Colin used a term to characterize Daniel that he distinguished from "detractor": "He's someone who... Anand should have given us some words for people that aren't necessarily negative, but are... he's a distractor." The distinction matters. Daniel is not actively opposing BayOne. He is absorbing meeting time with uninformed tangents.

### Pat's Additional Color on Daniel

Pratik Sharda (Pat) provided a more nuanced and personal assessment. He agreed with Colin's technical evaluation but reframed the root cause from incompetence to motivation: "I think his challenge is not, he's not technical enough. His challenge is he's not motivated at all."

Pat's characterization of Daniel's work style: "He's like a lot of young kids. He's very into like family, taking time off, a lot of time remote. He's never present in the office and that kind of... However, Pat then balanced this with a positive: "Having said this, the guy is very good, very supportive, and I think our... he definitely is not a detractor. He's neutral and towards us, so definitely can be a coach. Eventually we can nurture him to a coach."

**The combined assessment:** Daniel is technically underwhelming, unmotivated, and a distractor on calls, but he is personally amenable and not hostile to BayOne. He will not block the engagement. He can be managed as a neutral stakeholder who might eventually be brought on board as a coach through nurturing, but he should not be relied upon as a technical counterpart.

## Challenge 1: Scope Is Even Less Clear Than Before

### Three Different Views of the Problem

Colin identified that the three primary Lam stakeholders each have a different conception of the scope:

1. **Mikhail's view:** Specifically internal enablement. The problem is about protecting IP within Lam's own internal systems and workflows.

2. **Brad's view:** Both internal and at customer sites or competitor sites. The problem extends beyond Lam's internal environment to the places where Lam deploys equipment and personnel.

3. **Daniel's view:** Focus should be on customer sites. Colin paraphrased Daniel's position and then noted the contradiction: "Meanwhile, Brad and Mikhail are like, what are you talking about? We don't even have this internally. Why are we talking about something that's, you know, 3 levels of maturity later?"

Colin left this alone during the April 6 call: "I left that alone in the call because it's just not the right people on the call. I think Brad is the right person to be honest with you." The implication is that scope alignment requires a conversation with Brad, who has the organizational authority to resolve the disagreement.

### The POC Scope Is Clear; the Full Engagement Scope Is Not

Colin drew a sharp distinction: "The scope for a POC is clear. The overall scope is even less clear than it was before." He can proceed with the POC because it addresses the immediate problem (specific fields, specific entity types, specific application). But the full-scale engagement cannot be scoped until Lam internally resolves what the initiative is actually trying to accomplish.

## Challenge 2: No Business Value or Financial Justification

### Lam Has Never Articulated Why This Matters Financially

Colin's observation: "If you notice, they never really talk about business value. They keep on saying hours saved and this is really important, but it sounds like even internally they've never really had any financial justification to do this in the first place."

This is a costing challenge for BayOne. Without a stated business value or ROI framework from Lam, there is no anchor for pricing a full engagement. Colin identified this as a dual-edged condition: "Which makes our life a little bit tough, but that also means we can over-quote and be safe." If Lam cannot articulate what the initiative is worth to them, they also cannot credibly argue that BayOne's price is disproportionate.

Pat agreed with both challenges and restated them as "the technical aspect and the economic aspect." Neither Lam's internal capabilities nor their economic rationale has been established.

### The 18-Month Question

Colin raised a specific question he would want to put to Lam: "My question to them would be when did you shut off AI access for these things?" This refers to whether Lam has restricted generative AI tool access as part of their IP protection posture, and if so, when. The answer would help quantify the business impact, but Lam has not volunteered this information. Colin noted: "There's still not much clarity there. It still sounds like they're just trying to do this ad hoc as time goes."

Colin reframed the scope confusion as an indicator of opportunity size: "The positive signal that came out of that is that it was so disorganized that you could see that there's big opportunity because they don't even know what they don't know."

The logic: if Lam had a tight, well-defined scope, the engagement would be small and bounded. The fact that three senior stakeholders cannot agree on the problem definition means the problem is larger than any of them individually perceive, and BayOne can help define that scope as part of a broader engagement. The disorganization is evidence of unmet need, not organizational dysfunction that would prevent engagement.

## The T-Mode Question Revisited

### Is Lam in Existential Urgency or Just Proceeding Ad Hoc?

Colin had previously assessed Lam as being in "T-mode" (a framework term indicating the client is in a time-pressured, must-solve situation). In this call, he expressed uncertainty about whether that assessment still holds.

Colin: "That's the problem. Is, are they really in T-mode or are they, you know, kind of, they know they have something, they want to do it. They've definitely invested some substantial time with us. So it kind of tells me that they're serious, but I also don't know if they have a final vision for everything."

The evidence for urgency: Lam has invested multiple meetings, brought senior stakeholders (Brad as Managing Director), and engaged BayOne's technical team over multiple weeks. The evidence against urgency: 18 months of stalled work, no financial justification, no internally aligned scope, and a pattern of proceeding without clear direction.

Colin's conditional conclusion: "They're saying let's solve this immediate problem at hand. And I'm like, of course we're going to be able to do that with the POC." The POC can proceed regardless of whether Lam is in T-mode, but the full engagement trajectory depends on whether Lam has organizational urgency or is merely drifting toward a solution.

Colin articulated the POC's strategic value in terms that go beyond the POC deliverable itself. This framing directly informed the pricing discussion. Colin argued that the POC's dollar value is almost irrelevant compared to the access it provides: "Even if margin's 30%, that's 3 grand... the value we get out of those account and access is way greater than that."

## Open Questions and Unresolved Points

1. **When did Lam restrict AI access?** Colin wants to know when Lam shut off generative AI tools as part of their IP protection posture. This would anchor the business value discussion. Lam has not volunteered this.

2. **Who gives database access for the POC?** Colin asked: "Mikhail, is he gonna be the guy that gives me database access?" The organizational path from signed SOW to actual system access is unclear and, based on Cisco precedent (three calendar months), could be the primary timeline risk.

3. **What is Lam's budget tolerance?** The team agreed to "feel out what their budget is" but has no data point yet. Colin framed the POC price ($10,000) partly as a test of purchasing authority: "If you can't drop some money on the POC, then you probably also don't have the power to say here's a $750K SOW for these guys either."

4. **Will Brad engage on scope alignment?** Colin identified Brad as the right person for resolving the three-way scope disagreement, but no mechanism for getting Brad into that conversation has been discussed.

5. **Is the Suresh disagreement resolved?** Colin referenced a disagreement with Suresh (BayOne internal sales leadership) about whether to pursue opportunities where the client may not have budget. Colin's position: don't waste time if the full scope comes in at $50K when there are bigger fish, but do use the foot-in-the-door approach for smaller bites. The Suresh meeting was on the calendar for today but may not happen.

6. **The T-mode determination remains open.** Colin could not definitively assess whether Lam has organizational urgency. The POC will proceed either way, but the full engagement trajectory depends on the answer.
