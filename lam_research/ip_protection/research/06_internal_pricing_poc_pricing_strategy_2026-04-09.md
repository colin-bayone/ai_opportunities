## 1. The $10,000 POC Price Point

### Colin's Stated Number

Colin proposed a specific price point during the call: "Without, you know, putting too much thought into it, I think **10,000 is a good price point** to get at for something like a **three-week engagement** starting from the point that we have access to the needed information." [00:21:09]

Pat (Pratik) immediately endorsed it: "I think I'm in agreement on that." [00:21:21]

### Margin Awareness

Colin acknowledged the margin on the POC itself is modest: "It's nice to say 10,000 for the POC and feel good about that and, you know, have some margin. Even if margin's 30%, that's 3 grand." [00:16:15]

He then explicitly deprioritized the POC margin: "Even if we make 3 grand, it's like big deal. I think that's a nice thing for personal, but not in the scope of the company. But the value we get out of those account and access is way greater than that." [00:16:32]

### The Real Value: Access

Colin's central thesis is that the POC price matters less than what access it unlocks: "The moment they give us access, the moment that they give me access to a code base, access to data, access to their systems. The moment I have a Lam ID, it's game over. It's game over. We're good. It doesn't even matter if the SOW is signed at that point because I'm gonna get as much information out of that that we can." [00:15:35]

He cited precedent: "Same thing with Cisco, same thing with Sephora. You want to see things blow up and get us a trillion opportunities. Let's get that [access]." [00:16:15]

### Pricing as a Goldilocks Problem

Colin articulated the need to price neither too high nor too low: "In a way, even going a little bit low on a POC cost is better because if they're more likely to sign it. The trick is to also not set their expectations too low on pricing. So don't do a POC at cost or for 1000 bucks. Because then they're gonna have an expectation. Oh, the whole project is now 50,000." [00:16:54]

The $10,000 figure is designed to be low enough to clear quickly but high enough to anchor reasonable expectations for full engagement pricing.

## 2. Three-Week Structure: Discovery + POC Build

### The Timeline Breakdown

Colin described the structure as: "One week for quote discovery and be clear that that discovery is, you know, a lasting value that we don't have to do again if we choose to do the full project. And the following two weeks are for the actual build out of the POC." [00:31:54]

Total: **1 week discovery + 2 weeks POC build = 3 weeks.**

### Discovery Week Justification

Colin provided specific reasons why the discovery week has standalone value:

1. **Lasting value / non-repeatable:** "That discovery is a lasting value that we don't have to do again if we choose to do the full project." [00:31:54] -- This positions discovery as something the client gets permanent benefit from regardless of whether the full project proceeds.

2. **Finding who owns what:** "What are we trying to specifically solve for here. You know, they said we have this list of items and things like they were saying on the call. Well, where is that? Who owns that?" [00:31:54]

3. **Finding the final arbiter:** "Who's the final person that says, you know, yes, this passes, no, this fails." [00:31:54]

4. **Getting to know the team:** "So we can kind of get to know their team, set up meetings." [00:31:54]

5. **Not required pre-POC:** "Those are things we can find out in discovery. We don't need to have those established in the pre POC phase, but that is partially also what the time is for." [00:31:54] -- This shifts the burden of clarity from pre-signing to post-signing, making it easier to close.

### Two-Week POC Build Justification

Colin gave two explicit reasons for the two-week build window:

**Reason 1 -- Capacity management:** "Number one, I do not have a team that I can just pull people off to go full force on projects. So one day of effort is not the same as me saying that it'll take the team a day because they're already working on a bunch of things. So that's me managing capacity, making sure we can deliver with some buffer." [00:29:32]

**Reason 2 -- Appearance of complexity:** "If you do it too fast, it looks too easy." [00:29:48]

### Early Delivery Option

Colin built in flexibility: "If it's finished early, we'll deliver early, but at Max two weeks for the POC cycle because that way we can have beautiful polished..." [00:33:04] -- framing early delivery as a bonus rather than a signal that it was overscoped.

That's what really works for POCs, 'cause it's personalized to them." [00:29:58]

Colin positioned the time as an investment in customization: "Here I can customize it to be exactly, exactly resonating with them and even kind of thinking ahead. ## 4. Outcome-Based Pricing: Never Headcount

### The Core Principle

Colin stated the principle twice, emphatically:

"The reason why is that what we did wrong on the first Cisco project is we made it so that it was headcount." [00:20:11]

"The trick here is that there's no headcount in this. It is not billed by resource. If they want to know that, my answer is, you know, no. It's billed by outcome and by deliverable." [00:25:31]

### How It Works as a Negotiation Shield

Colin described how outcome-based pricing protects against discount requests, using the Cisco engagement as a concrete example:

- The Cisco SOW was **$500K**.
- Cisco came back requesting **$300K** (a $200K discount, 40% reduction).
- Colin's response: **"Good, go do it for 300K then, not my problem."** [00:20:28]
- When Cisco asked him to justify the cost ("show us why"), Colin showed them the detailed breakdown and said: **"Here's your levers to pull at Cisco. X things off of this list or extend the timeline. One of the two."** [00:20:44]
- The pricing was structured so that the only way to reduce cost was to remove deliverables or extend timeline -- not reduce headcount.

### Specific Phrasing on Headcount

"If you want it to cost less, have less deliverables. It's that easy. But there's no lever to say we're gonna do this at a lower cost because we're going to do it with three people instead of four. They won't even know that." [00:25:31]

### How Colin Structured the Cisco Pricing Document

Colin screen-shared two documents during the call:

1. **The written breakdown of costing:** "This is the written breakdown of costing. Now this is not the detail view. I gave them this 500K quote and they were like, how in the heck did you arrive at that number." It included "specific deliverables here as to what's happening, what our assumptions were, all that good stuff." [00:19:31]

2. **The detailed line-item breakdown:** "And then they asked for the specific 'how did you arrive there?' Give them one of these." [00:19:59] -- a second, more granular document with specific line items.

Colin proposed creating the same structure for the Lam POC: "We can do this in terms of the POC only. I think that's the smartest thing to do at this stage to say here's the POC. It'll be a lot smaller than this and more digestible." [00:20:11]

## 5. Never Free POC

### Amit's Question and the Immediate Override

Amit (who joined the call late) asked: "Do you think we should do it free of cost?" [00:27:15]

Both Colin and Anuj immediately rejected it:
- Colin: "No, no." [00:27:37]
- Anuj: "No, we should not do that. No." [00:27:37]

### Colin's Reasons Against Free POCs

**Reason 1 -- Sends the wrong message:** "No more free POCs because they sent the wrong message. Like for Cisco, we did that. And to be honest, we completely botched that pricing. We didn't even do any pricing model for that, frankly. We just said a number in a room and they said yes." [00:27:40]

**Reason 2 -- If they won't pay for POC, they won't pay for the engagement:** "If they don't wanna pay for a POC, that means they're not gonna wanna pay for the engagement is my view." [00:28:00]

**Reason 3 -- Tests whether the buyer has purchasing authority (the EBI/TBI test):** "It kind of moves out that EBI point, right? If you can't drop, you know, some money on the POC, then you know you probably also don't have the power to say here's a, you know, a 750K SOW for these guys either." [00:28:19]

**Reason 4 -- BayOne has enough business to be selective:** "We've got business coming out of our **** until that changes, until my team's sitting there waiting for work to do." [00:28:00]

### Anuj's Additional Reasoning

Anuj added that a paid POC signals urgency and priority: "It also shows urgency and, you know, motivation to solve this problem, right? If they're gonna go and take this and try and, you know, get budgeting and this is not prioritized for them, then it's gonna be a very long process." [00:28:34]

Anuj also argued the sticker shock concern is minimal: "For a two day effort, I'm pretty sure it's not gonna be in the, you know, hundreds of thousands of dollars. I think the sticker shock is not gonna be there." [00:28:51]

## 6. Pat's Three Options for What to Share

Pat (Pratik) articulated three possible approaches for pricing transparency before he had to leave the call:

**Option 1 -- Show the calculation methodology:** "Give them a little bit of how we are pricing it, like how that cost is being calculated... in the hope of building credibility to get a bigger pricing." [00:17:18]

**Option 2 -- Give price with large approximate for full scope:** "We can just give them a price point on what it may look like as a whole and it could be a very large approximate." [00:17:18]

**Option 3 -- POC price only, skip the rest:** "We could actually go with the thought of not giving them any at all, just give a very baseline price of the POC and then skip the rest." [00:17:18]

### Where the Team Landed

Colin drove the decision toward **Option 3** (POC price only): "We can do this in terms of the POC only. I think that's the smartest thing to do at this stage." [00:20:11]

His rationale: "The full scope costing we won't be able to do until there is a defined scope that comes from them. But at least for the POC we can do that." [00:20:44]

The consensus was to present:
- A detailed but digestible POC pricing document (deliverables, assumptions, line items)
- No full-scope pricing until scope clarity comes from Lam
- The ability to "feel out what their budget is" through the POC process

## 7. The Contingency: Start Date from Access, Not from Signing

### The Principle

Colin stated: "Whenever we send back that proposal, the proposal will be fairly easy. Make it contingent upon **the start date of when we have tangible access** to those things because there's going to be some churn to get people on boarded. If there's an NDA, if there's an account that needs set up, et cetera. So make that POC start date not rushed." [00:26:07]

He rephrased it again later: "From the point that we have the needed access, that's the key." [00:31:19]

### The Cisco Precedent

Colin cited a specific cautionary example: "Even for like Cisco, Cisco signed a SOW. It took **three entire calendar months** to get the access to things." [00:31:19]

This means the clock on the three-week POC engagement does not start until BayOne has actual working access to Lam's systems/data/accounts. The proposal signing and access provisioning are decoupled.

### Why This Matters for Lam Specifically

Colin noted uncertainty about who would provision access: "Mikhail, is he gonna be the guy that gives me database access? So it's that question." [00:31:38]

This connects to the discovery week justification -- the organizational ambiguity at Lam (who owns what, who has authority) makes access provisioning unpredictable.

## 8. Colin's Internal Reality: Actual Level of Effort

### What the Work Actually Entails

Colin was blunt with his internal team about the technical difficulty:

"I told my team members as well. I was like, don't get frustrated that we have to use AI because frankly the problem statement that they presented us with for a POC I could get done in **one day** without using AI." [00:11:14]

Anuj paraphrased back: "As per Colin, you know, this is not even an AI POC. He could do basically just with Python or whatever rule based, you know, data cleansing to a certain extent and from his standpoint, it's basically **a day's worth of effort**." [00:22:43]

### The Technical Assessment

Colin described the actual technical complexity: "It's just simple pattern matching, even no ML, even no NLP. I mean, I could do that with just a generic Python script. I could do that in the command line if I wanted to." [00:12:42]

Anuj confirmed: "It's more like a rule based or whatever it is in your mind. Or maybe machine learning?" Colin: "Correct." [00:12:39]

### The Explicit Internal-Only Framing

Colin made clear this assessment is internal knowledge only: "It's easy for us, but these are all internal conversations. They don't need to know that. **As far as they know, it's something they haven't been able to fix in 18 months.**" [00:29:04]

Anuj reinforced the framing with humor: "Yeah, it's extremely difficult. Exactly. We're building the moon here, absolutely." [00:29:11]

### The Gap Between Internal Effort and External Pricing

- **Internal reality:** ~1 day of actual technical work, simple pattern matching, no AI/ML/NLP required
- **External positioning:** 3-week engagement ($10,000), framed as a sophisticated AI proof of concept
- **Justification for the gap:** Capacity management, polish/personalization, discovery value, appearance of complexity ("smokescreen"), and the strategic value of access

## 9. POC as Strategic Entry Point

### The "Knock Their Socks Off" Strategy

Colin outlined his POC philosophy: "I think what we do is we knock their socks off with the POC and we say like, this is only what we're going to do in this case. I'll phrase this like it's an actual AI project... I'll say, here's what we're going to do. We're going to establish what your ground truth validity is, and that's your max achievable percentage for this. There's nothing that can be better than that without human refinement." [00:24:06]

### POC Deliverable Framing

Colin planned to frame the POC output with AI project rigor even though the underlying work is simpler: ground truth establishment, max achievable metrics, structured deliverables. The proposal itself would mirror the Cisco structure but smaller: deliverables, assumptions, and line-item pricing.

### Pat's Input on POC Goals

Pat argued for setting conservative expectations: "I don't want to set up their expectation. If it was 30% like missing and like accuracy, we don't need to bring it to 2% or 5%. What is their end game? We should bring it from 30 to 25 and 20 and tell them it's gonna happen in steps. And show it in a very small subset." [00:13:30]

Pat also emphasized the intelligence-gathering value of the POC: "Even if we keep it very low priced and very low goal to just get them excited is to get you the access of the environment of the system, see it in real action... you will discover a lot more of problem statement approach. Once you're in the system, I'm pretty confident you will learn a lot, lot more than what we have an understanding as of now." [00:13:30]

## 10. Full Scope Pricing Context

### Why Full Scope Can't Be Priced Yet

Colin identified the blocker: "From my perspective, there's not enough clarity on scope from them. And it's not just on scope, it's on their specific desired scope. So I could plan it out for them, but until they can say, yep, we're gonna foot the bill for it, we'll be wasting breath." [00:24:06]

He noted contradictory scope visions among Lam stakeholders:
- **Mikhail:** Focus on internal enablement
- **Brad:** Both internal and at customer sites or competitor sites
- **The third person (Daniel):** Focus on customer sites only
- Brad and Mikhail's reaction to the third view: "What are you talking about? We don't even have this internally. Why are we talking about something that's three levels of maturity later?" [00:10:36]

### The Over-Quoting Opportunity

Colin noted that Lam has never established financial justification for the project: "They never really talk about business value. They keep on saying hours saved and this is really important, but it sounds like even internally, they've never really had any financial justification to do this in the first place, which makes our life a little bit tough, but **that also means we can over quote and be safe**." [00:13:19]

### The Floor on Acceptable Deal Size

Colin referenced a disagreement with Suresh about minimum deal value: "If they don't have the money, don't even waste the time. Because at the end of the day, if they're going to give us $50,000, there's bigger fish out there." [00:34:28]

Where they disagreed was on the "foot in the door" concept -- Colin's position: "We can bite off smaller chunks if that's all they have the stomach for. But if they're like, you know, full scope, they want it to be done for 200K and they don't want to define the scope and they don't want this -- my best thing is don't get pushed around." [00:34:43]

### Lam's Weak Negotiating Position

Colin's leverage assessment: "What they've already found out is that they can't do this. If it's been 18 months and they haven't gotten anything done in an intern project, they cannot do this." [00:34:43]

This gives BayOne "standing room" in negotiations.

6. **Pat's request for benchmarking.** Pat wanted to set expectations on accuracy improvement targets (e.g., from 30% to 25% to 20% in steps). No specific benchmarks were established.

7. **NDA and onboarding requirements.** Colin flagged NDA and account setup as potential friction points but no one had clarity on whether these were needed.

8. **Suresh disagreement.** Colin referenced a disagreement with Suresh about minimum deal size and the "foot in the door" concept. The disagreement appears unresolved -- Colin acknowledged "I know who I'm disagreeing with" but maintained his position.

## 12. Summary of Pricing Methodology

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **POC Price** | $10,000 |
| **POC Duration** | 3 weeks (1 week discovery + 2 weeks build) |
| **Actual Effort** | ~1 day of technical work |
| **Pricing Basis** | Outcome/deliverable-based, never headcount |
