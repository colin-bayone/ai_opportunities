## 5. ROI and Business Case Framing

The ROI framing is multi-layered and was articulated by both Brad and Mikhail:

### 5.1 Productivity Gains (Primary)

- **Mikhail:** "Overall, what we're trying to achieve, we're trying to achieve productivity. We're trying to make sure we can bubble up as much information as possible without violating customer IP, without violating our IP as well, so that people can solve problems."
- The current over-restriction model demonstrably limits productivity: "If document three has the path to solution, but you only can look at Intel documents and this sits in a Samsung space, you're not going to see that."
- **Brad:** "We're also knowing that we're limiting the productivity, the capability, because we don't share that much."

### 5.2 Cost Reduction (Secondary, tied to escalation reduction)

- **Brad:** "When you're over here [in escalation], this is very, very costly. When you're in this phase, also, it affects customer trust [and] other things. So the goal is to drive this down and drive this up [self-help]. Even ask questions, so theoretically, you will never get [to escalation]. The goal is to get here [self-help] and drive this down, drive that up, because we want -- again, it's productivity, but it's cost, it's customer cost, it's other things."
- Escalation is expensive in time: "Hours or minutes, days, weeks, months sometimes."

### 5.3 Customer Trust

- **Brad:** Explicitly names "customer trust" as an ROI dimension alongside cost and productivity. The over-restriction model is a trust issue because Samsung trusts Lam that its data is not cross-shared with Intel, and vice versa.
- **Brad:** "Samsung trusts us that it's not cross-shared."

### 5.4 The Feedback Loop as ROI Multiplier

This is a critical and sophisticated business case element. Brad and Mikhail articulate a feedback loop that turns solved escalation tickets into self-help content:

- **Mikhail:** "There is the feedback loop, because we want to actually drive down the usage of these systems by doing what? Once the problem statement and the answer is identified, we feed this always back so next time people come in, they self-help and they don't have to ask the question."
- **The blocker to the feedback loop is the IP problem:** "The problem with that, all of this is unstructured data. So even if the user was identified, it doesn't help us because long term it has to feed into more general population."
- **Brad (paraphrased):** Until they have confidence they can redact properly, they cannot build this feedback loop. They cannot take unstructured data that is restricted, or potentially could be restricted, and feed it into the general training area.
- **Raquel (from BayOne):** Frames the predictive analysis ROI: "That's actually the ROI on the ML, AI, all of that is predictive analysis. Because once the root cause analysis of, let's say, 10, 100,000, the data points come in, then it predicts it in the very first layer itself."

**The feedback loop is the long-term strategic value proposition.** If IP detection/redaction works, Lam can:
1. Open up restricted tickets to general population
2. Feed redacted escalation data back into self-help
3. Drive down escalation volume (cost reduction)
4. Drive up self-help resolution rates (productivity gain)
5. Eventually enable predictive root cause analysis

### 5.5 Quantified Pain Points

- **Mikhail on labeling cost:** "It was over a thousand man-hours just to get the labeling up and then continuous maintenance. So that was fairly expensive activity."
- **Mikhail reframes the cost:** "If we say, yeah, this case is where all the things sit, maybe a thousand hours is just peanuts compared to the productivity gains we could gain by opening this up."
- **Brad on policy violations:** "When we started this project, I'm like, how many tickets is gonna take me to find a policy violation? Seven. Seven tickets, there was already a confidential violation." This demonstrates the scale of the problem: roughly 1 in 7 tickets contains a policy violation.
- **Previous model performance:** The ML models they trained achieved roughly 80% accuracy (20% false positive rate per ticket). Fine-tuning improved it from 20% to 17% false positives. Brad needs this "way below 1% end state."

## 7. Scope and Priorities

### 7.1 Two Distinct Business Cases (Swim Lanes)

Mikhail presented two separate business cases that Brad explicitly wants kept separate:

**Swim Lane 1: Search/Self-Help (Redaction Focus)**
- Use case: Field service technicians (and others) searching knowledge bases for troubleshooting information
- Problem: Over-restriction means relevant documents are hidden because they might contain customer IP
- Solution needed: Detection and redaction of customer-identifiable information so documents can be surfaced to broader audiences
- Performance requirement: Can run heavier, "an hour" is acceptable
- Accuracy focus: Over-redaction is tolerable (better to redact too much than too little); false positives less critical
- Current state: Six different search tools, fragmented data sources, no unified data lake

**Swim Lane 2: Escalation Entry Point (Detection Focus)**
- Use case: Users entering problem statements into escalation/ticketing systems
- Problem: Users include customer-identifiable information in free-text problem descriptions (1 in 7 tickets)
- Solution needed: Real-time detection and notification that the user may be violating confidentiality policy
- Performance requirement: Must run within "UI standards, two to five seconds max"
- Accuracy focus: False positive sensitivity is "significantly more critical." If every fifth notification is a false positive, users will stop trusting the system.
- Current approach: Notify, don't block. "Let's start not enforcing the policy, but let's notify you [that you] potentially could be violating. Still leave it up to the users to say, yes, I am. No, I'm not."

**Brad on keeping them separate:** "What I've presented today is two separate business use cases. When we say small piece and MVP, I would expect them not being jumbled together, but they could be separate swim lanes."

### 7.2 Starting Point: Two Data Fields

- **Brad:** "We tried to prove out in our initial [attempt] was just really about two attributes or two data fields."
- **Mikhail clarifies:** "Let's say customer name, file name. Sometimes those are just enough."
- The MVP is about proving the concept on the simplest possible case (customer name identification/redaction) before expanding scope.

### 7.3 Unstructured Data is the Primary Challenge

- **Mikhail:** "Majority of the data that we're concerned with today for this discussion is unstructured data. So it lives inside documents. It could live inside meeting transcripts. It could live in the procedures or a problem [description]."
- There is some metadata in some systems, but it is not comprehensive or consistent.
- The unstructured nature is what makes the problem hard: "Some people put Micro 11, some put F11, some put FAB space 11, FAB dash 11."

### 7.4 Cloud-First Direction

- **Mikhail:** "We do want to focus on cloud first going forward. So that's our plan."
- **Mikhail:** "We also want to focus on microservice architecture long term. Whether we do or not, let's say these solutions, that's a different thing, but that's our kind of aspirational goal."
- Current state is extremely heterogeneous: "Some of it on-prem, some of it is on cloud, some of it is eventually gonna move to cloud, some of it is LamGPT."

## 8. What Has Been Tried and Why It Failed

### 8.1 Machine Learning Models (Not Generative AI)

- **Mikhail:** "Some of the things we've actually tested was more with machine learning rather than generative AI. We actually have not used generative AI to prove any use cases."
- **Reason for choosing ML over GenAI:** "Because of unstructured output. If it does mean we can't, all I'm saying is we decided to go with something more definitive that can tell us redact, don't redact, rather than [the un]structured output that LLMs typically produce."
- **Specific models tried:** Transformers model, SpaCy, and an Azure AI model.
- **They set up MLOps infrastructure** on Azure cloud.

### 8.2 Results

- **False positive rate:** ~20% per ticket. Fine-tuning brought it from 20% to 17%. "And that's why we paused it."
- **Colin's assessment (stated on the call):** "When I heard 20%... or 80% success rate, that's pretty much out-of-the-box ChatGPT. So probably, to be honest, the fine-tuning didn't do anything. It's equivalent to what you would get out-of-the-box with an untuned model at all."
- **Mikhail confirmed:** "The fine-tuning actually went from 20% to 17% today, exactly."

### 8.3 Rule-Based Models

- Attempted but not far along: "We've started trying rule-based models, but we haven't gotten far."
- Problem: Variability in how data is entered. "A fab could be spelled many different ways... Some people put Micro 11, some put F11, some put FAB space 11, FAB dash 11."
- Maintenance burden: "We can actually put all these permutations in a rule-based model, but now we're policing this non-stop every day as new things come up."

### 8.4 Labeling Exercise

- Estimated at over 1,000 man-hours for initial labeling plus continuous maintenance.
- This was "fairly expensive" and contributed to the decision to focus on proving the concept with just two fields first.

## 10. Technology and Architecture Context (Business-Relevant)

### 10.1 Current Tool Landscape

- Six different search tools/formulas across the organization
- No single data lake or unified repository
- Some databases are segmented by customer
- Microsoft Teams meeting transcripts are automatically attached to escalation tickets
- LamGPT exists internally (Lam's internal GPT-based tool)
- Copilot is in use (uses GPT models)
- Mix of on-prem and cloud infrastructure

### 10.2 IAM / Identity Access Management

- An enterprise IAM program has been running for approximately two years
- Brad describes progress as real but not "super robust"
- Current access management (ASM) governs certain sensitive areas but not everything
- User identification exists: employees can be identified by work center, role, and some attributes
- Trade-restricted employees and embargo-country employees have special flags and different colored badges
- The IAM gap contributes to over-restriction: without fine-grained identity-based access, they default to restricting broadly

### 10.3 Colin's Unified Control Plane Concept

Colin proposed a strategic framing during the call:

- **Colin:** "There has to be commonality between these applications. The good thing with that is about 95% of a redaction application is identical. The only thing that really changes is maybe some strategy for ingestion, maybe the end use case is a little bit different, but the actual architecture fundamentally is relatively the same."
- **Colin:** "If you want to put your lasso around a lot of things at the org, taking that snowball approach, pick the things that are the highest impact for you first, absolutely good way to start. But overall, you don't want to keep things so fragmented because you're going to end up trying to play whack-a-mole with this."
- **Colin:** "Making sure that entry point is very robust, that is where the biggest bang for your buck is going to be here for sure. It's easier than you would think if you can have that common control plane."

Brad did not push back on this framing. He redirected to keep focus on the core business case but did not disagree with the architectural direction.
