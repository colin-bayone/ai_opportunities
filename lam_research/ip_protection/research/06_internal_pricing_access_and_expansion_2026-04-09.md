## 1. The Core Thesis: Access Is the Real Prize

Colin articulates a clear, forceful thesis about what matters in the Lam engagement. The POC revenue is incidental. The strategic value is getting inside Lam's environment.

**The definitive statement** (Colin, ~15:35):

> "The moment they give me access to a code base, access to data, access to their systems. The moment I have a Lam ID, it's game over. It's game over. We're good. It doesn't even matter if the SOW is signed at that point because I'm gonna get as much information out of that that we can."

This is not aspirational language. Colin frames it as a proven playbook, citing two active engagements as evidence:

> "Same thing with Cisco, same thing with Sephora. You want to see things blow up and get us a trillion opportunities. Let's get that."

Colin restates this later when briefing Amit (~24:06):

> "If they give me and maybe one of my team members a Lam account, you know, that's game over for them because we will be able to get way more out of that from their internal landscape for opportunities than we will get out of, you know, the costing for a POC."

**Key implication:** The POC is an entry vehicle, not a revenue event. The real yield is environmental access -- seeing their systems, their data, their organizational pain points, their internal capabilities (or lack thereof) -- which generates the intelligence needed to scope and sell follow-on work.

## 2. POC Pricing Logic: The Goldilocks Problem

Colin lays out a precise pricing philosophy that flows directly from the access thesis. The POC price must thread a needle between two failure modes.

### 2a. Don't price too high (because POC margin doesn't matter)

Colin (~16:15):

> "Which means that we don't have to go too crazy about the POC quote because the bigger value we're gonna get, you know, it's nice to say 10,000 for the POC and feel good about that and have some margin. Even if margin's 30%, that's 3 grand."

And immediately after (~16:32):

> "Even if we make 3 grand, it's like big deal. I think that's a nice thing for personal, but not in the scope of the company. But the value we get out of those account and access is way greater than that."

The math is explicit: $10,000 POC at 30% margin = $3,000 profit. Colin characterizes this as personally nice but strategically irrelevant compared to the intelligence and relationship access the POC provides.

### 2b. Don't price too low (because it sets expectations)

Colin (~16:54):

> "In a way, even going a little bit low on a POC cost is better because if they're more likely to sign it, the trick is to also not set their expectations too low on pricing. So don't do a POC at cost or for 1000 bucks. Because then they're gonna have an expectation. Oh, the whole project is now 50,000."

This is the anchoring problem: if the POC is $1,000, the client calibrates the full engagement price against that anchor. A $500K or $750K full-scope SOW becomes psychologically impossible to sell after a trivially priced POC.

### 2c. The Amit question: Why not free?

Amit Grover raises the natural question given that Colin described the actual work as trivially easy (~27:15):

> "So do you think we should do it free of cost?"

Both Colin and Anuj reject this immediately and firmly. Colin's reasoning (~27:40):

> "No more free POCs because they send the wrong message. Like for Cisco, we did that. And to be honest, we completely botched that pricing. We didn't even do any pricing model for that, frankly. We just said a number in a room and they said yes."

Colin extends the argument with a qualification-test framing (~28:00):

> "If they don't wanna pay for a POC, that means they're not gonna wanna pay for the engagement is my view."

And ties it directly to the Economic Buyer Identification (EBI) qualification (~28:19):

> "If you can't drop, you know, some money on the POC, then you know you probably also don't have the power to say here's a, you know, a 750K SOW for these guys either."

Anuj reinforces with a motivation/urgency signal framing (~28:34):

> "It also shows urgency and, you know, motivation to solve this problem, right? You know, from a POC, if they're gonna go and take this and try and, you know, get budgeting and this is not prioritized for them, then it's gonna be a very long process."

### 2d. Landing price point

Colin proposes $10,000 for the POC with a three-week engagement timeline (~21:09):

> "Without putting too much thought into it, I think 10,000 is a good price point to get at for something like a three-week engagement starting from the point that we have access to the needed information."

Pratik agrees. The group aligns on this as a working number to refine in the 2:30 PM follow-up session.

## 4. The Cisco Precedent: How Embed-and-Expand Works (and What Went Wrong)

Colin references Cisco repeatedly as both a success case and a cautionary tale.

### 4a. Success: Access led to expansion

Colin cites Cisco alongside Sephora as proof that once you have access, opportunities multiply. The "same thing with Cisco, same thing with Sephora" statement directly links Lam to these two proven embed-and-expand cases.

### 4b. Failure: Pricing was ad hoc

Colin on the Cisco pricing mistake (~27:40):

> "For Cisco, we did that [free/low-cost POC]. And to be honest, we completely botched that pricing. We didn't even do any pricing model for that, frankly. We just said a number in a room and they said yes."

The Cisco project eventually reached a $500K quote (Colin shows this on screen, ~19:31). But the path from free POC to $500K was apparently messy. Colin references that when Cisco asked for a discount (~20:28):

> "They came back to me and they said I want this for 300K and I said good, go do it for 300K then, not my problem. And they came back and said well show us why and I said this, and I said here's your levers to pull at Cisco. X things off of this list or extend the timeline."

This is the outcome-based pricing defense: because pricing is tied to deliverables, not headcount, discount requests require scope reduction, not rate cuts.

### 4c. Access delay as a real risk

Colin on the Cisco onboarding timeline (~31:38):

> "Even for Cisco, Cisco was assigned SOW. It took three entire calendar months to get the access to things."

This is why Colin insists the POC timeline start "from the point that we have access to the needed information" -- he knows from experience that the access provisioning cycle at large enterprises can add months of dead time.

## 5. Outcome-Based Pricing: The Strategic Moat Against Rate Negotiation

Colin describes a deliberate pricing model that protects against the staffing-firm trap.

### 5a. No headcount exposure

Colin (~25:31):

> "The trick here is that there's no headcount in this. It is not billed by resource. If they want to know that, my answer is, you know, no. It's billed by outcome and by deliverable. If you want it to cost less, have less deliverables. It's that easy. But there's no lever to say we're gonna do this at a lower cost because we're going to do it with three people instead of four. They won't even know that."

This is a structural pricing decision that serves the embed-and-expand strategy: if the client never knows the team size, they can't commoditize the work into rate-card negotiations. The value stays opaque and tied to outcomes.

### 5b. Cisco example: The detailed cost sheet

Colin shares his screen showing two artifacts he provided to Cisco:
1. A high-level written breakdown with specific deliverables and assumptions
2. A detailed line-item view showing how the $500K was calculated

He plans to replicate this structure for the Lam POC at a smaller scale (~25:16):

> "I can do the POC costing for sure. The full scope costing we won't be able to do until there is a defined scope that comes from them."

Pratik also advocates showing calculation methodology (~17:18):

> "We should also give them a little bit of how we are pricing it, like how that cost is being calculated, right? It's not something we need to do it. In the hope of building credibility to get a bigger pricing."

Colin reveals a tension with Suresh (referred to as "Rs" in the transcript) about deal strategy (~34:28):

Where they disagreed was on the foot-in-the-door concept. Colin's nuanced position (~34:43):

> "We can bite off smaller chunks if that's all they have the stomach for. But if they're like, you know, full scope, you know they want it to be done for 200K and they don't want to define the scope and they don't want this -- my best thing is don't get pushed around."

Colin's leverage argument (~35:23):

> "Because I think what they've already found out is that they can't do this. If it's been 18 months and they haven't gotten anything done in an intern project, they cannot do this."

**The resolution:** Colin is willing to accept smaller bites (the POC approach) as a foot-in-the-door, consistent with the access thesis. But he draws a line at accepting low pricing or undefined scope on a full engagement. The distinction is: small scope at fair price (good), large scope at depressed price (walk away).

**Open question:** Whether Suresh's disagreement was about the principle of walking away, or specifically about Lam. The transcript suggests it was a general strategic disagreement about how aggressively to pursue clients with unclear budgets.

He immediately names the trade-off (~26:07):

**Implication:** Lam is not being squeezed in between other work. This signals his genuine assessment that Lam has high embed-and-expand potential.

### 7b. The broader bandwidth crisis

This call opens with Colin and Anuj discussing a systemic resource problem that contextualizes the Lam allocation decision.

I don't really know what we're gonna -- you know, someone, we got the new person yesterday for the Google account which is great. Absolutely great. But I was like, what do you want me to do? Anuj confirms he is pushing this internally (~03:07):

And you know, you end up being the only person that we can go to. And you know, that turns out to be challenging for everybody."

Anuj describes what he is proposing (~03:07):

> "I've been, you know, proposing sort of expanding your team in general where you can basically say, hey, you know what, I'm not able to go on this call. Can you, or you know, can you work on the proposal for me?"

Colin on what he should actually be doing vs. what he is doing (~03:49):

> "If I wasn't doing things that I realistically shouldn't be doing in my role, you know, I shouldn't be the guy contributing a million lines of code to a code base. I shouldn't be the guy that's got 15 direct reports. That's where all the time goes. I would prefer to be on these solutions-oriented calls and have time to do that."

**Structural problem identified:** All AI conversations across BayOne funnel to Colin as the sole resource. Every new account manager or sales hire generates more demand on Colin without adding delivery capacity. Colin wants to operate at the solutions/strategy layer, but is pulled into execution and management. Anuj is advocating for team expansion but it has not materialized.

## 8. Perception Management: "We're Building the Moon Here"

### 8a. The complexity gap

Colin establishes that the POC work is technically trivial. On the actual difficulty (~12:39):

> "I told my team members as well. I was like, don't get frustrated that we have to use AI because frankly the problem statement that they presented us with for a POC, I could get done in one day without using AI."

And more specifically (~12:42):

> "I could just do that with simple pattern matching, even no ML, even no NLP. I mean, I could do that with a just a generic Python script. I could do that in the command line if I wanted to."

### 8b. Lam's internal failure

The contrast with Lam's 18-month failure is the basis for the perception advantage (~06:43):

> "They were doing it in a very, you know, infantile way. It's not even like tutorial level stuff. It was kind of just like summer intern type work. And the fact that it's been 18 months is also pretty telling."

### 8c. Anuj's distillation for Amit

When explaining the situation to Amit (who missed the earlier portion), Anuj captures the perception management play in a single line (~29:11):

> "We're building the moon here, absolutely."

This is said with knowing irony -- it is explicitly positioned as the external narrative, not the internal assessment. Colin's prior statement that "these are all internal conversations, they don't need to know that" (~29:04) confirms the deliberate gap between internal assessment (trivial) and external positioning (complex, specialized).

### 8d. Why two weeks instead of one day

Amit asks why the timeline is two weeks if the work is really one to two days (~31:12). Colin gives two reasons:

**Reason 1 -- Team capacity management** (~29:32):

> "I do not have a team that I can just pull people off to go full force on projects. So one day of effort is not the same as me saying that it'll take the team a day because they're already working on a bunch of things."

**Reason 2 -- Perceived difficulty** (~29:48):

> "If you do it too fast, it looks too easy. So it's kind of, you have to keep kind of the smokescreen up so that it looks harder than it is."

**Reason 3 (unstated but implied in what follows)** -- The extra time allows for polish and consultative value-adds (~29:58):

> "The other part of a POC is not just doing the work. I mean, some people work like that and it's kind of like a puppy mill. But for us, it can be very polished, very deliberate, you know, answer all those questions. That's what really works for POCs, because it's personalized to them."

Colin contrasts this with what Sripriya's partner organizations do (~30:28):

> "They're literally just churning out POCs that are --"

Anuj finishes (~30:34):

> "Just pushing a cookie cutter solution or whatever it is that they've done and they're not even listening to the customer."

## 9. The Three-Week Structure: Discovery as Lasting Value

Colin proposes a one-plus-two week structure (~31:54):

> "One week for discovery and be clear that that discovery is, you know, a lasting value that we don't have to do again if we choose to do the full project. And the following two weeks are for the actual build out of the POC."

This is strategically important for the access thesis. The "discovery" week is when the real intelligence gathering happens: meeting the team, understanding the data landscape, identifying pain points beyond the immediate scope. Colin frames it as value-add ("lasting value"), but it is also the primary window for embed-and-expand reconnaissance.

Discovery activities Colin envisions during that week (~31:54):

> "What are we trying to specifically solve for here? You know, they said we have this list of, you know, items and things like they were saying on the call. Well, where is that? Who owns that? Who's the final person that says, you know, yes, this passes, no, this fails. Those are things we can find out in discovery."

## 10. The Scoping Ambiguity as Both Risk and Opportunity

### 10a. Risk: Three different people, three different scopes

Colin describes the scoping confusion among Lam stakeholders (~10:36):

> "If you listen to Mikhail, you've got a problem where it's like specifically internal enablement. If you talk to Brad, it's both internal and at customer sites or competitor sites. If you talk to the third person, his take is we have to focus on customer sites."

> "Meanwhile, Brad and Mikhail are like, what are you talking about? We don't even have this internally. Why are we talking about something that's, you know, 3 levels of maturity later?"

### 10b. Opportunity: They don't know what they don't know

Colin (~11:14):

> "The positive signal that came out of that is that it was so disorganized that you could see that there's big opportunity because they don't even know what they don't know."

This is the embed-and-expand upside of scoping ambiguity: an organization that cannot articulate its own requirements is an organization that needs ongoing consultative guidance. Every scoping conversation becomes a discovery opportunity. Every phase boundary becomes a chance to expand.

### 10c. No financial justification exists internally

Colin (~13:00):

> "They never really talk about business value. They keep on saying hours saved and this is really important, but it sounds like even internally, they've never really had any financial justification to do this in the first place."

Colin notes this has an interesting flip side (~13:19):

> "Which makes our life a little bit tough, but that also means we can over quote and be safe."

The logic: if the client has never established an internal ROI benchmark, there is no number to compare against. BayOne can set the value framing rather than responding to a pre-established budget envelope.
