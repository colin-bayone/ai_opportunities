## Colin's Proposed Meeting Structure

Colin lays out a **four-phase meeting structure** for the 1.5-hour session:

### Phase 1: Restate the Problem (Credibility Part 1)

Colin: **"Started off with restating the problem to them, doing that quickly, high level, allowing for them to ask questions. But that establishes part one of credibility, which is these guys understand."**

He explicitly notes this is better than a cold start: "It's also better than a cold start at the meeting where we just dive right in and start hammering the questions."

### Phase 2: Coherent Experience (Credibility Part 2)

Colin: **"From that, springboard off of talking about Coherent."** (Note: "Coherent" is the company name, likely transcribed variously as "Cohere," "cohen," etc. throughout the transcript.)

Colin describes what to share about Coherent: "Here's what we really did. Here's a company just like you with the exact same constraints and even more complicated because they're talking about China and business and you guys aren't talking about that. You're talking about US-based employees having these problems. Here's why, here's what we tried. Here's what didn't work. Here's why a hybrid solution ended up being the right fit."

Colin frames Coherent as an exact precedent: "This exact project we did for Coherent. So that's the good news. And we got to do that from the earlier days of AI when things were a lot less defined. So that's definitely an advantage."

He describes his personal credibility: "There's an older group of guys that were with Oracle from the early days. They have this intimate, inside and out knowledge... I'm like, I get to be that for AI, because I got to be here from when it was born. And now, see how it's evolved."

### Phase 3: Questions -- First 45 Minutes

Amit proposes the structure, which Colin agrees with: **"We can start with the idea of just go through these questions, whatever number of questions we can come up with, without even talking about the solution, timeline, and budget. Is that something we could do? Colin, we can just go through the entire first half of the entire one and a half hours -- like 45 minutes -- only questioning."**

Colin's question strategy has multiple purposes:

**Extracting scope:** "The few questions that will help us, because I agree, I think they're still going to be ambiguous. But in order to do costing, we'll have to do scoping. And that's something that I can extract out of them probably in the meeting."

**Understanding in-house perception:** "One thing that I hope to get out of this meeting would be to understand what their in-house capability is like without explicitly asking the question. Because that will gauge how they perceive this problem in mind."

Colin describes two scenarios: "If they think that this is a really easy problem and any dummy could do it, of course they're not going to pay too much. But if they see this as something where they don't even think it's possible anymore because they've tried and failed and it didn't even get one person in the way there, which is where I'm thinking they are based upon talking with Mikheil, then we're in really good shape."

**Defining a concrete entry point:** "Basically what we need from them is an entry point, a starting point. Don't just say we want to help all, like how Mikheil was talking -- it's like a constellation. Like there's some stars here, some stars here, some stars behind me. That won't work because there's no concrete place for us to roll up our sleeves and get our hands dirty."

Colin proposes anchoring to prior work: "If they can pick an application and maybe they can just point us to what they tried before, that gives us a good entry point that we can scope."

**Building credibility while exposing competitor weakness:** Colin describes his approach to the prior failed attempt: "If you tell them, yeah, this was nice but they just logically was unsound, you're going to gain credibility with them and then it discounts other people too. You're not throwing them under the bus like, oh, they're terrible at their job, but even just the kind of side comments like, 'what was the goal for this?' -- asking basic questions gives you credibility while taking it from the other guy."

He frames this as a deliberate strategic move: **"It's almost like an RFP, but we're intentionally asking hard questions that we can answer great, but we know other people are going to fall over on."** He notes: "They're also going to remember those questions you ask because they're going to ask them to potential competitors."

**Defining success criteria:** Colin wants to interrogate their stated metrics: "He said 97% accurate in under five seconds based on one. Is that a need? Is that a want? Is that a dream? What's the actual requirement for success? And we'll know that once we know how their existing system works."

He suspects the metrics are aspirational, not baseline: "If they are even at the point of asking this later down the line, they are probably at the point of, we didn't know that this was even an issue... if they say I want a response in five seconds, they don't mean that, because their current application doesn't give them a response in five seconds. That would be improving two things at once."

### Phase 4: Teaser of Approach (Second Half)

Amit frames this: **"Maybe just give them a teaser of what we already know could be a potential answer and then tie those things together and then proceed towards the finish line which is sign off on the SOW or even pilot."**

Colin describes what to show: lead them to "this rosy picture of the future" -- not just that a POC proves something is possible, but what the actual end state looks like.

## The Information Sharing Strategy: "Secret Sauce" Approach

This is the most extensively discussed strategic element of the call. Colin, Anuj, and Amit all weigh in on what to share versus withhold.

### Colin's Core Thesis: Share the Architecture Openly

Colin describes his "first trickiness": **"Even this, I will sound intentionally like I'm giving away all the detail. And here's why. Technically speaking, I have confidence that they can give this to anyone else, and they won't be able to deliver it."**

Two reasons for this confidence:
1. **Execution capability:** "Because we're just so good."
2. **Missing critical details:** **"Because I'm leaving out some very critical bits of information here. Even though it looks complete, it's missing the spark plug that actually makes it work in practice."**

Colin's definitive statement on this risk: **"If they have someone on their end that would have that just in their mind, then they wouldn't need us anyway. And there's no deal anyway."**

### What Specifically Gets Shared

**The two-layer architecture (deterministic + custom):** Colin describes: "The deterministic layer, it's kind of like two layers to an onion. Deterministic layer is those tools that already exist, and we'll be able to talk a lot to those. So things like Microsoft Purview -- that's where things should go. And that gives you essentially role-based redaction for things like PII. That one is a solved problem."

He notes the cost reality they should not share: "With off-the-shelf tools, with their already existing ecosystem, they could do this today, and they would pay maybe $7 a month for it, and Microsoft. The trick is they don't need to know that it's $7 a month and that it would take someone like us to set up for an hour and a half. They don't need to know that. They just need to know that this is one part of a much grander solution."

**The approach description:** Colin: "I'm trying to relate back specific parts of what this solves to their problem without telling them how I'm actually going to do it. I can say that verbally, but anything we say is like ammunition for them to go give the other guy ideas."

**The unified data plane vision (the real differentiator):** Colin: **"Here's the secret sauce here, unified data plane. Not just a POC that says, yes, we can redact information on the fly. That's something that you're going to find a ton of competition for. The trick is making it work for them, in their environment, at their scale. That's the real magic to it."**

He describes what this means in practice: "You don't have all this complexity. We have 30 applications over here, and 10 over here, and three behind me. Regardless, they're all using one common layer that is able to be maintained by your IT department, but we can certainly help and provide support, but it's using standard tools, standard methodologies, as the orchestrator layer for all of this, as a solution."

### What Gets Held Back

- The specific implementation details ("the spark plug") that make the architecture actually work in practice.
- Cost and effort estimates for specific components (e.g., the $7/month Purview setup).
- Time estimates: Anuj says, "We should not give them if it's going to take us a day or a month. We need to gauge what their appetite is as well."
- Headcount and hourly breakdowns: Colin cites Suresh's advice on this later in the call -- "keep their procurement teams the heck away from headcounts and even that amount of hours per deliverable."

### Anuj's Concern and Agreement

Anuj validates the risk: **"They are evaluating and they're trying to grasp and get a lot of understanding on it. And it's very likely, to your point, they may share it with their competitors as well too. And they might also try to do it in-house if they feel 'this is how to do this.' So we have the competition on all aspects of what we have learned so far."**

His proposed mitigation: "We always remind them of the mutual non-disclosure and so forth. But at the same time, not give enough that they can actually move on their own."

### Amit's Balancing Point

Amit frames the tension: **"If we have to establish our credibility, we have to give them something. That's a tricky one."**

His proposed approach: "Yes, we understood the problem and we've already got something in our mind. And this is how it looks like, but not open the whole window. And just you can take a peek. And this is what you see."

He also suggests getting Daniel's buy-in on what was tried before: "We have to get his peace of mind too as to what was done before technically and how can that be improvised... how was that not useful and what we think is much more useful for you."

## Competitive Positioning Tactics

### The Prior Attempt Was "Logically Unsound"

Colin explicitly describes how to frame the prior failed attempt: **"Here's what you did wrong here. You went with a totally custom solution without even investigating. That was what you did wrong. You didn't try to do what is a known industry standard approach. Even if you need something more custom, you always start. Every AI project must start from that known baseline."**

This positions BayOne as methodologically sound while implicitly discrediting the prior vendor without naming them or being overtly negative.

Colin's engineering-not-sales framing: "I'm going to treat this like it's engineering and not sales, so bear with me on this. One thing with engineering is that you don't want to throw shade at people, but you also don't want to say everyone did a good job all the time."

### Credibility Through Questions That Double as Traps

Colin describes deliberately seeding hard questions: "Those questions you ask -- they're going to ask them to potential competitors. Essentially, think of it this way. It's almost like an RFP, but we're intentionally asking hard questions that we can answer great, but we know other people are going to fall over on."

### Competition Assessment

Colin's view: "Stuff like this, I can tell you right out of the gate, doesn't really have competition. It's more like... there's certain things that not everyone can do."

He breaks it into two questions:
1. "Is there a ready-made off-the-shelf solution? The answer is no."
2. "Of the people that would provide you a custom solution, do they get your business? And that answer is maybe. But the fact that they already tried and had some failure is good for us."

Colin is confident about the technical moat: "Not a lot of people are going to be able to have this conversation from the AI perspective. That's a good thing for us. There's a very good advantage we have from a technical standpoint because we can talk about it in an experienced way. The reason why this isn't common in history is because not a lot of people get this type of problem handed to them."

### The "Anyone Can Redact" vs. "Making It Work" Distinction

Colin differentiates sharply between the commodity offering and the real value: **"Anyone, like I said earlier, it's an intern-level project to do what they set out to do. The trick is to do it in a way that actually makes business sense."**

He warns against what typical competitors will do: "What would they do? They're going to hear the RFP. Imagine it was an RFP. They're going to hear the proposal and say they need redaction, identification, and classification. They're going to propose that. They're not going to propose the entire house along with the kitchen. And that's the strategy here."

### Enterprise Tools as Positioning

Colin: "Here's where the enterprise tools come in. To actually say, here's what you should use off the shelf versus here's the things and why you should do them custom as dual parts of the solution. They will make technical sense."

This positions BayOne as vendor-neutral advisors, not just custom solution sellers.

### The "Come Back in Nine Months" Confidence

Colin's assessment of what happens if Lam shops the approach: **"They could say this and they go, 'Aha, we're going to take this to Accenture and tell them to do it.' My advice to them is good luck. May the best man win, and I'll still be here when you call me in nine months whenever you went down the rabbit hole and didn't figure anything out."**

He adds: "It seems like it's a bad thing to give too much information, but if you do it in a very metered way, you can end up giving yourself some ammunition even if they're going to shop around. And even if they're not, it's still okay because their technical team is going to be happy, and a project like this, that's pretty much what matters."

## POC vs. End State: The Real Differentiator

Colin draws a critical distinction between what a POC proves and what the end-state vision communicates:

**"POC is one thing, but what do you actually want out of the POC? POC tells you it's possible. It doesn't tell you what the end state actually is going to look like. So if we tell them what the end state's going to look like, once again leaving out those secret ingredients that actually make this work, then that's something that other vendors are not going to be able to compare to."**

The unified data plane is the end-state vision: all applications using one common orchestrator layer, maintained by Lam's IT department, using standard tools and methodologies. This is what competitors cannot credibly articulate because "you can't say that unless you've actually done it."

Colin on why AI tools cannot substitute for this experience: "You can ask ChatGPT. You're never going to get the real answer out of it because this stuff changes all the time and it's very industry specific."

## Scoping Strategy

### Two Types of Scoping

Colin describes a **pillar model** for scoping: "We can do two types of scoping. Type one: for the immediate scope of work. Type two: we now understand the broader picture at Lam. So almost think of it like pillars in a Roman building. This is just the first column, which is this first project. Once we know what that one looks like, we have a good estimate of what the others do. So then the scope of work is just that times time or times effort, if they want to get it done faster."

### Lam's Urgency -- Not Optional

Colin argues Lam does not have the luxury of delaying: **"They're unique because they handle their competitor's data. They have TSMC data. So they can't have a screw-up here. They are effectively shutting off AI for that duration. So they don't really have a choice but to do this as soon as they can, or risk otherwise exposing information."**

He frames this as a safety cost, not a convenience: "It doesn't set a timeline for sure, but that also means that if it's expensive, it's expensive. It's a safety cost. It's not a normal business 'this is convenient, nice to have.' It's like, make sure we don't end up in a courtroom because our internal tools leaked customer or competitor information."

### Estimating Without Full Information

Colin describes his approach (using Cisco as a parallel): Start with the realistic estimate, then multiply significantly to build in buffer. For Cisco screen conversions: "He said, how long would it take you? And I said, once we're at full pace, probably two hours. And he said, what's your confidence? And I said, I don't know, I haven't seen their code yet. I have no clue. So instead of two hours, what I said was eight hours. Arbitrarily higher. Something where there's buffer built in for sure."

Rationale: "At the end of the day, all that we're going to do is deliver faster. And with solutions, they're not trying to do this at the lowest cost because they would do it in-house if that was the case or they'd go for a commercial solution if it existed."

## Pricing and POC Strategy

### No Free Pilot

Amit is emphatic: **"We should not do a free pilot at all, which I'm feeling we should definitely ask for -- whatever, even if it's a marginal cost dollar. We should tell them that it's going to cost a little bit."**

His reasoning: "If we say we'll do a free pilot, then they might be enticed to do the same pilot with other players also. That's one of the reasons Accenture, PwC, or any of these Deloitte's -- they do POCs, they do pay, because they don't want to keep doing POCs and not being paid and just keep solving problems."

### Milestone-Based, Not Headcount-Based Pricing

Colin describes the pricing philosophy (citing Suresh's guidance): "Rather than you saying based on headcount, you don't need to know the headcount. You don't need to know. If it takes me one person, if it takes me 100, that's my business, not yours. You need to know how much the deliverable outcome is worth to you in the timeline that you want it delivered."

Levers for the client: "Ask me for a discount or simply give you more time on the clock."

Why to avoid headcount exposure: "They're going to try to say, I'm going to divide that, and here's the hourly rate, and we're going to negotiate on that. We can cut it out entirely by just saying, how much is this worth to you?"

### The 1,000 Man-Hours Reference

Colin debunks the "1,000 man-hours for data labeling" figure that Lam had mentioned in a prior call: "They threw out 1,000, and I'm like... it's a round number because someone told you metrics are good on resumes." He notes this is useful because it reveals Lam does not actually know the scope: "The good thing is neither do they."

## The Holistic Solution Positioning

Colin describes how to frame BayOne's approach as going beyond just AI: "We can present to them a holistic solution. Not just we're really good at AI and it's just this piece... Here's how we're going to make this work with what you've already done. Here's the historical cleanup. We're not just going to say we're starting over from zero, but we're actually going to take into account all these different things."

He describes the "Goldilocks" problem most vendors face: "What usually turns people aside is what most vendors do, which is they either go too small or too big. They're not in that Goldilocks position where it's just right. We're not saying we're going to touch your entire organization. It's in the same scope of what you originally asked us to do."

The approach expands the value conversation beyond AI: "We're expanding the scope beyond just AI. It's not just an AI problem anymore. Now we're talking about credential and access management. Now we're talking about governance and visibility."

A key anti-pattern to avoid: "It's the opposite of those vendors that are like, hey, we're going to come in with a custom platform. And great, we've got the super cool tool, but now you guys have to use one more thing. And if it falls over, you guys have nothing. This still is building it on their system, so there's a level of comfortableness that is here."
