## 4. What the Proposal Must Contain

Based on the discussion, the proposal is POC-scoped only. The team explicitly deferred full-scope costing.

### 4a. Deliverables
Colin will specify deliverables in the proposal, following the Cisco model: "I gave specific deliverables here as to what's happening." [00:19:59] He will also include "option A, option B" pricing variants. [00:25:31]

### 4b. Assumptions
Listed as a required section: "Here are the assumptions we have going in." [00:25:31]

### 4c. Pricing (Outcome-Based, Not Headcount-Based)
This was a critical and repeated point. Colin described the lesson learned from Cisco:

- "What we did wrong on the first Cisco project is we made it so that it was headcount." [00:20:28]
- "If it's outcome based, then if they want a discount... they came back to me and they said I want this for 300K and I said good, go do it for 300K then, not my problem." [00:20:28]
- "This is based by outcome. This isn't based by headcount. We don't even expose that to them." [00:20:44]
- "If they want to know that, my answer is no. It's billed by outcome and by deliverable. So if you want it to cost less, have less deliverables. It's that easy." [00:25:31]
- "There's no lever to say we're gonna do this at a lower cost because we're going to do it with three people instead of four. They won't even know that." [00:26:07]

**Price point discussed: approximately $10,000 for the POC.** Colin: "Without putting too much thought into it, I think 10,000 is a good price point to get at for something like a three-week engagement starting from the point that we have access to the needed information." [00:21:09] Pat agreed: "I think I'm in agreement on that." [00:21:21]

## 6. POC Pricing Strategy and Rationale

### 6a. Don't Over-Optimize on POC Margin
Colin argued the POC margin is secondary to the access it provides:

- "It's nice to say 10,000 for the POC and feel good about that and have some margin. Even if margin's 30%, that's 3 grand." [00:16:15]
- "Even if we make 3 grand, it's like big deal. I think that's a nice thing for personal, but not in the scope of the company. But the value we get out of those accounts and access is way greater than that." [00:16:32]

### 6b. Don't Go Too Low Either
- Colin: "The trick is to also not set their expectations too low on pricing. So don't do a POC at cost or for 1000 bucks. Because then they're gonna have an expectation oh, the whole project is now 50,000." [00:16:54]

### 6c. Why Three Weeks (Not Less)
Colin gave two reasons for the three-week POC timeline rather than a shorter one:

1. **Capacity management:** "I do not have a team that I can just pull people off to go full force on projects. So one day of effort is not the same as me saying that it'll take the team a day because they're already working on a bunch of things." [00:29:32]
2. **Perception management:** "If you do it too fast, it looks too easy." [00:29:48]

He also noted the value of time for polish: "At max two weeks for the POC cycle because that way we can have beautiful polished... looks like it was built for them in a Santa's workshop type of feel to it." [00:33:04]

Anuj had expected a shorter timeline: "Between you and me, I was expecting you to say one week." [00:30:51]

### 6d. Internal vs. External Messaging
Colin emphasized that internal assessment of difficulty must not leak to the client:

- "These are all internal conversations. They don't need to know that. As far as they know, it's something they haven't been able to fix in 18 months." [00:29:04]
- Anuj reinforced sarcastically: "It's extremely difficult. We're building the moon here, absolutely." [00:29:11]
