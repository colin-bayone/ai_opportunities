## 1. The Friday Meeting with Siva: Listen, Don't Pitch

### Meeting Format and Setup

Siva offered a Friday slot, 11:00 to 12:00, for a remote meeting. The team was still finalizing the invite at the time of this call. Siva's plan was to give BayOne "an overall everything of what they're doing" -- an overview of his organization's responsibilities, current initiatives, and what they would like to accomplish. The attendees from Tailored Brands were to include Siva, Kalyan, and Kieran (Anitha could not join).

Suraj described Siva's communication style as open and forthcoming: "He'll give you as much details as we need. It's not going to be one of those clients where we have to pull information out." This is a significant contrast to other engagements and shapes the entire meeting strategy -- BayOne's role is to receive, not to push.

### Core Strategic Directive: Information Gathering, Not Selling

The single clearest strategic decision in this call was the directive to make Friday's meeting a listening session, not a sales pitch. This was articulated most directly by Suraj:

> "The last two things I would say here is definitely I would actually go lighter on case studies, more on just listening. I think we should not pitch. We should just listen and brainstorm, that's all."

Colin reinforced this by framing what the meeting should produce: "So that was my goal." The email going out to Siva would set expectations accordingly -- BayOne comes to learn, not to present.

The rationale was multi-layered:

1. **Information asymmetry favors waiting.** Siva's team just received their budgets on February 1. Their roadmap is set. BayOne needs to understand that roadmap before proposing anything. As one participant stated: "Once we understand what their roadmaps are and a lot of the pieces that we already have, we're able to incorporate that. Whether they give us an opportunity or not, we can definitely go back with a proposal."

2. **Siva will volunteer information freely.** The team repeatedly emphasized that Siva is not a client who holds back. He will lay out his problem statements, his team's scope, and his ambitions. The right move is to let him talk.

3. **Premature pitching would backfire.** Without validated intelligence about their actual priorities, any pitch risks being generic or misaligned. The team wanted to avoid the classic mistake of presenting capabilities that don't map to the client's actual needs.

### What BayOne Should Do in the Meeting

The meeting had two explicitly stated objectives:

**Objective 1: Establish BayOne as a partner, not just a staffing vendor.** As stated in the call: "One, for them to understand -- again, currently we are staffing. This is a big pain point... So we want to open up his mind and his mind to look at us as a partner." The team wants to shift the perception from "we place contractors" to "we solve problems with you." The phrasing used was: "AI, we know it. Help us understand what you're doing. We will come back to you with some proposals which will solve some of your problems."

**Objective 2: Gather roadmap and initiative intelligence.** The second objective was pure information collection -- understand Siva's plans, priorities, pain points, and organizational dynamics. This intelligence would then feed a formal proposal.

The approach within the meeting itself was described as: ask questions, brainstorm together, but do not come in with a pre-packaged pitch. Suraj drew on his experience: "When it comes to solutions... they don't want you to come in and just say, well, we've done this, we can do it all for you." Instead, the approach is to let Siva present his problem statements, then ask clarifying questions and think alongside him.

Colin's suggested approach was to "break it up in the pieces" rather than presenting a sweeping proposal. He specifically advised against big-number proposals: "Don't give the millions of dollars. Break it up in the pieces technically."


## 2. Ego Management: These Are Smart People

### The Core Warning

Suraj issued a direct and repeated warning about how to handle the personalities at Tailored Brands. This was not a throwaway comment -- it was emphasized as a critical success factor:

> "Keep in mind that they're very, very -- we have to make sure we stroke their egos. Very much where, don't tell us we don't know this, but yet they don't know this, so we have to make sure."

He elaborated further: "Siva's very protective, and these guys are very smart. Now, Anitha is junior director. These guys are extremely strong in their tech world. So we can't come in, just -- ask her questions like you do. I mean, you know your stuff, but I have to be always, like I always tell people when we get to the level, too. These guys are pretty smart."

### The Paradox: Smart but Inexperienced in AI

The strategic tension is that these leaders are genuinely intelligent and accomplished in their respective technical domains (UI, Java, engineering management), but they lack functional experience in AI. This creates a delicate dynamic:

- **Kalyan** was just made Director of AI approximately one week before this call. He does not have prior functional AI experience. Suraj described him: "This guy is very intelligent. Very. Like his IQ is top of the chart. But he's not a people person." More pointedly, Suraj reported that "Kalyan literally admitted in the meeting that he doesn't know anything about AI" just that morning. Whether this was a strategic move or genuine admission was unclear -- Suraj said: "Whether it's a front or not a front, you know."

- **Kieran** was described as "super, super humble" and someone who "won't advertise how much he knows about AI, but he's extremely sharp." He has managed teams of 140+ contractors and FTEs and is deeply versed in mobile and UI technology, but AI is new territory for him too.

Colin identified the strategic opportunity this creates: "If you have someone that's new, that's maybe a little bit out of their water, that's a ton of pressure... it doesn't make up for actually having done stuff like this." But the approach must be collaborative, not condescending. The framing should be: "You're the SME for your industry. We're the experts over here. Let's marry the two."

### How to Navigate This

The prescribed approach was what Suraj called "a gentle approach":

- Do not present information in a way that implies the client doesn't know things
- Frame AI knowledge as complementary to their deep domain expertise
- Brainstorm together rather than lecture
- Let them take what BayOne provides and run with it -- "Even though they might not know a lot about AI, they'll take what you have and then brainstorm with it. It's a good thing, right?"
- Respect the organizational hierarchy and seniority dynamics

Suraj specifically told Colin: "The way you approach it will be a little gentle approach. Does that make sense?" Colin confirmed: "Absolutely."

## 5. The Carl (CPO) Meeting After Leadership Conference

### Why Carl Matters

Carl Vasani is the Chief Product Officer at Tailored Brands. His significance is both budgetary and strategic:

- **Budget authority:** "All of our SOWs are approved by Carl for budget." Siva's budget runs through Carl.
- **Long relationship with BayOne:** "Carl knows us, and Carl knows me." Suraj specifically noted: "Carl was one of my biggest SOW sign-off person at Albertsons a long time ago."
- **Strategic perspective:** Carl has the big-picture view of what Tailored Brands is trying to accomplish across all product and engineering areas.

### The Proposal to Meet Carl

Rima raised the idea of a separate meeting with Carl: "If you have access to him, it may be good to meet him more casually to hear out the whole big picture story, what they're trying to do, etc., because that will help us align better through the engineering teams."

The reasoning was that Carl's perspective would provide the strategic overlay that the engineering-level conversations with Siva's team would lack. Understanding Carl's priorities would let BayOne align proposals to the business strategy, not just the technical roadmap.

### Timing: After the Leadership Conference

Suraj confirmed access to Carl is not a problem -- "When I go to the office, [Siva] invites me to all the leadership meetings." However, the timing was deliberate. All Tailored Brands leaders were attending a leadership conference the following week. The team decided:

> "Carl, they have leadership conference next week, all of them, so all of the leaders for all the areas. It's actually better to do it after."

The reasoning was strategic: after the leadership conference, Carl and the other leaders would have freshly aligned on priorities and budgets. Meeting Carl beforehand would mean getting pre-conference information that might shift.

Colin suggested the Carl meeting should be a "smaller call alone, so that we can validate or understand." The idea was to use the Carl meeting as a validation checkpoint -- compare what AI research and Siva's team provide against what the CPO says the actual priorities are. Colin said: "AI will give us a lot of data, but we've got to just validate whether it is accurate or not. And then when we talk to the engineering teams, we will be able to align a lot better to initiatives."

### Separation from the Group Meeting

Colin was explicit that the Carl meeting should be distinct from the engineering team meeting: "Let's do two separate things if we can make that happen." The Carl meeting should happen "either before or after" the in-person group meeting, but as a separate conversation. This avoids mixing the strategic business conversation with the tactical engineering discussion.

Suraj noted a caveat: "We don't have Siva to walk us in. That's not required, but if he's OK with it, that's fine."

## 8. Overall Positioning: Industry SME + Domain Knowledge = Partnership

### The Positioning Framework

Colin articulated the positioning strategy that should frame all engagement with Tailored Brands:

> "I think the positioning from it, if we come in from that angle of -- you're the SME for industry. We don't have to have use cases or anything that's directly specific, but we're the experts over here. Let's marry the two and now everyone has started."

This is a partnership positioning, not a vendor positioning. It acknowledges that:

1. **Tailored Brands' people know their business deeply.** They understand retail, their customers, their systems, and their competitive dynamics. They are the subject matter experts for their industry.
2. **BayOne brings AI and engineering methodology expertise.** BayOne doesn't need to pretend to be retail experts. They bring the technical capability, the delivery methodology, and the cross-industry pattern recognition.
3. **The combination is the value proposition.** Neither party has the complete picture alone. The partnership framing makes it collaborative rather than transactional.

### Why This Positioning Works for This Client

Several factors make this the right approach for Tailored Brands specifically:

- **The ego dynamic.** These are accomplished technical leaders who would bristle at being told what to do. The partnership frame respects their intelligence and expertise.
- **The AI knowledge gap.** They are smart but new to AI. Positioning BayOne as the AI expert who needs their domain knowledge creates a natural, non-threatening collaboration.
- **The staffing-to-solutions transition.** BayOne currently has a staffing relationship. Moving to a solutions partnership requires changing the dynamic from "we provide bodies" to "we solve problems together."
- **Siva's ambitions.** Siva wants to make a name for himself at Tailored Brands. He needs partners who help him succeed, not vendors who just fill seats.

### Case Studies: Use Sparingly

Suraj was explicit that case studies should not be the lead: "I would actually go lighter on case studies, more on just listening." However, they are not off the table entirely. The Cisco CI/CD work was mentioned as potentially relevant for the code modernization conversation. The idea is to use case studies reactively -- if a topic comes up where BayOne has directly relevant experience, mention it -- rather than proactively presenting a deck of case studies.

Colin noted that for the AI-powered features bucket (external-facing), "if they don't have experience, they're good idea guys, but they don't actually do it. They usually struggle there to gain any momentum traction. Which is a good place for us."
