FedRAMP: What It Is
FedRAMP stands for the Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program. It's a government-wide program that standardizes the security assessment, authorization, and continuous monitoring for cloud products and services used by federal agencies. Paramify Think of it as the federal government's stamp of approval that says "this cloud service is secure enough for us to use."
There are three impact levels: Low, Moderate, and High. The level determines how many security controls must be implemented, based on NIST 800-53 standards.
FedRAMP Moderate is the baseline most commonly required for handling Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI). It covers a broad set of security controls but does not impose geographic restrictions on where data is stored or processed.
FedRAMP High adds significantly more stringent requirements, including data residency restrictions that mandate information processing and storage remain within U.S. territories or locations under U.S. jurisdiction. This is the level that maps more naturally to defense work.
The authorization process is notoriously long and expensive. A cloud service provider gets assessed by a certified Third Party Assessment Organization (3PAO), and then the authorization is granted either through a federal agency or through the Joint Authorization Board (which includes CIOs from DoD, DHS, and GSA). A new initiative called FedRAMP 20x was introduced in 2025 to simplify and add more automation to the process. Paramify
Why this matters for the call: If Zeblok is targeting defense customers with their on-prem Ai-MicroCloud, the question becomes whether their platform itself needs FedRAMP authorization, or whether their on-prem deployment model sidesteps that requirement because data never leaves the customer's perimeter. This is a nuanced but important distinction to explore.

ITAR: What It Is
ITAR stands for International Traffic in Arms Regulations. It's a set of regulations that control the export, import, and brokering of defense articles, defense services, and related technical data Kiteworks, enforced by the State Department's Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC). Originally enacted during the Cold War, it remains one of the most critical pieces of legislation governing defense-related data and goods.
ITAR covers anything on the United States Munitions List (USML), which includes weapons systems, military electronics, protective equipment, military-grade software, and associated technical data. The core focus is preventing unauthorized foreign access to sensitive technologies that could impact national security.
The penalties for violations are severe. BAE Systems was fined $400 million in criminal penalties for ITAR violations in 2010. Agile IT Beyond fines, violations can result in loss of contracts, denial of export licenses, and even imprisonment.
The critical thing to understand about ITAR in practice: It's not a certification you earn. There's no "ITAR certified" badge for cloud providers. ITAR's regulations are broad rather than granular, so there aren't specific technical requirements a cloud provider must check off. Agile IT Instead, organizations handling ITAR data must ensure compliance through their operational and technical controls. The key requirements are:

All personnel with access to ITAR-controlled data must be U.S. Persons (U.S. citizens, permanent residents, or protected persons under asylum)
ITAR-controlled technical data must remain stored on U.S. soil
No unauthorized export of covered technical data to non-U.S. persons can occur
