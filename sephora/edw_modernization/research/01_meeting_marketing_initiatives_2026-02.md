#### Journey-Based Campaigns: The Loyalty Signup Example

Mani provides a concrete, detailed example of journey-based campaigns. He frames it conversationally, noting that Zara and the BayOne rep likely shop at Sephora:

1. **Trigger:** A customer signs up for the Sephora loyalty program.
2. **First touchpoint:** A welcome email is sent.
3. **Branching logic:** After the welcome email, the system evaluates whether the new loyalty member has made a purchase.
   - **If yes (purchase made):** The customer enters one branch of the journey with targeted follow-up messaging appropriate to a purchasing member.
   - **If no (no purchase after signup):** The customer enters a different branch designed to encourage a first purchase.
4. **Continued branching:** Mani describes this as continuing with additional if/else conditions -- "imagine like if/else conditions" -- where each subsequent behavior triggers another branch of the journey.

This is described as a "typical journey-based campaigns" pattern. The entire system of journeys, conditions, and branched communications constitutes the marketing orchestration function.

**Organizational history:** This CRM/orchestration area used to be under Ram. It has now been moved to Rizwan as part of Mani's restructuring.

**2026 projects:** Marketing personalization is called out as "one of the important projects for next year" (2026).

## 4. Enterprise-Wide Reporting Democratization (Context for Marketing)

While not exclusively a marketing initiative, this directly affects marketing reporting and is a major 2026 theme:

- **Current state:** One central team (Mani's team) handles enterprise-wide reports and analytics.
- **Future state:** Reports will be democratized to individual domain engineering teams (stores, e-commerce, supply chain, etc.). Mani's team becomes a lean "platform reporting" team focused on frameworks, governance, and tool standards.
- **Three models being piloted:**

| Model | Domain | Hiring Manager | Execution | Status |
|-------|--------|---------------|-----------|--------|
| **Model 1** | Stores (David's area) | David and Natalia hire directly | David's team delivers; Grishi/Andrew Ho's team advises as subject matter experts | Already underway |
| **Model 2** | E-commerce (Rajesh's area), starting with omni-channel order fulfillment (BOPIS, same-day delivery) | Grishi hires, but engineers sit in Rajesh's org | Rajesh's team owns delivery; engineers transition fully to Rajesh's org after ~6 months if successful (by June/July 2026) | Starting |
| **Model 3** | Supply chain/Merchandising (Kalyan's area) | Grishi hires and manages | Continues as-is (centralized delivery) until Models 1 and 2 prove successful | Continuing current approach |

The reason Model 3 is deferred: Kalyan's area (supply chain, merchandising) still runs on legacy technologies, while stores and e-commerce are already on modern technology stacks. It does not make sense to democratize reporting for legacy systems that are themselves slated for modernization.

## 5. EDW Modernization: The Cross-Cutting 2026 Mega-Project

The EDW (Enterprise Data Warehouse) Modernization project touches all marketing areas and is the single largest initiative discussed. Key details:

- **Scope:** Re-engineering all reports built over the past 15-20 years. Estimated "thousands" of reports.
- **Legacy technologies being retired:** IBM Cognos, SQL Server.
- **Target technologies:** Databricks (primary data platform), Tableau (some areas), ThoughtSpot (some areas). Some reports will remain in Cognos but with data sourced from the new Databricks platform instead of the old database.
- **Timeline:** Three-year plan (2026-2028). First year (2026) already started. Aspiration to compress to finish by 2027 or early 2028.
- **Approach:** Department-by-department, subdivision-by-subdivision. Finance department taken first; within finance, business planning is the first subdivision (accounting comes later).
- **AI opportunity:** Mani explicitly identifies two AI use cases within EDW Modernization:
  1. **Batch migration acceleration:** Instead of migrating reports one-by-one from SQL Server to Databricks, use AI to identify patterns and migrate groups of reports simultaneously (e.g., finish three or six reports in one shot).
  2. **Legacy codebase analysis:** Cognos reports built 15-20 years ago have deeply embedded, complex queries. Manual review "will take years." AI could analyze the entire codebase, surface areas of concern, identify dependencies, and proactively flag issues -- dramatically reducing the discovery and assessment phase.
- **Databricks partnership:** Sephora is already partnering with Databricks directly to evaluate AI-oriented tools for this migration. They have asked Databricks for recommendations.
