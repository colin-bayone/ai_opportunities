## Section 2: Analysis

### Anticipated vs. Surprise Questions

Most of the questions were anticipated. The validation methodology questions (Burhan asking "what are you validating against," Gariashi asking about source vs. target schema) are standard due-diligence that any technical evaluator would ask when seeing an AI-powered conversion tool for the first time. The security/network isolation question from Burhan was clearly expected -- Colin had the architecture diagram ready and commented that "someone told me I wouldn't need this diagram today, but I'm glad I have it." The portability and ADF questions from Vishal were moderately anticipated; the team had built everything on Azure as a deliberate design decision to preempt exactly this concern. The genuine surprises were: (1) Burhan's request to detect what has already been migrated to Databricks, which surfaced a discovery/inventory use case beyond the conversion pipeline itself; (2) Gariashi's pointed observation that target schemas differ from source schemas (not a lift-and-shift), which forced Colin to articulate the mismatch-handling logic in detail; and (3) Malika's question about whether the LLM would need to read live data for mapping, which pushed the conversation into MCP connector design territory earlier than expected.

### What Questions Reveal About Each Person's Priorities

**Burhan, Maher** asked the most questions (12 of 29) and focused overwhelmingly on operational readiness: deployment topology, security isolation, auto-fix toggles, database options, and scaling infrastructure. His questions reveal someone who is already thinking past "does it work" into "how do I run this in production." He is the technical decision-maker who will own the infrastructure, and his satisfaction with the Azure-only deployment and container flexibility was visibly high. His request for already-migrated detection signals he is managing a complex, partially-completed migration where inventory visibility is a real pain point.

**Sharma, Vishal** asked five questions, all focused on two themes: technical completeness (can the XML parsing catch all DataStage components, does it handle more than XML) and portability/independence (can we use ADF, can we run without your system, what if we stop using the platform). Vishal is clearly the person tasked with ensuring Sephora does not end up locked into a vendor tool, and his questions reveal an organizational concern about long-term maintainability and exit strategy.

**Chakrabarty, Gariashi** asked three questions, each architecturally precise: which schema is being validated, can the agent read from both sides, and the critical observation that target schemas differ from legacy. Gariashi is thinking about the data architecture integrity of the migration -- whether the conversion logic can handle the real-world complexity of a partially-modernized environment where source and target are not 1:1.

**Seth, Malika** asked five questions that reveal deep familiarity with the actual data artifacts. She tracked the specific files that were emailed (the four Databricks table schemas), asked where each file type routes in the pipeline, and pushed on whether automatic mapping would require reading live data. Malika is the hands-on practitioner who will be closest to the day-to-day operation of this tool, and her questions show she is already mentally mapping her current manual workflow onto the automated pipeline.

**Ho, Andrew** asked two questions, both strategic: what does day one actually look like, and can this scale to the full enterprise scope (hundreds of tables, multiple business areas in parallel). Andrew is the executive sponsor validating that this is not a toy demo but a production-viable approach that justifies investment. His satisfaction with the scaling answer and his immediate pivot to "we'll discuss and figure out next steps" confirms he was the decision gate for this meeting.

### Questions That Were NOT Asked

Several expected questions were absent. No one asked about **cost modeling** -- neither the Azure compute costs for running the pipeline at scale, nor the LLM token costs per conversion job, nor the overall ROI comparison against manual migration. No one asked about **error rates or accuracy metrics** in detail; Colin volunteered the 97%+ accuracy figure, but no one pressed on how that was measured, what the remaining errors looked like, or how accuracy degrades on more complex jobs. No one asked about **Cognos report testing** specifically -- whether the converted SQL would be validated against actual Cognos report output, though Colin proactively noted this would require MCP access. No one asked about **version control or rollback** beyond Colin's mention of a "mini git" -- in a migration of this scale, rollback strategy is typically a major concern. No one asked about **timeline estimates** for the full migration (how many reports per week, total elapsed time). Finally, no one asked about **model selection or model updates** -- which specific LLM is being used, what happens when models are updated, or whether they could swap in a different model. The absence of cost and timeline questions suggests this meeting was purely a technical validation gate, with commercial discussions reserved for a separate track (likely involving Mani, whom Colin referenced).
