Problem Summary

As quality engineering organizations mature and integrate AI across the software development lifecycle, visual quality assurance consistently emerges as one of the most difficult capabilities to automate effectively. The following represents BayOne's understanding based on conversations to date and will be refined through continued discussion.

The organization has adopted an AI-first mandate, is transitioning to the Agentic Micro Pod model (lean teams delivering small, frequent enhancements in continuous cycles), and has a mid-2026 target for addressing the visual QE gap. Four teams have been identified as consumers of this capability, each with overlapping but distinct requirements.

Consumer Group 1

Quality Engineering

Broadest scope: exploratory testing, visual regression, localization and translation verification, accessibility compliance, and content validation. The team has completed Playwright proof-of-concept work and is actively migrating from Selenium. The gap is in the automation infrastructure needed to execute visual QE at the pace the AMP model demands.

Consumer Group 2

Development Teams

Shift-left visual validation during development: comparing in-progress work against Figma design specifications to catch design drift before formal QE review. Integration point is CI/CD, triggered naturally on code changes.

Consumer Group 3

UI/UX Team

Governance-oriented: configurable pixel-level differential thresholds, baseline component standards, and approval routing workflows when deviations exceed tolerances. Defines and enforces the visual standards the organization holds itself to.

Consumer Group 4

Producers and Digital Content

Content publishing validation for product items, static content, images, and promotional materials. The current process is entirely manual in staging with minimal production validation. Strong candidate for demonstrating early platform value.

Common requirements: All teams need coverage across two distinct environments: nonprod preview (pre-release validation, including A/B test variant handling) and production (post-release anomaly detection without interfering with the live experience). Cross-device and cross-browser testing is required across the board. Localization testing applies to multiple consumer groups as a configurable dimension. All new tooling must support MCP server integration consistent with existing organizational requirements.

02

Proposed Architecture

A single ecosystem that is deterministic at its foundation, agentic where judgment is required, and configurable across all four consumer groups. Designed for long-term ownership and personalization, built from modular components on a standard, battle-tested technology stack.

The deterministic foundation (Layers 1 and 2) handles everything that can be done reliably without AI: browser automation through Playwright, deterministic playbook execution, CI/CD-triggered test runs, and codebase-aware test targeting based on recursive dependency mapping. AI (Layer 3) is layered on top for work that requires judgment: exploring new features, distinguishing intentional UI changes from defects, and discovering new testing workflows. Validated discoveries become deterministic playbooks, creating a self-improving cycle where the AI explores and the deterministic library grows.

Agents operating in production are restricted to non-destructive actions enforced at the tool access level. Human-in-the-loop is a design feature: confidence scoring builds from human-validated track records, and human involvement decreases naturally as trust is earned through repeated validated successes.

All four consumer groups use the same layered architecture. What varies is configuration: baseline reference (prior state, Figma designs, component standards, or expected content), tolerance level (pixel-precise to broad), and workflow integration (reports, CI/CD feedback, approval routing, or publishing validation). Tool connectors via MCP (Figma, Canva, WordPress, and others) extend the platform without modifying the core.

Engagement Models

BayOne offers three engagement models. The first two are structured around outcomes, milestones, and deliverables.

Recommended

Fully Managed Delivery

BayOne receives a defined scope and delivers end-to-end. The team works closely with stakeholders for requirements, feedback, and validation, but BayOne owns execution, methodology, and quality. Pricing is tied to deliverables and milestones.

Recommended

Variable Collaborative Delivery

BayOne leads while the internal team participates at a level that matches organizational goals: reducing cost through shared effort, building capability by working alongside BayOne, or accelerating delivery. The split is flexible and pricing adjusts accordingly. Well-suited to the structure discussed with the QE Center of Excellence: BayOne builds while the COE team shadows, provides governance and integration input, and develops expertise to own the platform after the engagement concludes.

Staff Augmentation

BayOne provides qualified personnel under the organization's direction. Available if staffing support is needed for other initiatives. Not recommended for this engagement.

Next Steps

This document represents BayOne's initial thinking based on conversations to date. These ideas are a starting point for discussion, not a specification, and will be refined through deeper discovery.

Implementation Pathway

The platform architecture supports a progressive buildout: start with one consumer group, validate the approach, and expand to the remaining three on the same backbone. The content production team is a natural starting point if the goal is rapid validation with contained scope. For the broadest organizational value, the QE or UI/UX consumer group provides a foundation that all teams benefit from. A firm sequencing recommendation requires a deeper requirements discussion with the team.

Requirements for Refinement

  • A working session with the QE Center of Excellence team to understand existing capabilities and integration requirements
  • Specific requirements for each consumer group, including current workflows and priority use cases
  • Access to representative environments, including A/B test variant behavior in the nonprod preview
  • Preferences on engagement model and sequencing that align with the team's capacity and priorities

BayOne is prepared to move into a detailed requirements session at the team's convenience. The platform described in this document is designed as a long-term asset that grows with the organization, and a companion technical detail document is available for deeper review of the architecture, production infrastructure, and implementation specifics.
