## Three Engagement Models Presented by Colin

Colin presented three standard engagement models that BayOne typically offers, explaining the trade-offs of each. This happened at approximately [11:54]-[12:31] in the transcript.

### Model 1: 100% BayOne-Owned Effort

Colin described this as: "you give us something, you know, we have effectively the requirements. We're always working kind of hand in hand with you, but effectively 100% effort on our team." [11:54]

- BayOne receives the requirements and owns execution entirely.
- Client works "hand in hand" with BayOne but does not carry execution burden.
- No explicit knowledge transfer component described for this model.

### Model 2: 70/30 Knowledge Transfer Model

Colin described this as: "a 7030. That can work well if there's a desire for there to be kind of like a knowledge transfer to the team. The existing team kind of gets upskilled at that same time." [12:02]

- 70% BayOne effort, 30% client team involvement.
- Existing team gets upskilled simultaneously during the engagement.
- Colin noted: "That can be better for costing, but that also then requires some involvement from the team at a higher level than the first." [12:12]
- Trade-off is explicit: lower cost but higher client team time commitment.

### Model 3: Pure Staffing (Recommended Against)

Colin described this as: "We give you good people. And you know you own it." [12:22]

- BayOne provides personnel, client owns execution entirely.
- Colin proactively recommended against this: "I never recommend that 'cause that always ends up being less than desired." [12:31]
- Colin stated this option "sounds like it's totally off the off the board for this, which I would recommend to keep off the board." [12:22]
- He preemptively took this off the table before Vaibhav could respond to it.

## Vaibhav's Chosen Model: Hybrid of 1 and 2

Vaibhav responded immediately with his preference, indicating he had already discussed this with Deepika prior to the call: "My thought process and I discussed this with Deepika as well. Of course, I'll be leaning towards between 1 and 2 where you own it, but under Deepika's team shadow." [12:46]

### Specific Elements of the Hybrid Model

Vaibhav defined five distinct elements for how the engagement should work:

1. **BayOne owns execution** -- "you own it" [12:46]
2. **Deepika's team shadows** -- "under Deepika's team shadow, so they know what's being done" [12:46]
3. **Training embedded in the build process** -- "They are being trained as things are being built" [12:59]
4. **Governance and integration input** -- "they can provide their input and some sort of a governance, you know on that tool, how it integrates with other tools that we already have in place" [12:59]
5. **Long-term maintenance handoff** -- "in the long run the maintenance and updates are fed, then obviously Deepika or future resources may deal with it" [13:14]

Colin's response: "Beautiful." [13:18] -- indicating full alignment with the hybrid model.

### Key Implication: Knowledge Transfer Is Embedded, Not a Separate Phase

The model Vaibhav described does not include a dedicated knowledge transfer phase. Instead, knowledge transfer occurs organically through shadowing and governance involvement during the build itself. The team "observes" and "gets trained as things are being built" rather than receiving a handoff or training block at the end.

## Proposal Format: Four Separate Plus Consolidated Option

### Colin's Proposal Structure Options

At [21:42], Colin raised the question of proposal structure directly: "For costing, I could do this as one big proposal for you or I could chunk it up into four just for the sake of budget. That might be easier even for timeline."

He also asked: "Do you want them to be run in parallel? Do you want it as one big thing? How would you prefer that?" [21:42]

When Vaibhav asked for clarification ("Go again, Colin, run parallel. I mean what different different phases or?" [22:04]), Colin clarified: "I could do it as one big project, one big includes all the full scope of everything. Or I could do it as four separate proposals for each of those high areas with not separate costing. It wouldn't be more more priced for that, but just so it's more digestible in case that helps you for budget purposes." [22:10]

### Key Detail: No Price Premium for Separation

Colin explicitly stated the four-proposal approach "wouldn't be more priced for that" -- the separation is purely for budget digestibility, not a cost increase. [22:10]

### Vaibhav's Response

Vaibhav agreed the four-proposal format gives more flexibility: "Yeah, I mean, that will give me more flexibility, yes." [22:26]

### Colin's Counter-Offer: Both Formats Available

Colin then offered to produce both: "OK, then I'll do it that way. And then if you say to me, Colin, I want the one master one, I can do that too. Power of AI, right? So we can get that to you, no problem." [22:30]

Vaibhav accepted both: "Well, if we can just do it. Instantly, then yeah, both will serve the purpose." [22:38]

### Final Agreement

The agreed approach is:
- Four separate proposals, one per consumer group (QE testing, development teams, UIUX team, content/digital production)
- Plus the option of a consolidated master proposal if needed
- Both formats to be provided
