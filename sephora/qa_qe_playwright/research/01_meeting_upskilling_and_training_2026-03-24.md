## 1. BayOne Internal Training Philosophy: "Everyone's an AI Engineer"

Colin describes a deliberate internal policy at BayOne: every new hire, regardless of their original specialty, receives AI engineering training.

**Key statement (Colin):** "Even on my own internal team, everyone's an AI engineer. Two of my web developers are agentic AI specialists that now are Django people, for instance, because it's very easy for them. And I can teach them what they need to know about web dev. And then they can do this very quickly."

The logic is bidirectional: Colin can teach web development fundamentals, while the developers absorb agentic AI skills. The combination produces people who can iterate fast across both domains. The prerequisite Colin identifies is not prior AI experience but willingness to learn quickly: "As long as they can iterate fast and are open to learning, it's a very good system."

**Concrete example:** Two web developers on Colin's team have been cross-trained into Django/AI roles. They are now described as "agentic AI specialists." This is presented as an already-accomplished transformation, not a future plan.

**Commitment for all new hires (Colin):** "One thing that will be coming for all the new hires is upskilling training that'll come from us. So even if their role has nothing to do with AI, they're going to come in with those skills from the start. That's from anyone that comes from BayOne from a staffing perspective."

This is framed as an imminent rollout ("will be coming"), applying universally to all BayOne staffing placements regardless of the role they are being placed into.

## 3. The Incubation Model

Colin proposes an alternative to traditional contract-to-hire that he calls "incubation."

**Key statement (Colin):** "What if instead of like a contract to hire situation? Because that's good. That still remains. What if it is a we incubate the person, they're working on your project for you, we incubate them while they're with us, we're teaching them what they need to know. They're still working. They're still getting everything done. But at the same time, they're learning and they're getting the tools and upskilling that they might need."

**How the model works:**
- The person is placed on the client's project and is productive from day one.
- Simultaneously, BayOne provides ongoing training and development.
- The person remains on BayOne's payroll during this period.
- After incubation, the person transitions to the client.

**Risk mitigation angle (Colin):** "In some cases, maybe they already have that, but anything can be put on a resume. Anything can happen in an interview, even for me, you know, I could miss something. So it gives us a chance to work with that person and really see how they are in the wild, I like to say, before we push off to them."

This positions incubation as a de-risking mechanism. BayOne absorbs the uncertainty of whether a candidate can truly perform, observing them "in the wild" before transitioning them to the client. The implicit value proposition: the client gets a pre-validated, upskilled person rather than a resume-based gamble.

**Relationship to contract-to-hire:** Colin is careful not to position this as a replacement: "Because that's good. That still remains." It is presented as an additional option, not a substitute.

## 5. Vaibhav's Upskilling Concern: Offshore vs. Nearshore Speed Gap

Vaibhav raises a specific concern about differential upskilling velocity across geographies.

**Key statement (Vaibhav):** "There are two things, two facts, that is very true today. [Offshore talent is] not upskilling at the same speed as the nearshore."

(Note: The speech-to-text renders this somewhat garbled, but the meaning is clear from context. Vaibhav is stating that offshore teams are falling behind nearshore/onshore teams in adopting AI skills.)

**Geographic implications Vaibhav draws from this:**
- Current state: "Majority of our workforce offshore" -- so this is a problem affecting the bulk of Sephora's QE team.
- Near-term prediction (6 months to 1 year): "A lot more positions will probably move nearshore and on-site, nearshore to start with."
- Specific geographies mentioned as nearshore targets: South America, Canada.
- Potential conversion ratio: "Maybe looking at converting to work, we offshore to one nearshore to one on-site, but bringing in presence more this side of the world."

**Driving factor:** Vaibhav ties this to the AMP (Agentic Micro Pod) model requiring co-location: "The way it's structured, we will need people to work together as two or three member people. It will literally be locked in a room for the entire day. They have to be co-located."

This creates a direct business case for BayOne's nearshore pipeline. Vaibhav is signaling that Sephora's geographic mix will shift toward nearshore, and the upskilling gap is one of the drivers.

**Open question:** Whether BayOne's upskilling/training offering could help bridge the offshore gap and slow or reverse this geographic shift is not explicitly discussed. It sits as an implicit opportunity -- if BayOne could upskill offshore talent to nearshore-equivalent speed, that would preserve the cost advantage of offshore while closing the capability gap.

## 6. Cutting-Edge Advisory: BayOne as Noise Filter

Colin positions BayOne as a vetting layer between the fast-moving AI tool landscape and the client's need for stability.

**Key statement (Colin):** "Staying on the cutting edge is hard and it's even harder to say, like, what is real and what is just hype."

**The open-source example (Colin):** "Like, open-cloth [open-source]. Great example. It fluctuates on a daily. Is this the biggest security risk known to mankind? Is this the next thing? Is NVIDIA sponsoring it now? What's happening? In reality, it doesn't really matter unless there's a business outcome tied to it."

(Note: "open-cloth" is a speech-to-text artifact for "open-source.")

**The advisory value proposition (Colin):** "The good thing is we can help with that, because we have to stay on that cutting edge a bit. We can still share that knowledge across, still keep people up to date. So if there's new tools, we can vet them first. And once we know especially what's going on, we can say, ah, this is going to be perfect for them. We can try it out."

The model described:
1. BayOne stays on the cutting edge as a requirement of its own practice.
2. BayOne vets new tools internally first.
3. Only tools that pass BayOne's maturity threshold get recommended to clients.
4. Knowledge sharing happens proactively ("we can still share that knowledge across").

## 7. Tool Maturity Threshold

Colin articulates a specific philosophy about when to adopt new tools.

**Key statement (Colin):** "There's a certain, even for us internally, there's a certain threshold that must be met for maturity of tool to be able to use it. I'm not a version 0.3 type. 1.0, I need this to have crossed its path into this."

This establishes a concrete decision rule: tools must have reached at least version 1.0 maturity before Colin will adopt them for production use. The "0.3" reference is to early/beta releases that may be technically interesting but operationally unreliable.

This threshold serves dual purposes:
- Internal discipline: prevents BayOne from chasing every new release.
- Client trust: positions BayOne's recommendations as vetted and production-ready, not bleeding-edge experiments.

## 8. Conference Culture Critique and the "Manic State"

Colin describes a pattern he observes at industry conferences that his advisory role helps counteract.

**Key statement (Colin):** "Usually when I go to conferences, I see people in this almost manic state, they'll be like, should I move now? Or what if something happens tomorrow, should I move then?"

He characterizes this as anxiety-driven decision-making where organizations feel pressure to adopt every new tool or paradigm immediately. The "manic state" language is vivid and specific -- it describes FOMO-driven technology adoption.

**Colin's counter-philosophy:** The antidote is outcome-driven thinking, not mechanism-driven thinking.

## 9. "Don't Let the Mechanism Drive the Outcome"

This is arguably the most quotable single line from the entire conversation, and Colin delivers it in the context of both the confidence/scoring discussion and the broader advisory positioning.

**Key statement (Colin):** "Don't let the mechanism of the outcome drive what you want the outcome to be."

The full context: "So is your goal to get to 100% agentic QA/QE? No. Your goal is to get to the velocity and the quality that you want. The rest is just secondary. If there was a magic rock that fell from space and said, oh, plug this into the process and everything works perfectly, it wouldn't even care about AI anymore."

This statement does double duty:
1. **For Vaibhav's immediate situation:** Don't chase "100% agentic" as a goal. Chase velocity and quality. If agents get you there, great. If something else does, also great.
2. **For BayOne's advisory positioning:** BayOne is outcome-focused, not technology-focused. They are not selling AI for the sake of AI. They are selling business results that happen to leverage AI.

The "magic rock" metaphor underscores the point with humor -- if a non-AI solution achieved the same outcome, the mechanism would be irrelevant.

## 10. Screening and Hiring Philosophy (Related to Training Value)

Both Colin and Vaibhav discuss hiring philosophy in ways that reinforce the value of BayOne's training/incubation model.

**Colin on screening (Colin):** "I have the mindset I don't hire the resume. You can put anything on a resume. I've interviewed people from some way better schools than I went to and haven't picked them. And the reason why? You can tell."

**Colin on his screening process:** "They've got to survive an hour with me on a call." He describes it as "the last filter" and notes it is deliberately different from standard technical screening (HackerRank-style tests).

**Vaibhav's alignment (Vaibhav):** "I always put them on the whiteboard. Simple problem. It's actually nothing related to Java or anything. I'm not testing that. This is a common problem. Tell me what you're thinking. That's it... I just want your mental attitude, attitude. You are relating to your problem. That's all."

Both leaders value problem-solving mindset over specific technical credentials. This directly supports the incubation model: if the right mindset matters more than the right resume, then BayOne's ability to teach technical skills to people with the right mindset is a genuine differentiator.

**Vaibhav on resume quality in the AI era:** "Anybody can create a catchy potato" (speech-to-text artifact, likely "catchy portfolio" or "catchy resume"). "A lot of these resumes, we've just started conversing and said, yeah, thank you. You can tell."

This implies AI-generated or heavily embellished resumes are a growing problem, making BayOne's human-judgment-based screening process more valuable.

## 13. Sephora's Internal Upskilling Context

Vaibhav provides important context about how Sephora is already handling upskilling, which frames what BayOne's training would complement rather than replace.

**Key statements (Vaibhav):**
- The QE function has evolved from pure testing to a quality umbrella covering "quality of the stories that are created, quality of the code, not the bugs and all, but just the quality, testing, quality of testing, and quality of deployment."
- His leads are being repositioned: "All our leads are now getting converted into not an engineer, but a decision-maker. That's what I'm telling all my leads now, because everybody's worried about the job. Agents are doing everything, what are we doing? Hey, you wanted to be a manager, right? Now you have a team of agents."
- He acknowledges the psychological dimension: people are worried about job displacement.

**Colin's complementary framing:** "You almost transition from a sous chef or a line chef to the master executive chef for the kitchen. You're walking around tasting everyone else's stuff. You're not actually cooking yourself. That's a good place to be."

This metaphor (sous chef to executive chef) could be powerful in training materials. It reframes the transition from "losing your job to AI" to "getting promoted to manage AI."
