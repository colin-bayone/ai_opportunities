Refresher: What You Told Vaibhav (March 24)

Quick recap of your positioning, methodology, and where you two aligned.

Your Core Methodology

  • Deterministic-first, AI-last. Minimize the surface area where AI variability can compound. You target 97%+, not 80%.
  • State graph mapping. Autonomous process that deterministically explores the codebase and produces a dependency map — modules, CVEs, libraries, dead code. This is always step one, even outside QA/QE.
  • CI/CD integration. State graph plugs into pipelines so file changes map to specific tests that need to run. Change-aware, dependency-informed test targeting — not running the full suite every time.
  • Coverage = recursive downstream dependencies. A unit test that only covers branches within a function but ignores what it feeds into is incomplete.
  • You do this with two other people. You emphasized the leverage — small team, big output.

Playwright

  • You called Selenium-to-Playwright "night and day." Playwright eliminates the delay/wait problems, works much better with AI.
  • Playwright is Microsoft-native — perfect fit for their Azure environment. Visual understanding tools within Azure, everything hosted in one place. IT and cybersecurity happy because no separate tool.
  • Handles mobile testing, password-protected flows, ADA compliance testing.
  • Writing Playwright tests is "the easiest part by far" — you have an agent that writes playbooks for everyone else to run.

Agent Evaluation & Trust (Where He Was Most Engaged)

  • Evaluate agents like people. Same way you assess whether you trust a person to do a task — that's how you assess agents.
  • Never let agents self-assess or peer-assess. They oscillate between "everything's perfect" and "everything's awful." Use human-defined KPIs instead.
  • Confidence = reinforcement from human feedback. Agent does task, human approves/rejects. Repeated approvals build a pattern library. Threshold for human-in-the-loop decreases over time. Human re-enters only on mistakes.
  • The reliability score analogy. You used the example of a person who promises Wednesday delivery but always delivers the following week — what score would you give them? Same question for agents.
  • Proportional response to mistakes. 99% perfect, one mistake — don't throw it out. What would you do with a person? Pull them aside, give better instructions. Same for agents. Always iterative.
  • Humans must stay for course correction. Agents "tend to simplify" and "tend to go off-road" when self-correcting. Human expertise is structurally necessary.
  • Chef analogy. You transition from line cook to executive chef — walking around tasting everyone else's work, not cooking yourself.

Multi-Model Strategy

  • Claude for ~90% of tasks. Gemini for visual understanding (better at it than Claude). Very little OpenAI.
  • Agents checking agents — visual understanding catches things like button repositioning that would break brittle Selenium tests.

Observability & Transparency

  • You stressed this as commonly missed. "How are my agents doing?"
  • Traceability matters especially for testing — if an error occurs and should have been caught upstream, why didn't it?
  • Mature systems produce the most interesting edge case analysis — is it being too strict? Are we fundamentally wrong about something?
  • "System autonomy" not "autonomous systems." The autonomy is a property of the design, not of any individual agent.

Key Philosophy Statements You Made

  • "Don't let the mechanism of the outcome drive what you want the outcome to be." Your goal isn't 100% agentic QA/QE — it's the velocity and quality you want.
  • "If there was a magic rock that fell from space and everything works perfectly, you wouldn't even care about AI anymore."
  • Framework quality determines the ceiling, not model quality. If you see a cap forming, it's the framework, not the models.
  • No out-of-the-box solution from anyone (you, OpenAI, Anthropic) is 100% ready. The trick is to evaluate agents like people.

Training & Upskilling (You Pitched This)

  • All BayOne new hires get AI upskilling training, regardless of role.
  • Incubation model: person works on client project while BayOne trains them simultaneously. De-risks the hire — you see them "in the wild" before transition.
  • External team upskilling as a service offering (demand-driven, already had requests).
  • BayOne as noise filter — you vet tools at the cutting edge so clients don't have to chase every new release. Version 1.0 minimum maturity threshold.

Where Vaibhav Aligned With You

  • Trust scoring. His biggest unsolved problem. He wants a composite trust index but admitted "How do you compute 4.8? I don't know." Your reinforcement approach directly answers this.
  • Deterministic-first. He was visibly engaged with this philosophy.
  • Agent evaluation like people. He validated the screening parallel — "To be able to screen that mindset, you need a like-minded person."
  • Human-in-the-loop necessity. He described trust progression from 40-50% to 60-80% as needing thorough human review. Called it "a big hurdle we have to cross together."
  • Business outcomes over buzzwords. His North Star is "don't be the bottleneck" — velocity and quality, not agentic-for-the-sake-of-agentic.
