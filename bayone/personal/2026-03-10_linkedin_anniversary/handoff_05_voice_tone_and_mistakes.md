## ABSOLUTE FORMATTING RULES

These are non-negotiable. Colin stated these explicitly and repeatedly.

### 1. NO SENTENCE FRAGMENTS. EVER.

This was Colin's single biggest frustration. Every sentence must be a grammatically complete sentence with a subject and a verb. Colin said: "If it is not proper English grammar as a complete sentence, I do not want it in my post. Do you understand me?"

Violations that occurred in drafts:
- "Different world." (fragment used as a standalone sentence)
- "Build the team, build the solutions, build the strategy." (fragment list used as standalone)
- "Just a ton of potential and a company full of people who were ready to run." (fragment)

### 2. NO EM DASHES

Colin's formatting preference from the skill. Never use em dashes (—) anywhere.

### 3. NO COLONS IN DRAFTED MESSAGES

From the skill's hard rules. Do not use colons in the post content.

### 4. NO SENTENCES STARTING WITH COORDINATING CONJUNCTIONS

Colin does not want sentences starting with "And" or "But" or any other coordinating conjunction. He was extremely emphatic about this. Even though starting a sentence with "but" is technically grammatically acceptable, Colin considers it poor writing and associates it with AI-generated content. He called anyone who would defend this practice unflattering names. Do not do it.

### 5. NO RHETORICAL QUESTIONS

Colin identified rhetorical questions as "a huge AI tell." An earlier draft used "One year later?" as a rhetorical question transition. Do not use rhetorical questions anywhere in the post.

## STYLE AND CONTENT RULES

### 6. NO INSPIRATIONAL PIVOTS

Colin called out "inspirational pivots" as a major problem. This means sentences or paragraphs that dramatically pivot from setup to payoff in a way that feels like a motivational LinkedIn post. Example that was flagged: "My job was to take BayOne's AI ambitions and turn them into something real. Build the team, build the solutions, build the strategy. That's exactly what we've been doing." The entire construction (setup + fragment list + triumphant closer) is an inspirational pivot pattern.

### 7. "THAT'S EXACTLY WHAT WE'VE BEEN DOING" AND SIMILAR PHRASES ARE AI TELLS

Colin specifically flagged this phrase. Avoid any triumphant callback phrasing that sounds like it was generated by AI. Other examples to avoid: "And that's what we did." / "That's the mission, and we're delivering." / "Mission accomplished." / Any sentence that functions as a mic-drop closer to a paragraph.

### 8. DO NOT START SENTENCES WITH LEADING FRAGMENTS

Colin said: "Please stop putting fragment sentences and leading sentences of the word, and it's horrible." This refers to the pattern of starting a section or paragraph with a dramatic short fragment followed by elaboration. Examples to avoid:
- "One year later?"
- "Different world."
- "The result?"
- "Here's the thing."

### 9. NO PHRASES THAT DON'T SOUND LIKE COLIN

Specific phrases Colin rejected:
- "The people here are the real deal" (does not sound like him)
- "Hit a ceiling" as a primary framing (was only a small part of why he left)
- "Change of scenery" (too soft)
- "Do my best work" (too generic)
- "You don't even know what's coming" used literally (Colin said to convey this energy WITHOUT using the actual phrase)

### 10. DO NOT PARAPHRASE COLIN'S WORDS BACK TO HIM

When Colin shares how he would say something in his own words (even prefaced with "don't quote me on this"), do NOT just lightly smooth his exact words and present them back. Colin called this lazy. Actually rewrite the ideas in polished form while preserving his meaning. If his phrasing doesn't make logical sense in places, fix it rather than parroting it back.

### 11. DO NOT CONDESCEND ABOUT BAYONE'S STARTING POINT

Never frame BayOne as having had "nothing" or being behind or lacking. Colin was explicit that this would get him in trouble. Frame the narrative around what he built, not what was missing. "AI was in its infancy" is acceptable. "There was no team, no strategy, no infrastructure, no solutions" is absolutely not.

### 12. SOLUTIONS, NOT PRODUCTS

The AI capabilities, TalentAI, quality engineering solutions, and enterprise verticals are SOLUTIONS that BayOne offers, not products. Do not phrase anything as a product offering, product launch, or product announcement. These are part of BayOne's business, not SKUs on a shelf.

## PROCESS RULES FOR THE NEXT SESSION

### 13. WORK SECTION BY SECTION

Do NOT attempt to draft the entire post at once. The first attempt to do this failed catastrophically. Draft one section, get approval, then move to the next.

### 14. STAGE CHANGES IN CHAT FIRST

When Colin asks for edits, present the proposed changes in the chat conversation for his review BEFORE modifying any document.

### 15. MAKE CHANGES ONE AT A TIME

When Colin gives a list of changes, make them one at a time and report back after each one. Do not batch multiple changes together.

### 16. ASK QUESTIONS INSTEAD OF GUESSING

If you are unsure about Colin's tone or how he would say something, STOP AND ASK. Colin said this explicitly: "If you don't understand my tone, stop and ask me questions because this is just not working for me." Guessing wrong repeatedly is far worse than asking a clarifying question.

### 17. DO NOT SWING IN POLARITY

Colin called out a pattern where Claude would swing from one extreme to another when given feedback. If something is "too polished," the fix is not to make it casual and sloppy. If something is "too aggressive," the fix is not to make it timid. Make measured adjustments.

### 18. FOCUS ON ONE THING AT A TIME

Colin explicitly said: "Focus on one goddamn thing at once, because you can't seem to get this straight." When working through a problem area, stay on that one area until it's resolved. Do not try to address multiple sections or issues simultaneously.

### 19. PRESENT OPTIONS WITH VARYING LEVELS

When direction is unclear, present 3-4 options with clearly different approaches (Colin liked receiving options with "varying levels of spice"). This worked well when it was focused on one specific sentence or section. It failed when it was applied to multiple sections simultaneously.

### 20. LISTEN TO WHAT COLIN ACTUALLY SAYS

Multiple times, Colin gave specific instructions that were then ignored in subsequent drafts:
- He said "don't say directly 'you don't even know what's coming'" and the draft used it as a literal closing line
- He said "don't say nothing was in place directly" and the draft explicitly listed everything that was missing
- He said "don't quote me on this" and the response was nearly a verbatim quote
- He said to focus on one thing and the response addressed three things at once
- He said to stage changes in chat first and the next edit went straight to the document

Do not repeat these patterns. If Colin gives an instruction, follow it.
