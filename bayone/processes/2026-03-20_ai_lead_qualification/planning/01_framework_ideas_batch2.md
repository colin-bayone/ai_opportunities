## Idea 8: Scope Lock and Anti-Bait-and-Switch Mechanism

**What:** Once a meeting scope is agreed upon internally, sales cannot unilaterally change it with the client. If the scope changes after internal alignment, the meeting must be rescheduled or reconvened.

**The specific pattern this addresses:** The Tailored Brands incident where leadership had a positive internal discussion setting the tone, and then almost immediately Rima told the customer they'd be presenting something completely different. The internal agreement meant nothing because sales changed the scope externally without checking back.

**The mechanism:**
- Meeting scope is locked at the time it's scheduled (part of the calendar/Salesforce record)
- If sales changes the scope with the client after internal alignment, the meeting gets rescheduled
- Sales must know that they cannot bait-and-switch: agree to one thing internally, promise another thing externally
- Expectations must be clearly communicated both internally and to the customer

**Why this matters:** The technical team preps based on what was agreed. If the scope shifts after they prep, their preparation is wasted, and they walk into a meeting they're not ready for. That makes BayOne look disorganized to the client.

## Idea 9: The Venn Diagram Approach (Project Overlap as Flywheel)

**What:** When evaluating new opportunities, assess how much overlap exists with work BayOne has already completed. Related projects have a lower barrier to entry because they result in less work for the team and higher value output.

**How it affects qualification:**
- High overlap with existing work = lower qualification bar (less effort required, more confidence in delivery, faster turnaround)
- Low overlap = higher qualification bar (more risk, more effort, needs stronger justification)
- No overlap = needs exceptional justification (strategic value, significant revenue, clear path to capability building)

**The flywheel effect:** By prioritizing related work, BayOne builds momentum. Each completed project makes the next related one easier, creating a snowball of capability, speed, and credibility. This should be actively rewarded and pursued.

**Overlap must be based on real solutions work, not staffing history.** (See Idea 9B below.)

## Idea 9B: Staffing vs. Solutions Distinction (Separate from Venn Diagram)

**What:** Confront the staffing mindset and fabricated case study problem directly. Staffing engagements are fundamentally different from solutions engagements, and they must not be misrepresented.

**The distinction:**
- **Staffing engagement:** BayOne hired someone with a predefined role at a predefined company to do a predefined task managed by the client
- **Solutions engagement:** BayOne designed, built, and delivered a solution

**The problem:** People misrepresent staffing as solutions ("we deployed a solution and managed it") when the reality is they placed a person. Case studies and capability claims built on this misrepresentation are fabricated and will damage credibility when a client digs in.

**The rule:** "Have we done this before?" must be answered strictly within a curated solutions portfolio. Not wishful thinking. Not overrepresentation. Not overextrapolation of a staffing engagement.

## Idea 10: Capacity Planning and Steering Committee

### The Problem
Everything is happening in a vacuum. There's no collective view of the pipeline, no prioritization, and no resource allocation planning. Sales throws things at the technical team ad hoc.

### Steering Committee (Weekly or Biweekly)
**Purpose:** Review the current queue of projects, new pipeline items, priority ordering, and team resource allocation.

**Must include (no more than 5 people total to avoid coordination overhead):**
- Leadership representative
- Solutions/delivery representative (Colin or equivalent)
- Sales representative (VP of Sales level)
- CTO (Priya) as recommended steering committee lead

**What gets discussed:**
- Current project queue and status
- New items that have entered the pipeline
- Where new items fall in priority order
- Team resource count and allocation
- Capacity constraints and trade-offs

**Must have a firm owner.** Must be someone with decision-making power and organizational authority. **Recommended: Priya (CTO)** as the steering committee lead.

### Weekly Stand-up (Sales + Solutions + Delivery)
**Purpose:** Quick operational meeting to look at what's coming down the pipeline for the week and address items that need attention.

**Characteristics:**
- Quick meeting, not a marathon
- Must happen regularly (weekly)
- Covers near-term pipeline, not strategic planning
- Gets things prioritized before they become emergencies
- Stops things from happening in a vacuum

## Idea 11: AI Sales Certification / Learning and Development Requirement

**What:** Salespeople must complete internal training before they are authorized to sell AI solutions. Not optional, not a suggestion. A requirement.

**What this is:**
- An internal training or L&D effort with real criteria
- An evaluation that salespeople pass to demonstrate competence
- Something that shows they are truly learning and growing
- Objective, not subjective (avoids any appearance of favoritism)

**What this is NOT:**
- Something subjective where it looks like favoritism
- A one-time checkbox that gets forgotten

**Verification that training stuck:**
- The training itself is straightforward
- There should be some kind of formal evaluation at the end to confirm learning
- Exact format needs more thought (could sound bad if not designed carefully)
- Must be objective and fair, not gatekeeping based on personality or politics

**The logic:** You cannot sell what you don't understand. If someone can't fundamentally do qualification, they cannot be selling AI products. BayOne can enable and support the learning, but it must happen.

**Two paths forward (not mutually exclusive):**
1. Train existing sales team (L&D effort, get everyone to baseline, then differentiate based on adoption)
2. Hire people who already have it (the SEAL Team 6 approach with specialized AI sales hires)

**Without one of these paths, there is no path forward.** The current state where salespeople have zero AI literacy and offload everything to Colin is not sustainable.

## Idea 12: Outcome-Based KPIs for Sales (Value Over Volume)

**What:** Measure salespeople not just on quantity of activity (meetings, positions filled, MSAs signed) but on the actual business value created across customers.

**The shift:** Relationship-focused to outcome-focused.

**What doesn't count as value:**
- 30 meetings that produced nothing
- 20 new staffing positions with no strategic growth
- "I have a really good lunch talk with this guy"

**What counts as value:**
- Revenue generated from qualified, delivered engagements
- Conversion rate from lead to actual project
- Quality of opportunities brought (did they survive qualification?)
- Growth within existing accounts (not just new logos)

**The question:** What business value is it bringing back to BayOne? That is the only question that matters.

**Why this matters for the framework:** If KPIs reward volume (meetings held, MSAs signed, accounts claimed), the incentive structure drives exactly the behavior the framework is trying to fix. The KPIs must align with the framework's goals: fewer, better-qualified opportunities that actually convert to revenue.
