# Discussion: Colin & Anand (External Sales Consultant) - Mar 2026

## Context
Anand is an external consultant (Melahimit Group) hired to run a 3-day sales workshop for BayOne's sales team the following week. He has 35 years of sales experience, 14 years in consulting/training. He previously worked with Surej at IntelliSwift. This was a pre-workshop alignment call where Colin gave Anand the unfiltered picture of what's happening.

**Note:** Colin was politely receptive to Anand's tool/automation ideas on the call but has no actual interest in helping Anand with his tools. The valuable content here is the problem diagnosis, not the tool discussion.

---

## Colin's Three Core Problems (As Articulated to Anand)

Colin distilled everything into three problems for an outsider. This is the cleanest framing he's given yet.

### Problem 1: People Don't Know What We're Selling
- Sales has no idea what BayOne actually does on the AI side
- They've transitioned to being "just call people" with no substance behind it
- They don't document, record, or take notes
- Every conversation that tangentially mentions AI results in "can you get on the next call?"
- Colin pushes back and says most aren't real opportunities, or they're misattributed

### Problem 2: People Make Stuff Up
- Direct result of not knowing what BayOne does
- **Staffing mindset:** In staffing, if you don't have a capability, you say you do anyway because you can hire for it. That logic doesn't transfer to solutions.
- **The chatbot example:** Someone called Colin three hours before a meeting with a VP-level person at a major client asking if he had a demo for an "integrated chatbot for Walmart that is aware of the customer's card and can give personalized recommendations." Colin: "Do you think I just have that sitting here? What would give you that inclination?"
- **Fabricated case studies origin story:** The process is literally: open ChatGPT, type "I have an upcoming meeting with Walmart, generate me a case study," save it, forget how it was created, later present it as real. Colin confirmed this is exactly what happens.

### Problem 3: No Structure or Gatekeeping
- Very flat organization
- No one filters before things reach Colin
- The boss (Anuj) is always copied but never gate keeps. "Anything that glitters is gold, force it up the food chain."
- Sales gets on a call with Colin, a non-technical person tries to explain a technical thing, Colin has to write his own notes, distill it, and enter a feedback loop
- Sales doesn't take no for an answer (good externally, bad internally)

## New Patterns and Dynamics Revealed

### The "Shiny Thing" Dynamic
Anand confirmed this directly: "You are the shiny little thing for them, which they want to showcase to everybody. Every time I go to a client, I need that shiny thing to show something new."

This reframes the problem. Sales isn't just being lazy about qualification. They're using Colin as a prop to make meetings seem more valuable. His presence = legitimacy for the meeting, regardless of whether it's productive.

**Surej confirmed this independently to Anand:** "The moment they hear AI, they call up, they dial him and say let's get on the call."

### The Kitchen Sink Problem (Fishing Expeditions)
Colin described a pattern where sales builds massive decks for short meetings:
- 40-60 slide decks for 20-minute meetings
- 10 slides about BayOne's company history "which frankly no one cares about"
- Every possible service and capability shown, hoping one hook lands
- Thought process: "We want to look bigger than we are"
- Example: Client meeting about an Oracle contract, sales pitches a learning and development platform in the same deck

**Colin's client-side perspective (from his time at Coherent as a BayOne customer):**
- Too many people on calls adding no value
- Intimidating when 10 people are on camera but 8 are silent with no clear role
- Client feels the vendor doesn't care about them ("we had a meeting about Oracle, why are you pitching me something else?")
- Hastily assembled presentations that make BayOne look bad

### Staffing vs. Solutions Culture Clash
This is a foundational dynamic that explains many of the other patterns:
- BayOne's culture and muscle memory is staffing
- In staffing: if you don't have a capability, say you do and hire for it. Low risk.
- In solutions: if you don't have a capability, saying you do creates commitments you can't meet. High risk.
- The sales team hasn't made this mental transition
- Leadership is still adjusting too. The transition from staffing to solutions is "very rocky, even for leadership"

### Quality Standards Are Missing
- No baseline quality for client-facing materials
- Unformatted Word documents with typos sent to SVPs at major clients
- No company branding standards enforced
- LinkedIn used as an internal platform more than external
- Website not up to date
- No "Gordon Ramsay gatekeeper" for quality before anything goes to a client

### Pod Structure Creates Uneven Capability
- Sales is organized into client-dedicated pods
- Some pods are strong (Cisco pod has good AI qualification and produces real opportunities)
- Other pods are "completely all over the place" with no technical knowledge
- Pod structure means there's no specialized AI sales capability that cuts across clients

### The Self-Cannibalization Risk
Colin raised a nuance specific to BayOne's staffing business: sales shouldn't pitch AI automation as a replacement for the 200-person QE team they've staffed at a client. That's literally pitching the client to fire BayOne's own staffing revenue. Sales needs to understand the difference between "AI can help" and "AI replaces everything we've been billing you for."

## What Anand Is Bringing

### Meeting Planner Tool
- Simple form that takes ~15 minutes to fill out before any meeting
- Forces written documentation of meeting purpose, desired outcomes, context
- Colin's intended use: "Next time someone comes to a meeting, tell them to fill up the meeting planner and send it to me. Your 20-minute story will help, but won't help me plan for the call."
- Creates a natural gate: either they fill it out (and Colin has context) or they stop booking unplanned meetings

### Common Language/Terminology
- BayOne currently has no shared vocabulary for discussing opportunities
- Different people mean different things by the same words
- The tool/process will introduce standard terms

### Role-Based Presentation Approach
- Different presentations for different stakeholders (CEO vs. director vs. finance head)
- Currently BayOne sends the same kitchen-sink deck to everyone regardless of role

## Anand's Honest Assessment

- Change will take **3-4 months minimum** before significant behavioral improvement
- The team has been working this way for 15-20 years and takes pride in it
- "Nobody loves change, and bringing change for adults who are doing well is the hardest"
- The organization is doing well overall, so there's no burning platform to force change. People have a "chip on their shoulder" that their current approach got them here.

## Colin's Key Questions for the Workshop

### Baseline Everyone vs. SEAL Team 6?
Two approaches to building AI sales capability:
1. **Baseline everyone:** Get all salespeople to a minimum AI literacy level with standard talking points
2. **SEAL Team 6:** Train a specialized subset of the sales team on AI topics as pre-sales qualifiers

Colin's view: some salespeople are so non-technical that even with training they'll fall apart the moment a client asks a real question. ("We're talking about my mom-and-dad level... she asked if the internet was open after 9pm.")

Anand's recommendation: Start by treating everyone the same, then within 1-2 months you'll naturally see who the early adopters are vs. who reverts to old behavior. Then differentiate.

### Colin's Automation Warning
"If I get frustrated enough, I will find a way to automate a process. Don't let me get to that point."

He's willing to enable the right process with AI tools, but won't circumvent the process. The trade-off: helping sales with the right tools vs. doing their job for them.

---

## Implications for Framework

1. **The meeting planner is an immediate gate.** Even before the full framework exists, requiring a filled-out meeting planner before Colin joins any call creates a documentation gate. This aligns with Anand's workshop.

2. **Staffing vs. solutions distinction must be explicit.** The framework needs to call out that solutions selling is fundamentally different from staffing selling. What works in staffing (promise and hire) doesn't work in solutions (promise and build).

3. **Role-tailored approach.** The framework should account for who the client stakeholder is. A VP meeting has different requirements than a manager meeting.

4. **Quality gate for materials.** Before anything goes to a client, someone needs to review it. The framework should define who approves client-facing materials.

5. **The SEAL Team 6 question remains open.** The framework should work for both models (everyone baseline + specialist subset). It should define what a salesperson can handle alone vs. what requires escalation to the AI team.

6. **3-4 month adoption timeline is realistic.** The framework should plan for gradual adoption, not overnight compliance.
