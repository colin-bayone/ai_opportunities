# Recurring Problem Patterns with the Sales Team

Extracted from the sales-response-buddy skill and playbook, which documents real incidents and battle-tested responses.

---

## Pattern 1: "Come present our AI capabilities"

**What happens:** Sales gets a meeting (or wants one) and asks Colin to join and present BayOne's AI capabilities to generate interest. There's no problem statement, no confirmed client need, sometimes not even a company name.

**Root cause:** Sales treats Colin as a pitch tool rather than a solutions resource. They don't understand the difference between "generating interest" (their job) and "scoping solutions" (Colin's job).

**Impact:**
- Colin's time wasted on calls with no substance
- Client gets a vague pitch instead of a targeted conversation
- Close to 0% success rate on these generic pitches (per Colin's track record)

**The line:** Colin is the closer, not the opener. By the time he's in the room, there should already be a reason for him to be there.

---

## Pattern 2: Scope Changes and Moving Goalposts

**What happens:** A meeting is agreed upon with a specific angle (e.g., internal initiatives, known work streams, scoping call). Then the ask shifts to something completely different (present on AI in retail, bring a POV on ecommerce trends). Colin is expected to prep for the new scope without being consulted.

**Root cause:** Sales agrees to things with clients without checking back with Colin, then passes the changed expectations downstream. No one owns the scope alignment between what sales promises and what Colin prepares for.

**Impact:**
- Colin preps for one meeting and shows up to a different one
- Creates a pattern where Colin can't trust what he's told about upcoming meetings
- Makes BayOne look disorganized when the person in the room isn't prepared for the actual conversation

**The line:** "This feels like a slightly different conversation than what we had discussed."

---

## Pattern 3: Promising Things That Don't Exist

**What happens:** A salesperson or delivery leader tells a client that BayOne has AI accelerators, chatbot demos, agent-based products, or standalone AI products. Colin is then asked to produce or present these things. They don't exist.

**Root cause:** People confuse Colin's prior experience and personal expertise with BayOne's current capabilities. Some are actively fabricating or inflating what BayOne has. Delivery leadership (specifically Amit) has been a repeat offender here.

**Impact:**
- Client has expectations that literally cannot be met
- Colin is expected to clean up someone else's false promises
- Damages BayOne's credibility when the truth comes out
- Colin has already flagged this multiple times and it keeps happening

**The line:** "I never saw or provided whatever deck was sent, so I can't speak to what was promised, and I won't own commitments made without my input."

---

## Pattern 4: AI-Generated "Opportunities" with No Human Thought

**What happens:** A salesperson finds a LinkedIn post, runs it through ChatGPT, and generates a pitch, pilot framework, and discovery questions. This is packaged as a real opportunity and Colin is asked to get on a call about it. No one has talked to the prospect. Zero qualification.

**Root cause:** Sales is using AI tools to create the appearance of work without doing the actual work of qualifying leads. The output looks polished but has no substance behind it.

**Impact:**
- AI-on-AI loop with no human in the middle (AI writes a post, AI writes a response, Colin gets asked to call about it)
- Manufactured "opportunities" that have no real prospect, no budget, no decision maker
- Wastes everyone's time and makes BayOne look foolish if they actually pursue these
- Creates fake pipeline that leadership might mistake for real pipeline

**The line:** "At some point a human needs to be in the loop here."

---

## Pattern 5: Committing Colin's Time Without Asking

**What happens:** Sales tells a client what Colin will present, what he'll bring, or what expertise he'll share without consulting him first. Colin finds out after the fact that he's expected to deliver something he never agreed to.

**Root cause:** Sales treats Colin's time as a free resource they can allocate. No accountability for over-promising because Colin absorbs the fallout (either scrambles to deliver or looks difficult for pushing back).

**Impact:**
- Colin is either forced to comply or forced to be the "difficult" one
- Sets precedent that his calendar and commitments are not his own
- Salesperson faces no consequences because Colin absorbs all the risk

**The line:** "I can't own deliverables or talking points that were offered up without my input."

---

## Cross-Cutting Observations

These five patterns are not isolated. They reinforce each other:

- Pattern 1 feeds Pattern 5 (sales books a generic pitch and tells the client Colin will present)
- Pattern 3 feeds Pattern 2 (someone promises capabilities, then shifts the meeting scope to match the fake promise)
- Pattern 4 is Pattern 1 with extra steps (instead of just asking Colin to pitch, they manufacture the whole "opportunity" first)
- All five patterns share a common root: **sales is not doing the qualification work before involving Colin**
