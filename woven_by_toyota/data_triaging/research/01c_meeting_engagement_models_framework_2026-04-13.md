## Overview

In the second half of the 2026-04-13 internal sync, Colin Moore introduced a three-model framework for positioning BayOne AI practice engagements. This framework had not previously been articulated in writing for any BayOne engagement. Colin explicitly acknowledged the framework as forthcoming BayOne-wide positioning rather than an ad hoc construct for Woven. The conversation then evolved through Pratik Sharda's proposed elaboration into a variable-split model, Colin's guardrail that ownership must always add up to one hundred percent, and an extended discussion of a staffing continuity technique that Colin had been piloting at Sephora to address a cannibalization problem. Jesse Smith affirmed the practical value of the three-model construct in light of uncertainty about the Woven stakeholder's AI maturity.

This document captures the framework and the surrounding discussion in exhaustive detail because the principles apply beyond Woven and represent a reusable positioning asset for the AI practice.

---

## Framework Introduction and BayOne-Wide Positioning Intent

Colin opened the framework discussion by explicitly signaling that he had not yet documented the model but that formalization was imminent. In his words, "I don't have this on a page yet, but that'll be coming soon. We are going to give starting out like 3 models for people for how we're doing solutions." This statement confirms that the framework is intended as AI practice positioning rather than as a one-off construct for Woven, and that the Woven discussion was the first time the three models were being articulated aloud with a sales counterpart.

Colin's framing in the meeting is therefore the earliest preserved articulation of what is expected to become standard BayOne AI practice positioning.

---

## Model 1: Full Managed Solutions

Colin described Model 1 as a fully managed solutions approach in which the client provides a problem and BayOne returns an outcome. The execution is entirely on the BayOne side, and pricing is structured by the engagement rather than by headcount. In his framing, "the first one is like a full managed solutions approach where they give us a problem. We give them back. We're going to do it 100% us. Don't worry about headcount. Don't worry about anything other than outcomes, deliverables, timelines, things like that. It's priced by the engagement. It's nothing to do with headcount. They actually aren't allowed to see those numbers, just like with Lim."

Several defining characteristics emerge from this framing:

- Execution ownership sits entirely with BayOne at one hundred percent.
- The commercial construct is outcome-based and priced by engagement scope, timeline, and deliverable, not by a staffing rate card.
- Headcount is treated as a BayOne internal concern. The client is not shown headcount figures, and visibility into resource loading is intentionally withheld.
- The client's obligations are limited to problem definition, acceptance of deliverables, and engagement-level governance.

Colin's reference analog is the Lam Research engagement, which the transcript renders phonetically as "Lim" and which Pratik separately references as "Lambir" later in the conversation. Both terms refer to the Lam Research intellectual property protection engagement, which Colin treats as the canonical illustration of how a full managed solutions engagement is scoped, priced, and governed.

---

## Model 2: Hybrid Collaboration

Colin described Model 2 as a hybrid collaboration model, using a seventy-thirty split as an illustrative anchor. In his words, "Type 2 is what I'm calling 70-30. We do 70% of the work in direct collaboration with the client team. That team could be our own SAL people. So they're still reporting into and getting daily instruction from someone at the client. But they're working with us internally too, so that there's a good knowledge transfer."

Key attributes of Model 2 as Colin articulated it:

- BayOne performs the majority of execution but operates in direct collaboration with a client-side team.
- The client-side team can take either of two forms. It can be BayOne staffing placements (which Colin phrased as "our own SAL people," interpreted here as staff augmentation or sourced labor resources) who are placed at the client and report into client leadership for day-to-day instruction. Alternatively, the collaborating team can consist of engineers already employed by the client.
- Regardless of which form the collaborating team takes, those resources receive daily instruction from a client-side manager while maintaining a tight working relationship with BayOne's AI practice team.
- Knowledge transfer is an explicit design goal of the model, not a byproduct.

Colin emphasized that the seventy-thirty ratio is a placeholder rather than a fixed specification. He later confirmed, "the percentage doesn't really mean anything. It just means we're collaborating. We're doing most of the work still, but there is that tight intermingling between the two teams that helps things move forward. And it does give them kind of a lasting value there."

The intent of Model 2 is therefore to combine BayOne's delivery ownership with structured interaction with the client's team so that the client retains knowledge, capability, and lasting value after the engagement closes.

---

## Model 3: Pure Staffing

Colin described Model 3 as pure staffing, rendered in the transcript phonetically as "TNM" and interpreted here as time and materials. In this model, BayOne supplies qualified resources to the client but is not involved in execution direction. In his framing, "type 3 is pure staffing. We're not in the loop at all."

Colin then framed the model's appropriate use case and its principal failure mode. The appropriate use case is a client that already has strong AI leadership in house. In his words, "if you have good people internally and you already have an AI guy in house that knows how to do this stuff, you don't need me. Fine. You can come back to me later on when it doesn't work, that's fine."

The failure mode is a client that requests staffing without having internal AI leadership capable of directing the resources. Colin used Sephora as the cautionary tale. "This avoids the other situation we have at Sephora, which let's say like someone said, give me four of your best people. And we give him that. The guy's never done an AI project in his life. So you give him four really good people and he's like, I don't know what to do with them. And the project doesn't work. And he's like, well, BayOne is bad. And I'm like, no, you're bad. If you would have let us manage this the whole time, you would have had an outcome by now."

The operative lessons from Model 3 as Colin framed it are:

- Pure staffing is acceptable when the client's AI leadership is strong enough to direct the resources productively.
- Pure staffing is dangerous when the client lacks AI leadership, because BayOne carries reputational risk for outcomes that were never under BayOne's execution control.
- The three-model framework allows BayOne to have the conversation explicitly before engagement, rather than discovering the mismatch in retrospect.

---

## Pratik's Variable-Split Elaboration

Pratik built on Colin's three-model construct by proposing that Model 2 be expressed as a range of selectable splits rather than as a single seventy-thirty ratio. His reasoning was that clients appreciate customization and that a single illustrative ratio may not match the preferences of every client.

In his words, "the option two, what I feel is we can also give them an option, do their three options so that they feel okay, there is more to choose from because sometimes maybe 730 itself may not work for them. And I'm thinking of in the second model, we can do 50-50 where we do half of it and you do half of it. We can do 75, 25 where we do 75 and you do 25 versus there could also be scenario they do the 75, they just want the 25. So maybe that's another way to put it."

Interpreted together with Colin's original endpoints, Pratik's elaboration yields a five-way split menu:

| Split (BayOne / Client) | Description |
|---|---|
| 100 / 0 | Model 1, full managed solutions |
| 75 / 25 | Hybrid, BayOne-heavy |
| 50 / 50 | Hybrid, balanced |
| 25 / 75 | Hybrid, client-heavy |
| 0 / 100 | Model 3, pure staffing |

Pratik closed his proposal with the observation, "they like the custom," confirming that the motivation behind the variable-split framing is client psychology. A menu of five options allows the client to feel that they are selecting from a meaningful range rather than accepting a preformed template.

---

## Colin's Ownership Guardrail

Colin accepted Pratik's variable-split elaboration and added a single guardrail: ownership must always sum to one hundred percent. In his words, "we can go like a variable mode. Like you define the parameters if you want it to be, you know, whatever the thing that, as long as it adds up to 100, I'm good. And our accountability and ownership is relative to that percentage."

Two points follow from this guardrail:

- The framework is flexible on where ownership sits but strict that ownership must be fully allocated. There is no scenario in which part of the work is unassigned.
- BayOne's accountability is scaled to its share. A seventy-five percent BayOne split carries seventy-five percent of the outcome accountability, and a twenty-five percent BayOne split carries twenty-five percent. This linkage between commercial share and accountability is the principle that prevents the Sephora failure mode, because it makes explicit at engagement start that the client cannot outsource outcome accountability without also outsourcing execution ownership.

Jesse reinforced the guardrail with a concise restatement: "As long as 100% of the work gets completed." Pratik echoed the same framing with his own phrasing that the outcome is one hundred percent regardless of split, and that the variable is only which party owns which portion of the work.

## The Sephora Cannibalization Problem

Before introducing the three-model framework, Colin raised a related concern about engagement cannibalization and used Sephora as the illustrative case. The concern is that successful AI-enabled modernization eliminates the need for the traditional workforce that was performing the work manually. In his words, "we cannibalize, like even with Sephora, Sephora we're cannibalizing right now. We have a team of QE engineers. They are not going to exist after we get done with the next project. That also means all of our competitors, QE teams, are also not going to exist, which is great. But that's the caution I had for Neha. Like, we're going to cannibalize."

The structural tension Colin surfaced is that the same engagement that delivers the value BayOne is contracted to deliver also removes the traditional staffing relationship that BayOne and its competitors would otherwise be billing against.

---

## The Hire-Then-Retain Technique as a Workaround

Colin then described a staffing continuity technique he has been using at Sephora as a response to the cannibalization problem. The technique is to hire the client-side engineers onto BayOne's payroll during the engagement, have them perform the same work they would have performed otherwise, and retain them as traditional staffing placements after the engagement closes.

In Colin's words, "the reality is what I'm trying to do for even that one is shift people so that they're technically on our roster. They're doing the work and they're still available. They're still like working at Sephora for things. But effectively, we are hiring them onto our team, building the product with them, and then they're staying after the project's done as like a traditional staffing. So that's good because they're already up to speed on everything that we did and you know, they're kind of a part of it. It fills that staffing need, but it also keeps them, you know, future proof as well. So even after you get it done with the project, it's not like, you know, hey, we're out of a job now because you guys automated my job. They're trained on how to use the new tool stack of everything too, so they give more value to the customer. You can squeeze a little bit more money out of the customer for those jobs too, which is great."

The structural features of the technique are:

- During the engagement, the engineers sit on BayOne's payroll and roster while continuing to report to and receive daily instruction from the client.
- The engineers participate in the product build alongside BayOne's AI practice team, so they absorb the new tool stack in real time.
- At engagement close, the engineers remain with BayOne in a traditional staffing relationship with the same client. The client retains continuity of personnel, and BayOne retains margin on the ongoing placements.
- The engineers avoid the displacement outcome that would otherwise follow from successful automation. Their skill profile has been upgraded to the new stack, which makes them more valuable to the client and commands a higher bill rate.

The technique therefore addresses three problems simultaneously. It neutralizes the cannibalization effect for BayOne. It de-risks the workforce transition for the client. It gives the retained engineers a meaningfully improved career trajectory relative to their pre-engagement position.

## Application to the Woven SOW

Jesse introduced the context for applying the framework by noting that Woven's procurement contact had flagged an incoming statement of work that would bring on approximately three to four people for the data triaging project. In his words, "they already have like three to four people that they need to bring on in an SOW piece, right? And so that's something that we can explore. We're not sure exactly the names or titles or skills that they would need to do something like that."

Colin then offered the Sephora hire-then-retain technique as applicable to those three to four positions, flagged explicitly as a suggestion rather than a recommendation. In his words, "if you wanted that for those four people, if they would want to help with such an effort, or if they are meant to be like, you know, owned and managed and that kind of a directed by Woven, completely fine. Just a food for thought for what we were doing for Sephora."

Pratik affirmed that the same cannibalization pattern could play out at Woven. He expanded the frame to argue that the longer-term play at Woven is an AI-enabled modernization of the triaging workflow rather than a simple three to four person placement. In his words, "AI is going to like, it's already is not is going to, but it's already is going to shift the like focus on the priorities of the actions of the work that's going to be done by the QAs, how they're doing, like you've already done it, Sephora. So even this one, even before they engage on these three and four. Giving that value to this team and the team on the procurement and others shows the competence we have as an organization."

Jesse then tied the three-model framework back to the specific Woven stakeholder profile. He noted that Travis Millet's AI experience was unknown and that the framework is especially useful in that scenario. In his words, "I think that's especially helpful. I think when it comes to something like this, right? It's like we don't know how much experience Travis Millet might have in automating things or implementing AI into data triaging, right? And so for that part of it, you know, you would need an SME or somebody to kind of lead the reins on that."

Jesse's affirmation is significant because it validates the framework's practical utility in its first live application. When client AI leadership is uncertain, the three-model construct gives BayOne a structured way to steer the client away from Model 3 and toward Model 1 or Model 2, where outcome accountability is appropriately matched to execution control.

## Synthesis

The framework articulated in this meeting has three interconnected components:

1. The three-model construct, with full managed solutions at one end, pure staffing at the other, and hybrid collaboration in the middle, anchored by a canonical analog in the form of the Lam Research engagement.
2. Pratik's variable-split elaboration, which converts the hybrid middle into a five-way menu and is governed by Colin's guardrail that ownership must sum to one hundred percent and accountability is scaled to the BayOne share.
3. The Sephora hire-then-retain technique, which operates orthogonally to the three models by addressing the cannibalization risk that arises whenever a successful AI engagement eliminates the traditional workforce that the client and its vendors had been relying on.

All three components apply beyond Woven. The three-model construct is intended to become BayOne-wide positioning. The variable-split is a commercial construct applicable to any engagement where the client prefers customization. The hire-then-retain technique is applicable to any engagement where the execution outcome would otherwise eliminate client-side roles.
