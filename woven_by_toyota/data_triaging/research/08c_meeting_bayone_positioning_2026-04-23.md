## Track One: Pratik Sharda's Framing Moves

### Opening Posture and Company Introduction

Pratik opened BayOne's introduction section after Travis finished his problem statement about the triage scale-up from roughly seven thousand tickets per month to close to one hundred thousand tickets per month by mid-July. Pratik's first framing move was to position BayOne as a locally based organization in the Bay Area. This placement was deliberate. Travis had just finished describing a landscape of roughly twenty-plus vendors he has been evaluating, many of which were described as offshore pure-staffing plays and many of which were described as auto-labeling or auto-annotation product companies. By establishing a local identity first, Pratik separated BayOne from the offshore staffing category before any other characterization had a chance to land.

### The "No Product" Statement and Its Correct Interpretation

The most important framing move Pratik made on the call was the statement that BayOne does not have a competing product for this particular opportunity. Pratik made this statement more than once. The first formulation appeared during the company introduction, where Pratik described BayOne as not having a competing product against the twenty-odd partner vendors who are pursuing this opportunity. The second formulation appeared later in the call, where Pratik said that BayOne has a product for technical screening, referred to as TechScore, and a couple of others, but not for this particular problem statement, and suggested putting a pin in that question.

This statement requires careful interpretation. Read flatly, it sounds like a concession that BayOne has nothing to offer on the solutions side and should be considered only for staffing. That reading is not correct. The statement reflects BayOne's methodological posture, which is that engagements are designed custom for the specific problem rather than sold from a catalog. The intent of the statement was to differentiate BayOne from the product-vendor category that Travis has already been evaluating, not to close the door on solutions work. BayOne's position, consistent across engagements, is that every engagement is tailor-made, grounded in a proven methodology, and built around the actual problem the client has, which is the opposite of the pattern Travis described where vendors show up claiming their existing product can handle the use case and then struggle when the dynamic nature of the bug catalog is explained to them.

For internal accountability purposes, the statement should be read as a differentiating move against the product-vendor category. It is not a disqualification of BayOne's solutions capability. BayOne's solutions work is delivered by engineering-led consultants using a custom methodology, and the call reinforced that capability through the BayOne technical lead's subsequent content. The statement and the subsequent solutions content are coherent when interpreted correctly, and the record of the call supports that interpretation.

### Services and Solutions Alongside the Human Element

Pratik's next framing move was to clarify that BayOne is not merely the staffing side of the picture. He stated explicitly that BayOne has solutions and services alongside the human-in-the-loop staffing element. He framed the approach as one in which BayOne works with customers to identify the problem and then work on the solution itself, rather than showing up with a pre-built product and trying to fit the client into it. Pratik characterized the human-in-the-loop approach as the right way to proceed given that no ready AI solution exists for this specific problem today, and that vendors in the space are, in his words, building the plane as they are trying to fly it.

This placement is strategically important. By framing staffing and solutions as a combined offering, Pratik kept the solutions door open without pushing a solutions sale. That allowed the BayOne technical lead to contribute substantive engineering content later in the call without the conversation feeling like a sales pivot.

### Preferred Partner Status at Woven

Pratik framed BayOne as an existing trusted and preferred partner at Woven across other teams. This was introduced in the context of Travis's direct question about why he would switch from his current staffing supplier to BayOne. Pratik's answer emphasized that BayOne is already among the select partners that Woven works with, which means procurement friction is lower and that aggressive pricing negotiations are not required on every new engagement. The framing move was to treat the preferred partner status as a procurement advantage rather than a claim of general superiority.

### TechScore and the Internal Technical Assessment Process

Pratik described BayOne's internal technical assessment process, referred to as TechScore, as part of the quality differentiation. He framed this as a proprietary in-house AI-enabled screening tool that scores candidates on technical criteria before they are submitted to the client. He described the tool as producing a specific number, which allows clients to benchmark candidates against a minimum threshold before reviewing them. He also acknowledged flexibility in the tool's use, noting that some managers look for attributes beyond the technical skills the tool measures, and that BayOne can skip that part of the process if the client prefers a wider pool. Pratik explicitly framed this flexibility as another differentiator.

### Nimble Commercial Structure

Pratik responded affirmatively when Travis asked whether BayOne could do a single statement of work covering multiple resources rather than one contract per head. Pratik confirmed that BayOne is willing to structure the commercial package as a single statement of work for ten or more resources. The framing move here was to position BayOne as nimble on commercial structure, which contrasts with more rigid staffing firms that transact one role at a time.

### Engineering-Heavy Leadership as a Differentiator

At the close of his framing contribution, Pratik named what he described as the biggest differentiator against other partners in the industry. He stated that BayOne's co-founders, Colin himself, and most of the team have engineering backgrounds. Pratik mentioned his own background in electronics and communication, semiconductors, and networking. He framed the contrast as one against staffing firms that are staffed primarily with functional recruiters who do not have a technical background, noting that this engineering depth helps BayOne understand the entire stack end-to-end when evaluating candidates and when engaging on technical problems.

## Track Two: The BayOne Technical Lead's Framing Moves

### Background and Credibility Setup

Colin Moore, introduced by Pratik as the Director of AI at BayOne, opened his contribution with a brief personal background. He described prior work as a research and development engineer on solid state electrolyte for electric vehicle batteries, with the business subsequently acquired by GM. This was a load-bearing credibility move because Travis had earlier described the triage problem by referencing GM's approach, specifically noting that Cruise is now part of GM and that GM was using fourteen static categories for a comparable triage effort. The reference established immediate context familiarity without any additional explanation.

Colin then described subsequent work at a Santa Clara company called Coherent, characterizing the space as optics, semiconductors, advanced materials, and manufacturing. He explicitly drew the parallel to Travis's domain by noting that while Coherent was not automotive, the problem class was similar, and he specifically mentioned that the reliability of systems was the central engineering concern at Coherent. This set up the framing that reliability engineering in complex, dynamic, technical environments is his home turf.

### Skepticism of Eighty Percent Accuracy

Colin's first substantive framing move was to state that engineers do not accept eighty percent accuracy as sufficient. He referenced the tendency during the initial wave of Gen AI to apply generative AI to every problem, and he characterized the engineering reaction to eighty percent accuracy as visibly uncomfortable. This positioning was aimed at Travis's earlier statement that the in-house tool was performing at about forty percent of the time. The framing move established that BayOne's technical posture is higher accuracy is required for engineering contexts, and that accuracy thresholds cannot be waved off.

### Rejection of the Binary Framing

Colin then stated that the problem is not binary between AI-only approaches and human-only approaches. He acknowledged the limits of human reliability, citing a statistic that the very best PhD researchers given ten thousand images to classify top out around ninety-two percent on a simple binary task like giraffe versus car. The point was that humans are inconsistent and that consistency is where AI contributes. Framing the problem as non-binary opened the space for the hybrid architecture he described next.

### The Hybrid Architecture

Colin laid out a specific hybrid architecture in three parts. The first part was deterministic classifiers as early as possible, applied before any AI is invoked. The second part was combined methods layered on top of each other, which he framed as a way to avoid multiplying decimals together and getting progressively smaller numbers. The third part was the reservation of multimodal or generative AI for fringe cases only, rather than as the primary mechanism. Colin framed this approach as the one he has used successfully in his career and the one that allows accuracy targets of ninety-six to ninety-eight percent rather than eighty percent.

### Progressive Human-to-AI Offload with Continuous Sampling

Colin then described the operational pattern that pairs with the hybrid architecture. He framed the progression as starting with human in the loop for early decisions, then moving to a state where the AI makes the same call and is checked against the human, then progressively offloading more of the decision volume to the AI while retaining continuous sampling. He used an assembly-line quality assurance analogy, noting that things are never fully hands off and that sampling continues to verify that correct calls are still being made. This was a direct methodological statement that maps naturally to Woven's own human-in-the-loop buffer structure, which Travis described as a contingency plan rather than a permanent outsourcing move.

### Confidence Scoring Reframed

Colin's next framing move was to reframe confidence scoring. He stated that confidence scores from AI models are mostly aggregate scores of many factors and not particularly meaningful in isolation. He proposed treating confidence as a track record of correct decisions over time, analogous to how confidence in a human colleague is built from prior successful assignments. This reframing is methodologically consistent with the progressive offload pattern, because the model's confidence to handle a given type of decision is established by its prior track record on that type of decision, not by a generic self-reported confidence number.

### Engagement with Follow-Up Technical Questions

Travis asked follow-up questions on fine-tuning existing models, training of models in-house, and use of commercial off-the-shelf components. Colin answered that the pattern is both, depending on cost and convenience. He framed off-the-shelf models as the preferred starting point when they work, because the training work has already been done and does not have to be paid for again. He noted that the simplest solution should always come first because these things sound great but are expensive to do at scale. He then referenced Vertex AI on Google Cloud as an example of hosted capability that can reduce cost at scale, specifically noting the context of the call being on Google Meet as a cue that the Vertex AI stack was likely to be accessible to Travis's team.

### Secondary Return on TechScore

Later in the call, Colin returned to the TechScore topic. He disclosed that he is the architect of the next evolution of BayOne's in-house screening platform, which he described as going deeper than TechScore. He drew an explicit parallel to Travis's own problem, noting the irony that the new platform uses AI to do something while retaining a human-in-the-loop backup override, which is exactly the architecture Travis was describing. The framing move here was to connect BayOne's internal tooling to the client's external problem in a way that demonstrates methodological consistency rather than a pitch.

## Cumulative Framing Outcome

The combined effect of the three tracks produced a specific framing outcome. The conversation became a staffing-oriented engagement on the immediate problem, which is the one backfill role that Travis described as currently open with one unnamed agency. At the same time, the conversation established a technically credible solutions subtext that was not pushed. The BayOne technical lead contributed substantive engineering content on hybrid architecture, progressive offload, confidence scoring, and cost-aware model selection, but none of that content was converted into a solutions pitch on the call. Pratik's "no product" framing, interpreted correctly, reinforced the posture that BayOne does not attempt to sell a catalog item into a problem it has not scoped.

Travis's reception of the technical content was positive. He engaged with follow-up questions on fine-tuning, off-the-shelf model usage, and training from corrections. He stated, paraphrasing, that he enjoys talking to AI companies because he can pick their brains and see how the approaches compare to where his own team is. That reception confirmed that the engineering framing was received as credible and welcome, not as off-topic for a call that started on the staffing side.

The commercial resolution of the call was narrower than the full opportunity surface. Travis agreed to open the one backfill role to BayOne as a head-to-head against the one existing agency. He acknowledged that the thirteen times scale-up work remains unresolved, with the question of how many incremental people, who they are, and whether they come through a contractor or vendor model still under evaluation. The solutions side, which would include any engagement by BayOne's AI practice on the triage architecture itself, was not commercially defined on the call. It was introduced, received well, and left open for future exploration rather than pushed toward a near-term commitment.

The summary position is that BayOne framed itself on this call as a locally based, engineering-led, preferred partner with nimble commercial structure, a proprietary technical screening process, and genuine solutions capability that is custom-built rather than packaged. The framing was cumulative across three tracks and landed coherently.
