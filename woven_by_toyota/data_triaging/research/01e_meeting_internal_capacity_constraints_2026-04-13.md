In the same breath he signaled that Pratik is being treated as an exception to that deflection pattern, describing Pratik and by extension Jesse as "kind of a special one right now." The implication is that Colin is selectively burning his own bandwidth to keep Woven alive despite being capped.

## Why Labeling Is the Worst-Case Capacity Scenario

**FLAG: INTERNAL ONLY.**

Colin specifically called out labeling work as the scenario that most strains the current team. His framing in item three:

> "If it's true labeling, that's where we're going to have a problem right now with the team... if it's labeling, labeling, you inherently need people that are focused on that, that are spending time, dedicated time with the team right now. That's going to be a challenge."

The mechanics of why labeling is the worst case:

- **Dedicated headcount.** True labeling work requires people whose time is committed to the labeling task. It is not a part-time or advisory engagement that Colin's current senior practitioners can absorb on the side.
- **Focused time with the client team.** Labeling requires sustained interaction with the client, not discrete consulting touchpoints. This multiplies the people-time required.
- **No elastic capacity on the current team.** Colin has already described the existing team as capped at 15 people against 15 large projects. A labeling engagement at Woven would not fit inside that envelope.

Colin contrasted this implicitly with the correlation, root-cause, and classification variants of the problem (discussed in items one and two of his response). Those variants are "still labeling at the end of the day" in his framing, but as "a subclass of labeling" they are more compatible with BayOne's current AI-heavy, automation-leveraged delivery model. True bulk human-in-the-loop labeling is the scenario that cannot be absorbed without dedicated new headcount.

The upshot: the worst-case capacity outcome for Woven is that the discovery reveals a pure, large-scale, human-dependent labeling need. In that scenario, BayOne either staffs up specifically for it or declines the commitment.

## The "Do It Now Or Later" Sequencing Logic

**FLAG: INTERNAL ONLY.**

Colin was explicit that his "now or later" phrasing is not a proposal to delay the engagement. It is a proposal to use the discovery call as a decision gate for headcount action. His exact wording:

> "What I want to do before then, so what my plan would be here is go into that meeting, get some info, understand the problem, and figure out if we can do this now or later. And when I say later, I don't mean that I want to do it later. I mean that let's figure out how hard I need to push Yogesh and Surej to say, get me people now if you want business."

The decomposed logic:

- **"Now" path.** If discovery confirms a viable, commercially meaningful engagement, Colin uses that commercial signal immediately to force the headcount conversation with Yogesh and Surej. Deploy on the engagement as soon as people are approved.
- **"Later" path.** If discovery suggests the engagement is real but the timing allows sequencing, Colin still pushes for headcount, but he sequences the engagement for a window when capacity is available rather than triggering a scramble.
- **What is explicitly rejected.** Delay for its own sake. Colin is not suggesting Woven be deprioritized or deferred on principle. The sequencing is about matching engagement start to available capacity, not about slowing the commercial motion.

Colin stated the outcome he is most focused on avoiding: "we don't have to have that awkward time, you know, where they said, hey, you know, we're ready for you to come in and do some work. And we say, great, we don't have anyone to do it. So I'll avoid that awkwardness." The sequencing discipline exists specifically to prevent that client-facing failure mode.

## The Internal Staffing Path: Vinayak as Coordination Contact

**FLAG: INTERNAL ONLY.**

Colin confirmed that the sourcing bottleneck is not the actual constraint. Internal staffing at BayOne is a workable channel. His framing:

> "Even for us internally, it's not that bad for us to get people. It's just, it's like a, you know, it's like our own internal staffing, right? Like, so, you know, like I gotta chase down Vinayak to go and grab people. We can do it, we can do it fast. It's just that that support has to be there."

The key data points:

- **Sourcing contact:** Vinayak is the internal person Colin coordinates with to bring staffing bodies onto an engagement.
- **Speed:** Colin characterizes the Vinayak path as fast ("we can do it fast").
- **Actual bottleneck:** Executive support, not sourcing. "That support has to be there" refers back to Yogesh and Surej approving the headcount direction in the first place.

The implication is important for understanding the Woven capacity picture. BayOne is not constrained by the mechanics of finding and placing people. BayOne is constrained by an executive-level unwillingness to authorize AI team scaling. Sourcing is the easy part; the political unlock is the hard part.

## How Capacity Constraints Shape What BayOne Can Commit to on Woven

**FLAG: INTERNAL ONLY.**

Reading item three against the rest of the meeting, Colin's capacity position translates into the following commitment posture for the Woven engagement:

- **Discovery call participation: committed.** Colin will attend the Travis Millet discovery call and extract diagnostic information about data types, problem shape, and intended outcomes.
- **Scope definition: conditional.** Colin will not pitch a labeling approach in the discovery call. He explicitly flagged that pitching the wrong labeling type is a failure mode he wants to prevent. The point of discovery is to distinguish classical root-cause correlation work (which fits current delivery) from true large-scale labeling (which does not).
- **Delivery model: mapped to three options.** Colin introduced his three-model framework to Pratik and Jesse during this meeting:
  1. **Fully managed solutions** — BayOne owns outcome, deliverables, and timelines; priced by engagement, not by headcount.
  2. **70-30 collaboration** — BayOne does roughly 70 percent of the work in direct collaboration with a client-side team that may include BayOne staffing bodies or existing client employees.
  3. **Pure staffing** — BayOne is not in the solution loop at all; client directs the work.
  Pratik suggested extending model two into a variable split (50-50, 75-25, 25-75). Colin accepted the variable framing ("as long as it adds up to 100, I'm good. And our accountability and ownership is relative to that percentage").
- **Capacity-sensitive positioning:** Model one (fully managed) is the hardest to commit to under current capacity. Models two and three are more achievable because they route staffing bodies through the Vinayak channel rather than through the constrained core AI team.
- **The SOW-for-three-or-four-people path:** Jesse surfaced that the Woven opportunity includes a near-term request for three to four staffing positions via a procurement SOW. Colin flagged this as a potential capacity workaround. If those positions are filled via BayOne staffing, those people become technically on the BayOne roster and can be leveraged both for the immediate engagement and for future continuity.
- **Sephora precedent as capacity context:** Colin offered the Sephora engagement as a cautionary and instructive example. On Sephora, BayOne is cannibalizing its own QE team ("We have a team of QE engineers. They are not going to exist after we get done with the next project"). The workaround has been to hire client-side engineers onto BayOne's roster during the engagement, train them through project delivery, and retain them as staffed resources afterward. Colin positioned this as a model that could apply at Woven, with two benefits: it future-proofs the client-side engineers (so they are not displaced by the automation being built), and it builds BayOne's bench with engineers already trained on the new tool stack. This is partly a capacity workaround and partly a commercial play.
