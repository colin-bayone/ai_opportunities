# The Costing Root Cause — Colin's Debrief

**Date:** 2026-04-15
**Context:** Colin's explanation of why the internal pricing meeting was a disaster, provided after the transcript was processed.

---

## The Chain of Failures

### 1. Rahul Sharma set the price before understanding the problem

In the very first meeting with Cisco, before any discovery, before any work estimate, Rahul Sharma (President of BayOne) threw out $100-150K/quarter. This was not a calculated bid. It was a number said in a room to sound reasonable and get the deal moving.

### 2. Colin was shut out of the pricing process

Colin approached Rahul Sharma multiple times to ask how gross margin would be calculated. Each time: "Don't worry about it. We need to get our foot in the door." Colin was never consulted as a planner for the costing.

### 3. Colin was then loaded into the costing as an hourly resource

Despite being the salaried Director of AI for BayOne, the team decided to include Colin's time as an hourly line item in the project costing. This eats into the already-thin margin created by the arbitrary $100K number.

### 4. Headcount was exposed to Cisco

Instead of positioning this as an outcome-based or effort-based engagement, BayOne showed Cisco the headcount breakdown. This means Cisco can now divide the total by the number of people and back into per-person rates. This eliminates BayOne's ability to manage margin invisibly and creates a permanent constraint on pricing flexibility.

### 5. The internal team cannot align on basic financial math

The April 15 meeting transcript shows:
- Loaded cost calculations giving different answers depending on who is doing them
- Confusion about what "loaded" means (base + overhead vs. base + overhead + GM)
- Arguments about whether to use actual payroll ($8-15/hr offshore) or market replacement ($20-24/hr)
- Multiple formula errors in the spreadsheet being fixed in real time
- The meeting ending without full alignment, with the Anand call already moved earlier

### 6. The ask nearly doubled with no justification the client will accept

Going from $100K to $168-178K is a ~70-78% increase. The scope has not changed. The work turned out to be simpler than expected. The justification is entirely internal: the original price was wrong, and the team composition changed. None of these are reasons Cisco would accept for nearly doubling the bill.

---

## Two Separate Problems Being Conflated

Colin was explicit that these are unrelated:

**Problem A: The price was wrong from day one.** This is a BayOne internal problem caused by Rahul Sharma setting a number without analysis. It has nothing to do with Cisco's behavior, scope changes, or access delays.

**Problem B: Cisco changed the scope and caused massive delays.** This is a Cisco-caused problem. Procurement took 2 months. Hardware took another month. Access is still an ongoing nightmare. Srinivas changed the deliverables from the agreed A-F to different precursor work. DeepSight is still not deployed.

These two problems require completely different conversations with Anand. Conflating them makes BayOne look like they are using Cisco's delays as cover for their own pricing mistake.

---

## The Outcome-Based vs. Headcount-Based Mistake

If the engagement had been positioned as outcome-based from the start:
- BayOne's internal margin is invisible to Cisco
- Adding or removing resources is a BayOne decision, not a Cisco negotiation
- The "ask" conversation is about deliverables and phases, not headcount math
- Cisco cannot divide and back into hourly rates

Because headcount was exposed, every future conversation about money is now a conversation about people and their rates. This is a structural disadvantage that cannot be undone for the current SOW.
