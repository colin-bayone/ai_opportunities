# Anticipated Objections & Responses

Yogesh is experienced. He's probably had conversations like this before. Some of these pushbacks are in good faith; some are deflections. Pre-drafted responses for the most likely ones below.

---

## "We don't do mid-cycle raises — you'll have to wait for the annual cycle."

**The trap:** Accepting this means waiting 6-12 months for a process that may not produce meaningful movement anyway.

**Response:**
> *"That's actually the core of what I'm raising. If there's a functional annual cycle, I'd like to understand what it looks like — when it runs, what kind of adjustments are typical, who decides. If there isn't one, or it's been inconsistent, then the 'wait for the cycle' framing doesn't apply. Either way, I've been here over a year without any comp conversation, and the work I'm doing has expanded substantially. That needs a review now, not on a calendar."*

---

## "Your bonus was your annual compensation adjustment."

**The trap:** Letting this stand equates a one-time $15K gross / $10K net payment with a base salary adjustment. It isn't the same thing, and it doesn't compound.

**Response:**
> *"A bonus is a bonus — it's non-recurring, it's heavily taxed, and it doesn't touch base. My base is what determines my long-term earnings, what the market compares against, and what future bonuses are calculated from. A ten-thousand-dollar net payment doesn't address the gap between Director comp and three-role delivery. I want to separate those conversations."*

---

## "You haven't been here that long — let's revisit in six months."

**The trap:** Open-ended deferral. "Six months" has a way of becoming "let's circle back next quarter."

**Response:**
> *"I've been here over a year. I've delivered across nine client engagements, built the capabilities positioning, built an entire internal qualification framework, delivered the Sephora demo, and I'm the technical lead on every active pursuit. 'Longer tenure' isn't the variable. If you're telling me there's a tenure threshold before compensation can be discussed at all, I need to understand what that threshold is and why."*

---

## "Sripriya will be back soon / Sripriya is the right person for this conversation."

**The trap:** Punting to an absent decision-maker. Especially dangerous because Yogesh — as founder and effective co-CEO — absolutely can act on this himself without Sripriya.

**Response:**
> *"I'd welcome that conversation with Sripriya. But it's been two months since I was told she'd be my reporting line, and I haven't had a substantive engagement. I can't wait indefinitely. And honestly — you and Surej are the founders of this place. If you and Surej decide this is right, it happens. I'm not asking you to wait on Sripriya to advocate for me. I'm asking you to act."*

---

## "We're a services company with tight margins — the budget isn't there."

**The trap:** Making this about company constraints rather than your value.

**Response:**
> *"I understand margin pressure in services is real. What I'd ask you to weigh against that is the revenue I'm directly enabling — the Sephora EDW pursuit, the Cisco CI/CD engagement, the Lam Research proposal, the McGrath RFP, the Cisco EPNM POC. The practice is not a cost center; it's the thing that's creating this pipeline. The question isn't whether there's budget — it's whether BayOne wants to retain the person building the pipeline at the comp level the market is setting for this work."*

---

## "Colin, we value you enormously. You know that."

**The trap:** This is flattery as deflection. Don't argue with it; don't accept it as payment.

**Response:**
> *"I appreciate you saying that, and I believe it. What I'm asking is for that value to show up in compensation and structure, not just in how it's described. Valuing someone and paying them fairly are separate things, and right now only one of those is happening."*

---

## "We have a plan for you — an AI Manager is coming, Sripriya will re-engage, etc."

**The trap:** Promises without dates. You've heard versions of this for months.

**Response:**
> *"I want that to be true, and I want to believe the plan. What I've learned is that I can't operate on promises without dates and accountability. So I'd like to hear: when is the AI Manager being hired, by whom, with what start date? When will Priya re-engage, and what does engagement look like — a weekly 1:1, a quarterly review, what? When do I find out about my own comp and career path — a specific date I can mark on my calendar?"*

---

## "Are you looking for another job?"

**The trap:** The answer "no" weakens your position; the answer "yes" forces a confrontation you're not ready for.

**Response:**
> *"I'm committed to BayOne. What I'm telling you is that the current equilibrium — this pace, this comp, this structure — isn't sustainable for me to keep going the way I've been going. I want to find a path that works here. That's why I'm having this conversation with you now instead of later."*

**Key:** This answer is honest (you said so yourself — you're not trying to leave) AND it preserves the leverage without threatening. You're describing the system, not your own intentions.

---

## "What would it take to make you happy?"

**Not a trap — but a test.** He's checking whether you have a real ask or just a feeling. Have the number and terms ready.

**Response:**
> *"Three things. One: a base adjustment that reflects the three roles I'm actually filling — I think [target number from `04_asks_and_red_lines.md`] is fair and defensible against the market. Two: a named path to VP of AI within twelve months, with what the criteria look like. Three: real management support — the AI Manager hire closed by a specific date, and clarity on who my actual boss is. I'd take any two of those as meaningful movement. I'd consider one without the other two as a half-measure."*

---

## "Let me think about it and get back to you."

**The trap:** Open-ended. You walk out with nothing.

**Response:**
> *"Of course. When should we talk next? Can we put a specific date on it — even two weeks out, but an actual date — so we both know when this conversation continues?"*

If he resists putting a date on it: that itself is data. *"I'd really like us to set a checkpoint. Otherwise these things drift. Can we say [specific date two weeks out]?"*

---

## "I'm not sure I can advocate for this — it's above my level."

**This is almost certainly a deflection, not a real constraint.** Yogesh founded BayOne and is effectively a co-CEO with Surej. There is no "above his level" for a Director-level comp decision.

**Response:**
> *"Yogesh, respectfully — you founded this company, and you and Surej run it. If this is a decision that needs to get made, you're the person who can make it or make it happen. I'd rather have you own this with me than route me to someone else."*

Said warmly, not confrontationally. You're flattering him by naming his actual authority while also holding him accountable for using it.

---

## If he seems to absorb the conversation well and is genuinely supportive

Don't oversell. Don't overthank. Just close the loop:

> *"Thank you. I want you to know I raised this with you first because I trust you. I'd like to leave this conversation with one concrete thing — [the specific commitment you've converged on]. Can we confirm that before we wrap?"*

Get the confirmation verbally, then follow up in email within 24 hours.
