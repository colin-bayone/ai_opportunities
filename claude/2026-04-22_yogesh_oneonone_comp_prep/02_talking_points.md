# Talking Points — Opening, Key Lines, Closing

**Use these as scaffolding, not a script.** Read them out loud a couple of times before the meeting so the phrasing lives in your head and sounds natural.

---

## Opening (first 90 seconds)

Don't lead with small talk if the meeting is on a calendar where the regular cadence is operational. Save the pleasantries for a minute, then pivot:

> *"Yogesh, I want to use most of our time today on something different than the usual status. There are a few things I need to put on the table — about compensation, about the structure I'm operating in, and about where this is going long-term. I want to be direct with you because I respect you, and because I think you and I share the problem."*

Then let him respond. He'll say something like "okay, go ahead" or "of course, what's on your mind." Do not rush through it.

---

## The core statement (plant the flag)

Deliver this calmly, slowly. It's the sentence the whole meeting turns on.

> *"I recently learned that BayOne doesn't appear to have an annual review or cost-of-living adjustment mechanism. I want to confirm with you first whether that's accurate. If it is, I need to tell you plainly: I'm not going to continue at my current pace and scope without a change in compensation. I'm not threatening to leave and I'm not trying to create a problem. I'm telling you what my position is."*

**Stop talking.** Let him respond. Do not keep explaining. Do not soften.

If he confirms there's no such mechanism: move to the three-role framing below.

If he says "we do handle comp on a case-by-case basis" or something similar: *"Good. Then I'd like to make the case for mine."*

---

## The three-role framing

> *"My title is Director of AI. In practice I'm doing three roles simultaneously: Director of AI, Technical Sales Lead, and Lead Engineer. Any one of those is a full-time senior role. The capabilities deck, the opportunity catalog, the lead qualification framework, the client discovery, the proposals, the pricing, the POCs, the actual engineering work on TalentAI and on client delivery — that's all me. I have over ten direct reports and no managers underneath me. I don't have an engaged boss — Sripriya was named as my reporting line in February, and I haven't seen meaningful engagement there."*

Then the pivot:

> *"I'm not raising any of this to complain. I'm raising it because the company is getting three senior roles from me and paying for one. I've done this for over a year with no adjustment. That has to change."*

---

## The market comparison line (use carefully, but use it)

This is your sharpest point. Deliver it without anger:

> *"One thing that surfaced this for me — I'm currently helping fill individual contributor AI engineering roles where the offers exceed my base salary. I need to understand what BayOne thinks it's buying from me, and what it thinks it's paying for."*

Don't elaborate. Don't name candidates. The point lands without detail.

---

## The career path pivot

After the comp conversation has been established, broaden to the structural question:

> *"The other thing I need clarity on is the path. I've been Director of AI for over a year. I have no visibility into what's next — VP of AI, an expanded practice, equity, something else. And I need to know who my actual boss is. If Sripriya is my reporting line, she hasn't been present. If that's changing, I need to know. Right now I'm running this practice without a manager and without a peer to escalate to."*

---

## Closing the meeting with a concrete next step

Do not let this end with "let me think about it" or "I'll talk to Surej." Push gently but firmly:

> *"I'd like to know when we can talk about this again with a concrete answer. Can we put a specific date on it? I don't need the decision today, but I do need to know by when I'll have it."*

If he offers two weeks: accept and confirm the date.
If he offers "a few weeks": pin it down. *"Can we say by May 6?"*
If he tries to push past the end of May: that's a signal. *"That's too long. This has been accumulating for over a year. I need a response within two to three weeks."*

Confirm who's accountable:

> *"Who's actually making this decision — you, Surej, Sripriya? I want to make sure the right person has the case."*

(Note: given Yogesh is the founder and effectively a co-CEO with Surej, the honest answer is probably "Yogesh and Surej together." If Yogesh tries to route this elsewhere, gently push: *"I'd expect you and Surej are the people here. Am I wrong about that?"*)

---

## Lines to have ready for the emotional moments

**If you feel yourself getting angry:**
Slow down. Take a breath. Don't hide it — acknowledge it cleanly: *"I want to be transparent with you — I'm frustrated, which is part of why I'm raising this now. I'm working to keep that frustration from driving how I talk about it, because I want this to be a productive conversation, not a venting session."*

That kind of meta-honesty disarms. It shows composure without pretending you're not affected.

**If he tries to flatter:**
("Colin, you know how much we value you, you're amazing, etc.") Don't argue with it. Don't accept it as a substitute either. *"I appreciate that. What I'm asking is for that value to show up in how I'm compensated and supported, not just in how it's described."*

**If he gets defensive:**
*"I'm not putting this on you. I know comp decisions don't sit with you alone. What I'm asking is that you partner with me on this — help me get the right people in the conversation, and help me understand what's realistic here."*

**If he pivots to "we have budget constraints":**
*"I understand budgets are real. What I'm telling you is that my continuing at this pace is also a budget question — the practice can't run if I burn out, and I'm closer to that than the company probably realizes."*

**If he asks "what would make you happy?":**
Don't dodge. Have the number ready (see `04_asks_and_red_lines.md`). *"A base adjustment that reflects the three roles I'm actually filling. The specific number I think is fair is [X]. I also want a clear path to VP of AI within twelve months and real management support under me."*

---

## The silence rule

Yogesh has been in leadership a long time. He will use silence, and he will be comfortable letting you fill it.

Don't. After you state a position, an ask, or a question — **stop talking**. Count to five in your head if you have to. The person who speaks first after a hard statement loses ground. Let him respond.

---

## Words to avoid

- "Sorry" (you have nothing to apologize for in this conversation)
- "I was wondering if maybe..."
- "I don't want to be difficult, but..."
- "I hate to bring this up..."
- "I think I deserve..." (too squishy — state what you've done, state what you're asking for)
- "A lot" / "way more" (vague — use specific numbers)

## Words to use

- "I've concluded..."
- "What I'm telling you is..."
- "I need..."
- "By when..."
- "Who is actually making this decision?"
- "What's the path?"
