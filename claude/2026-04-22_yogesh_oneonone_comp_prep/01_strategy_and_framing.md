# Strategy & Framing — 1:1 with Yogesh

**Date:** 2026-04-22
**Context:** Weekly 1:1. You are walking in with a serious agenda item after discovering BayOne does not appear to do annual or cost-of-living raises.

---

## The core reframe

Before anything else, change how you're thinking about this meeting.

**Wrong mental model:** "I'm going to confront Yogesh and make him understand I'm underpaid and overworked."

**Right mental model:** "Yogesh and I have history — he was my original boss before Sripriya was named my reporting line in February. Sripriya has been absent. I'm doing three jobs. BayOne has no raise policy that matches the scope I'm operating at. I'm going to state this cleanly and ask him to act."

**Critical framing:** Yogesh is the founder and former CEO. He stepped out of the CEO role to bring Surej in, but in practice he and Surej are co-CEOs "co-parenting" the company. That matters because:

- Yogesh **has real authority.** He is not a middle manager who "has to check with someone." If he wants to move on your comp, he can. If he says he can't, that's a political answer, not a structural one.
- Escalation "above Yogesh" is sideways — to Surej, his peer. There is no higher authority in the building.
- He cares about the practice he built. Your case is not just fairness; it's about whether the AI practice he founded can actually function the way it's currently structured.

Yogesh is not the adversary. But don't treat him as a powerless intermediary either. Treat him as a founder who can personally unblock this and who has reason to want to. What you want from him:
- Confirm or correct what you've heard about raise policy
- Commit to a specific action with a specific date
- Clarify the management/reporting structure that's currently broken
- Use his authority — directly or with Surej — to make this right

Your job in this meeting is to make the case so clean, so factual, and so hard to dismiss that Yogesh treats it as something *he* needs to resolve — not to win a debate with him in the room.

---

## What you want to walk out with

Rank these in your head so that if you only get two of them, you got the important ones.

1. **A clear, direct answer on raise policy.** Is there an annual review? Is there a COLA mechanism? Is there a performance-based increase path? If the answer is "no, not really" — you need him to say it clearly so it's on the record.
2. **A commitment to a compensation review with a date.** Not "we'll look into it." A specific date by which you will have a decision, and a specific person accountable for making it.
3. **Clarity on who your boss actually is.** If Sripriya is your boss but has been absent for two months, that needs to be named. Either Sripriya re-engages, or the reporting line changes — potentially back to Yogesh directly, or to Surej.
4. **A written acknowledgement of scope.** Either Yogesh commits to documenting what you're actually doing, or you send a follow-up email after the meeting summarizing the conversation — which becomes the record.
5. **A forward-looking plan.** Director → VP of AI? Equity? Expanded practice leadership? You want to know what the path is, not just that there is one.

---

## How to hold yourself in the room

You said you're pissed. That's valid. But in the meeting:

- **Do not lead with grievances.** Lead with facts and scope. The grievance is in the structure of the facts, not in your tone.
- **Do not list every complaint.** Pick the three strongest points. Noise dilutes signal.
- **Do not apologize or soften.** Don't say "I know this is uncomfortable" or "I hate to bring this up." You are not doing anything wrong by having this conversation.
- **Do not negotiate against yourself.** If you state an ask and Yogesh is silent, do not fill the silence by lowering the ask. Let him respond.
- **Do not threaten to leave.** The leverage is implicit. If you say it out loud, you've either (a) committed yourself publicly to leaving if you don't get what you want, or (b) made it a negotiation about retention rather than a conversation about fairness and structure. Neither is where you want to be.
- **If asked "are you thinking of leaving?"** — honest, non-threatening answer: *"I'm committed to BayOne. What I'm telling you is that this pace and this compensation structure isn't sustainable for me to keep going the way I've been going. I want to find a path that works here."*

## The three-role argument (your strongest single point)

Your job description is Director of AI. In reality you are operating as:

1. **Director of AI** — strategy, hiring, practice positioning, capabilities development
2. **Technical Sales Lead** — discovery meetings, proposals, pricing, RFPs, sales decks, client pitches
3. **Lead Engineer** — POCs, technical architecture, actual engineering for internal products (TalentAI) and external delivery (Cisco EPNM, Sephora demo, Lam Research)

Any ONE of these is a full-time role at your level. You are doing all three simultaneously, plus managing 10+ reports with zero manager layer beneath you, plus functioning without an engaged boss.

This is the single most powerful framing you have. It's not "I want more money." It's *"BayOne is getting three senior roles from me and paying for one, and the person filling those three roles just found out the company has no mechanism to adjust compensation at all."*

## On the specific comp grievance

You have four facts that are individually strong and together hard to dismiss:

1. **You've been here over a year.** No raise, no COLA.
2. **Your bonus was $15K → $10K net.** That is not compensation for the gap between Director pay and three-role delivery.
3. **You are interviewing AI engineers who will earn more than $185K base.** This is the most visceral point. Use it plainly: *"I'm helping fill roles for individual contributors whose offers exceed my Director salary. That's a problem I need you to help me understand."*
4. **You work 60–80 hour weeks, have for 5 months.** Not as a complaint — as a descriptor of the current equilibrium. The implicit question: *"What does BayOne think it's buying from me, and what does it think it's paying for?"*

---

## What NOT to do in this meeting

- Don't send a written pre-read or agenda. This is a 1:1, not a board meeting. The conversation is the deliverable.
- Don't come in with a spreadsheet. Have the numbers in your head. Pulling up a document makes it feel transactional and lets him dismiss it as "prepared."
- Don't ask for advice. You are not confused about what's wrong. You know what the issue is. You are informing him and asking for his partnership to fix it.
- Don't let it become a venting session. If you catch yourself listing things, stop and re-anchor on the ask.
- Don't agree to "let me think about it" as the final outcome. Push for: *"When can we talk again about this, specifically?"* — and get a date.

---

## After the meeting

Whatever was said, send a short email to Yogesh within 24 hours that:
- Summarizes what you understood from the conversation
- Names the next step (who is doing what by when)
- Copies no one else (yet)

This converts a verbal conversation into a documented record without being aggressive about it. If he pushes back on the summary, you learn something about whether he's actually aligned. If he accepts it, you've established the record.
