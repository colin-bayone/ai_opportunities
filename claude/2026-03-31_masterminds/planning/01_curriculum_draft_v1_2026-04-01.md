# Curriculum Draft v1

**Topic:** Artificial Intelligence, Productivity, Growth, and Evolution
**Duration:** 45 minutes content + 15 minutes Q&A
**Audience:** Non-technical professionals (accounting, law, insurance, financial planning)

---

## Section 1. What AI Actually Is (10 min)

**Goal:** Build a mental model for the three branches of AI so they stop thinking "AI" means one thing.

### The Three Branches

**Generative AI and Language Models**
What most people think of when they hear "AI" today. Systems that generate text, images, code, or other content. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. Also includes the agentic frontier, where these systems can take actions and chain decisions together, not just answer questions.

Real examples they know:
- Drafting emails, summarizing meetings, rewriting documents
- Zox-style meeting summarization (relevant to the group without naming anyone)
- Legal research and brief drafting tools built on top of language models
- Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini as the "big three" consumer-facing tools

**Machine Learning**
Systems that learn patterns from historical data to make predictions or decisions. Not generative. These have been around for over a decade and are quietly embedded everywhere.

Real examples they know:
- Credit card fraud detection (flags unusual transactions in real time)
- Netflix and Spotify recommendations
- Email spam filtering
- Insurance risk scoring and underwriting models
- Financial forecasting and anomaly detection
- "People who bought X also bought Y" on Amazon

**Computer Vision**
Systems that interpret visual information. Often combined with ML. A distinct discipline with its own set of problems.

Real examples they know:
- Face unlock on their phone
- Tesla Autopilot / self-driving technology
- Medical imaging (detecting tumors, reading X-rays)
- Security cameras with person/vehicle detection
- Check deposit via phone camera (reading the check)
- License plate readers at toll booths

### Why This Matters

Knowing which branch you are dealing with changes the conversation entirely. A business owner asking "should I use AI?" is asking the wrong question until they know which kind of AI fits their problem. A recommendation engine is not the same tool as a document summarizer.

## Section 2. Productivity (15 min)

**Goal:** Show them how AI makes their specific work more efficient today. This is where it gets personal and practical.

### The 60/40 Framework

Every professional's work splits roughly into two categories:
- The required work (scheduling, note-taking, reporting, formatting, data entry, routine correspondence)
- The value work (judgment, counsel, relationship building, creative problem solving, client trust)

AI is best at absorbing the first category so professionals can spend more time on the second. This is not about replacement. This is about reallocation of time toward the work that actually matters and that clients pay a premium for.

### Industry Walk-Through

Walk through practical, real use cases across each discipline represented in the room. No hypotheticals. Things that work today.

**Accounting and Tax**
- Categorizing transactions from bank feeds
- Drafting client communication for recurring updates
- Summarizing tax code changes into plain language
- First-pass review of financial statements for anomalies

**Legal and Estate Planning**
- Document summarization (upload a 50-page trust, get a structured breakdown)
- Research acceleration (not replacing Lexis, augmenting it)
- Drafting routine correspondence and engagement letters
- Identifying relevant precedent from a constrained document set

**Insurance and Risk Management**
- Policy comparison across carriers
- Summarizing coverage gaps for client review
- Claims intake triage and initial assessment
- Generating renewal review summaries from prior-year data

**Financial Planning and Wealth Management**
- Post-meeting summaries and action item extraction
- Client communication drafts tailored to specific situations
- Research synthesis across fund families or market data
- Preparing review meeting agendas from CRM notes

### What Works and What Does Not (Yet)

Honest assessment. Where AI is reliable today:
- Summarization of content you provide to it
- Drafting from templates or structured inputs
- Reorganizing and reformatting information
- Brainstorming and ideation

Where AI still struggles:
- Domain-specific accuracy (insurance policy language, tax code nuance, legal citation)
- Math and numerical reasoning
- Anything requiring empathy, judgment, or client trust
- Open-ended internet research without guardrails

### The PII Question

Brief, practical guidance on when it is safe to put client information into AI tools and when it is not. Enterprise vs. consumer. What "enterprise" actually means in practice. Which tools are safe for what. No fearmongering, just clarity.

## Section 3. Growth (10 min)

**Goal:** Shift from efficiency to expansion. How AI helps practitioners serve more clients, enter new conversations, and increase the value of their practice.

### Capacity Expansion

If a typical advisor serves 125 households and AI reduces post-meeting work by 40%, that is not just time saved. That is capacity to serve 10-20 more households without adding staff. Same principle applies across every discipline in the room.

### Entering Conversations You Could Not Before

AI gives professionals the ability to quickly understand adjacent disciplines enough to ask better questions and hold smarter conversations. A financial planner who can speak intelligently about estate tax implications. An insurance broker who can reference accounting treatment of premiums. Not becoming an expert, but becoming conversant.

### The Cross-Discipline Opportunity

This ties directly back to the group's purpose. When every professional around the table is using AI to broaden their knowledge of adjacent fields, the referral quality improves. Instead of "you should talk to my accountant," it becomes a warm, specific handoff with context.

## Section 4. Evolution (10 min)

**Goal:** Where AI is headed in the next 12-18 months. Not science fiction. What is actually coming and what it means for professionals.

### Agentic AI

The next wave. Systems that do not just answer questions but take actions. Book the meeting, file the document, run the analysis, prepare the report. Moving from "assistant I ask" to "colleague that executes."

What this looks like in practice:
- An AI that monitors your inbox, drafts responses, and holds them for your approval
- Systems that prepare your client review package before you ask
- Tools that detect a life event (birth, death, sale) and trigger a workflow across multiple professionals

### The Legislation Landscape

Brief, practical overview of what is coming:
- AI governance frameworks are real and accelerating
- SOC 2, ISO 42001, ISO 27001 are the acronyms to know
- What this means for small firms vs. large firms
- Why this is actually good news (clarity enables confidence)

### What to Watch For

Three practical things to pay attention to over the next year:
- Tool consolidation (fewer, better tools rather than the current explosion)
- Industry-specific AI (tools built specifically for legal, insurance, finance, accounting)
- Integration over novelty (AI embedded in the tools you already use vs. standalone apps)
