## 1. The Founding Vision for Cross-Discipline Collaboration

### 1.1 Zac's Framing of the Group's Purpose

Zac (Saunders, Confluence wealth manager) laid out the genesis of the group explicitly as a collaboration-first model, deliberately distinguished from traditional networking groups:

> "Phil and I a while ago got together and said, hey, what would be a nice idea to continue something on a monthly basis where there could be a level of collaboration, a group of professionals that could learn from one another, be a trusted resource in different disciplines, whether it's legal in a state work, whether it's insurance, or it's artificial intelligence, whether it's accounting, whether it's financial planning, and maybe that's just the initial core."

Zac explicitly positioned this as **not** a BNI-style referral group:

> "Not your traditional BNI group, Derek and I talked about that, I know they could, even Ryan and I talked about that."

He articulated a two-tier value proposition:
1. **Primary:** Growth-minded professionals learning from each other, having trusted resources to call on across disciplines ("you just have somebody you know you can just shoot an email")
2. **Secondary:** Organic referral opportunities when appropriate ("if there's an opportunity to print something")

Key constraint Zac placed on referrals:

> "There's no mandate like, oh, every meeting you that come with a name, no, that's not necessarily the genesis of all this."

### 1.2 Phil's Reinforcement

Phil (Sardis, CPA) echoed the positioning:

> "Try to keep it a small group of trusted professionals that we can just kind of rely on. If there's a referral on the back end, great. If not, not really a big deal. That's not really the point of this. It is more of the development and to actually get better at what the heck we're doing."

### 1.3 Zac's Proposed Meeting Structure for Knowledge Transfer

Zac outlined a phased approach to cross-discipline education:

- **Phase 1 (Early meetings):** Each member "owns" a meeting to share insights about their discipline, how they practice, what their firm does. He gave explicit examples: "Hey Derek, you're the owner of that meeting. Share some insights about what you do, how you do it from a firm perspective, from a practitioner's perspective. You know, maybe the next meeting is focused more on the legal estate planning, AI."
- **Phase 2 (Months 6-7+):** Either bring in outside speakers with specific expertise, or the group collectively tackles a topic scenario. He gave the scenario example: "Hey, here's a topic, you know, helping business owners that are transitioning to business or exiting and selling. How does it impact insurance? How does it impact the state? How does it impact accounting? How does it impact technology or AI? Or what are some things we can learn from each other in terms of adding value?"

## 3. Tax Code Changes as a Cross-Discipline Catalyst

### 3.1 Zac on the "One Big Beautiful Bill"

Zac raised the pending federal tax legislation ("one big beautiful bill") as a concrete example of how external changes ripple across every discipline:

> "As you mentioned the tax code, like one big beautiful bill, what are the implications for that? Four, someone that's 65 and older versus someone that's obviously looking at AGI. We still don't know."

He acknowledged the uncertainty:

> "I mean the changes that have come from... It's wide sweeping and it affects probably everybody in this room and how we're going to talk to clients."

And pointed to the planning impact:

> "Things that we've done for clients over the last couple of years that maybe they're no longer worried about. And how do we bring them up, put them in something that's more suitable moving forward."

### 3.2 Ryan's Detailed Estate Planning Complexities

Ryan (Brody, estate planning attorney at Strasburg McKenna Gutmaking Gefsky) provided the most technically detailed illustration of how tax law changes create cross-discipline planning needs:

> "We thought that the federal estate tax exclusion was going to sunset and be cut in half. And we're looking at $7 million exemptions per individual. That's different planning than if it's 15. Your unified credit with your spouse is 30 versus 14."

He then described a specific client scenario where the planning calculus has shifted:

> "We've had clients ask, they've done Hupert's. They want to roll out at some of these things because they're just not there. They're not going to get there. Their retirement age and net worth isn't going to... skyrocket at this point."

*Note: "Hupert's" is likely a transcription of "HEMS trusts" (Health, Education, Maintenance, Support) or possibly "QPRT" (Qualified Personal Residence Trust), but in context appears to refer to irrevocable trust structures designed for estate tax avoidance.*

Ryan articulated the fundamental trade-off clients face:

> "So it becomes a discussion of do we want a step up in basis or do we want to avoid federal estate tax? And you can't have both. What's better for me now?"

And then the overarching observation about how legislation drives planning across disciplines:

> "But it's crazy how one act changes the landscape and the outlook for so many different people."

**Assessment:** Ryan's explanation is a textbook example of the cross-discipline value this group can provide. The step-up-in-basis vs. estate-tax-avoidance trade-off touches:
- **Estate Attorney (Ryan):** Drafting and unwinding trust structures
- **CPA (Phil):** Capital gains tax implications of step-up vs. carryover basis
- **Wealth Manager (Zac):** Investment and financial planning recalibration
- **Insurance (Derek):** Life insurance policy adjustments tied to estate plans
- **AI/ Tech (Colin):** Potentially modeling scenarios or building planning tools

- **Colin** sees the client through the lens of **operational efficiency and technology risk** -- how AI can make businesses more productive, what regulatory frameworks are emerging, how to separate signal from noise in tech adoption

## 12. The Value Proposition Beyond Individual Practices

The group collectively articulated several dimensions of value that transcend what any member provides individually:

1. **Trusted referral network:** Not a volume play, but a quality play. Zac: "I know exactly how they handle clients and business. And if I have a certain business client that would be a good fit, and hey, I'm going to bring his name up when it's appropriate."

2. **Cross-discipline sophistication:** Derek's point that understanding adjacent fields makes him a better advisor in his own field, which then generates referral opportunities naturally.

3. **Rapid-access expertise:** The "shoot an email" model. When a question arises in one discipline that touches another, having a known, trusted contact eliminates the search-and-vet process.

4. **Client protection through coordination:** The "protecting from BS" ethos means that when a client is referred within the group, all members can have confidence the client will receive ethical, competent advice.

5. **Environmental scanning across disciplines:** Tax code changes, AI regulation, insurance market shifts, estate planning law evolution -- each member tracks their own domain, but the group creates a shared intelligence feed across all domains.

6. **The "financial quarterback" model formalized:** Zac described already functioning as a coordinator across client advisors. This group formalizes and strengthens the bench of professionals available for that coordination.
