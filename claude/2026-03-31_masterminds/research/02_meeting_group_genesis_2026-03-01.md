## 1. Zac's Opening Remarks: The Founding Narrative

Zac opens the meeting by framing it explicitly as "really just an informal kickoff meeting." He immediately credits Phil as co-originator of the idea:

> "Phil and I a while ago got together and said, hey, what would be a nice idea to continue something on a monthly basis where there could be a level of collaboration, a group of professionals that could learn from one another, be a trusted resource in different disciplines."

He then enumerates the disciplines he envisions in the group: "whether it's legal in a state work, whether it's insurance, or it's artificial intelligence, whether it's accounting, whether it's financial planning." He frames these as "the initial core" and immediately opens the door to future expansion: "Maybe there are some other folks that we might want to consider in the near future."

### What This Is NOT

Zac makes a deliberate point of distancing the group from BNI (Business Network International), mentioning he has had this specific conversation with multiple members already:

> "Not your traditional BNI group. Derek and I talked about that, I know they could, even Ryan and I talked about that."

This is a significant framing choice. BNI groups are structured around mandatory referral quotas and weekly meetings with rigid obligations. Zac is explicitly rejecting that model.

### The Dual Purpose (Development First, Referrals Second)

Zac articulates a clear hierarchy of purpose:

**Primary purpose:** Professional development and trusted resource network.
- "Folks that are growth minded, that want to continuously be getting better"
- "How can we learn from one another, how can we trust resources"
- Having someone "you know you can just shoot an email" to when a professional question comes up in your work
- He gives an example: "I wonder if Brian knows the answer to this or how would you view this from a legal perspective, any insights"

**Secondary purpose:** Referrals as a natural byproduct, not a mandate.
- "Really secondary of all that is potentially is okay, is there an opportunity from a growth standpoint that you know, we can introduce or refer folks to each other if there's a good fit."
- He explicitly rejects any referral quota: "There's no mandate like, oh, every meeting you that come with a name, no, that's not necessarily the genesis of all this."
- Referrals should be organic and trust-based: "I know exactly how they handle clients and business. And if I have a certain business client that would be a good fit, and hey, I'm going to bring his name up when it's appropriate."

### Humility and Collaborative Ownership

Zac frames the meeting agenda as a starting point, not a directive:

> "This is just kind of a template to guide our first meeting in terms of conversation. Nothing is really set in stone."

He explicitly positions himself as a facilitator, not a unilateral decision-maker:

> "Candidly, I don't have all the answers nor this is really about the group collectively having input and deciding as a group, hey, what we think this should look like."

He also solicits input on group composition: "I don't want this to be a group of 30 people. If we still want to keep it somewhat tight, less than 9 or 10, who are some other folks who we may want to consider don't need answers today."

**Key number:** Zac envisions a cap of fewer than 9 or 10 members total. At the kickoff meeting, there are 5 members present (Zac, Phil, Colin, Derek, Ryan), with a sixth (Chris Slater, described as Phil's "other associate") arriving during the meeting.

## 2. Phil's Reinforcement of the Vision

When Zac asks Phil to add to the genesis framing, Phil's response is brief but confirms the same hierarchy:

> "I think you laid that out pretty well. To try to keep it a small group of trusted professionals that we can just kind of rely on. If there's a referral on the back end, great. If not, not really a big deal. That's not really the point of this. It is more of the development and to actually get better at what the heck we're doing."

Phil's language is notably more casual and blunt than Zac's. "Not really a big deal" and "get better at what the heck we're doing" strip away any pretense. The emphasis is unmistakable: development is the point, referrals are incidental.

## 3. The Concept of Themed / Owned Meetings

Phil introduces the meeting structure concept in detail. This appears to be something he and Zac have already discussed ("what we, preliminarily, and I've talked to each of you on this"):

> "It may mean, in maybe the first couple meetings, it might mean, hey Derek, you're the owner of that meeting. Share some insights about what you do, how you do it from a firm perspective, from a practitioner's perspective."

He lists examples of future meeting topics by discipline: "Maybe the next meeting is focused more on the legal estate planning, AI."

### The Progression Model

Phil describes an evolution of meeting formats over time:

1. **Early months (months 1-5ish):** Members take turns owning meetings, sharing about their discipline and how they practice.
2. **Later months (month 6-7):** Transition to bringing in outside speakers with expertise on specific topics.
3. **Scenario-based workshops:** Phil also introduces the idea of cross-disciplinary topic meetings: "Here's a topic, you know, helping business owners that are transitioning to business or exiting and selling. How does it impact insurance? How does it impact the state? How does it impact accounting? How does it impact technology or AI?"

This progression model is significant: it starts with building internal understanding and trust (learning each other's disciplines), then graduates to external knowledge acquisition (outside speakers), and at various points includes collaborative scenario-based exercises.

## 4. Colin's AI / Technology Role as the Unique Differentiator

Phil explicitly calls out the AI presence as what makes this group distinctive:

> "I'm kind of interested, I think the unique thing is a lot of the disciplines around the room are generally pretty consistent based on servicing a certain type of client. But I also think it's interesting in having the AI piece and having something with a technology background."

This is a notable framing. Phil acknowledges that law, insurance, accounting, and financial planning are "generally pretty consistent" in terms of servicing overlapping client bases. The AI and technology discipline is the outlier, the thing that makes the group composition non-obvious. Phil sees this as an asset, not a mismatch.

### Colin's Self-Framing of His Role

Later in the meeting, Colin describes how he sees his role in the group and what he hopes to contribute and gain:

**What Colin brings:**
- Grounding the group on AI reality vs. hype: "AI is probably the most BS-prone of a field that's going on right now."
- Practical knowledge of which AI tools are trustworthy (especially regarding PII concerns): "I can at least help by telling you which ones are okay and which ones aren't."
- Understanding AI legislation and compliance frameworks (SOC 2, ISO 42001, ISO 27001) and how they affect businesses of all sizes.
- A bridge between corporate-scale and small business application: "For me, interesting to see how those two things play out. This smaller business and much larger business, because I think both can learn from each other in a lot of ways."

**What Colin wants to learn:**
- Industry-specific use cases and patterns: "How do we know when something's a good fit if we don't know the industry? We can't even touch it."
- He frames the consulting industry's problem: "We are very hammer-looking for now. Like, that's just how it is." (i.e., every problem looks like a nail when you're selling AI solutions)
- Understanding the real-world workflows of these professionals so AI solutions can be properly matched to actual needs.

### Zac's Response to the AI Value Proposition

Zac validates Colin's role enthusiastically and articulates a concrete personal interest:

> "Just being somewhat naive, but also I don't know what I don't know. Like the AI thing in itself, how it impacts each one of our listeners, or how it's going to evolve over time."

Zac rejects the fear narrative: "Not being naive to say hey, I think it's gonna replace me but I'm saying the really great advisors that use it as a tool to make me more productive or provide growth."

He then provides a detailed example of his firm's AI exploration: he sits on both the investment advisory committee and a newly formed AI committee (with Gregory Weimer and four other associates). He describes a tool called "Zox" (likely Zocks or similar), an AI voice-to-text tool for financial services that converts review meeting conversations into structured summaries. Key detail: instead of spending 1.5 hours post-meeting on notes, advisors spend about 25 minutes.

Zac even frames a business case for AI adoption: "If a typical advisor serves 125 households, by utilizing technology to be more productive, maybe they can service 150 households."

He also floats the idea that insights from the group could spark product ideas: "There might be ideas that come from this group that give you a little fuel and you create something internally with Bay One."

## 5. What Each Member Hopes to Accomplish

When Zac asks what members hope to get from the group, the responses reinforce the development-first thesis:

### Derek (Insurance)

Derek articulates the value of cross-disciplinary knowledge for his own practice:

> "There are so many different things changing in the marketplace, not from an insurance, but on the other industries that we directly work with. So like tax code and things of that nature. I think like hearing more from the professionals and like your estate planning and how those things coordinate together."

He frames it as a competitive advantage: "If I can seem more sophisticated, I'm going to be a real more well-rounded risk advisor. And that also opens the door for you guys."

Derek also proposes the small business transition scenario as a concrete workshop exercise:

> "The next five to ten years is going to be like the largest transfer of wealth in the history of the United States because the Boomers are small businesses and they're retiring and they have no plan on what's going to happen. Is kid in the business or kid doesn't want the business and do we have a key employee and maybe working through that sort of as a group might be a good exercise."

### Colin (AI / Technology)

Colin frames his two-sided interest:

1. **Learning industry patterns** so he can apply AI solutions more intelligently rather than being a "hammer looking for a nail."
2. **Serving as an AI reality check** for the group: debunking hype, explaining what's real, sharing what's happening at the corporate level.

He also makes a notable observation about large vs. small companies: "One thing that I learned as I got into bigger and bigger companies is no one has their crap together. Everyone's the guy chasing around. You can be at Apple's campus, and there's still someone looking not for an HDMI cable, I guess, but for a lightning cable around a conference room."

## 6. The AI Discussion as a Window into Group Dynamics

The AI conversation that unfolds mid-meeting is worth documenting because it reveals how the group naturally operates. What starts as a formal agenda item becomes an organic, wide-ranging discussion that touches on:

- **Ryan** shares his firm's experience with Lexis Nexis AI for legal research and brief writing, including the disciplinary risks of lawyers submitting AI-generated content without verification.
- **Zac** adds that on the financial side, AI is "bad at math" but "unbelievable" at summarizing uploaded documents.
- **Ryan** notes AI works well in "closed circuit" scenarios (constrained data sets) vs. open internet scouring.
- **Phil** mentions using Claude to try to build Outlook tracking systems for his practice.
- **Derek** shares his firm's attempts to use AI for insurance policy reviews, noting persistent errors: "Even though we're uploading it and including the entire policy, there's still some errors." He also mentions his wife at Blank Rome using AI to build client-facing portals for large litigation case management.

### Colin's Grounding Role in Action

During this discussion, Colin naturally falls into the "grounding" role Phil and Zac envisioned for him:

- Reframes AI as "just a tool" and dismisses the "super intelligent, sentient, terminator thing" as "complete fabrication for marketing hype."
- Pushes back on replacement fears: "You'll never hear me saying people are going to get replaced by AI. I think people that fail to adapt to it will, in the same way that there's no one that lights gas lamps on the streets anymore."
- Contextualizes the hallucination problem: "Have you used Google? People act like this is a spouting dragon over here."
- Offers practical guidance on PII concerns: "I can at least help by telling you which ones are okay and which ones aren't."
- Introduces the regulatory/compliance dimension (SOC 2, ISO 42001, ISO 27001) as both a challenge and an opportunity.

### Zac's PII Caution

Zac raises a significant concern about PII in AI tools, even in enterprise deployments:

> "Unless it's an enterprise system, generally speaking. And I use that term loosely because you know what that means... But there's even a sense of cautious internally with us of putting any PII information that's even in the enterprise system until we fully understand what this really works."

He clarifies their current use is "mostly general" and "theoretical" with no client-specific data going into AI tools. Colin offers to help the group navigate which tools are safe and which aren't.

## 7. Mission Statement Discussion

Zac presents three draft principles for the group's mission:

1. "A group of trusted professionals, your growth mind, want to improve personally and professionally."
2. "Experts in their respective discipline who can serve as a trusted resource and can learn from one another."
3. "Provide colleagues with introductions, referrals, that's just it."

### The "Protecting Clients from BS" Thread

Derek introduces a phrase that resonates with the group. Zac had earlier used the framing (about his own firm): "I think half of what we do, candidly, is protecting people from all the other BS that's out there."

When the mission statement comes up, Ryan picks up on this:

> "I liked where you were going before... exactly. We've spoken about this before and for the protecting your clients and your customers from all the other bullshit that's out there. And I think that's sort of like, that's an ethical obligation."

Ryan suggests "trusted" covers this, but the group considers whether it should be more explicit. Derek jokes: "I think we should add to cover the bullshit part. Protecting clients from the other bullshit. Put that on a t-shirt."

Zac reframes it as: "There's a lot of noise out there."

This exchange is notable because it surfaces an implicit fourth principle beyond the three Zac drafted: the group sees itself as a bulwark of professional ethics and quality in a marketplace full of bad actors, bad advice, and noise. This is not formalized in the mission statement but clearly animates the group's sense of purpose.

## 8. Group Expansion Criteria and Philosophy

### Size Constraints

Zac states the target: "Less than 9 or 10" total members. Currently at 5 core members (plus Chris Slater as Phil's associate).

### Selection Philosophy

Derek articulates a philosophy of intentional, curated membership near the end of the meeting:

> "This was a very sought- thought over, considered group. Anybody could be, you could call any brokerage and they'd send anybody. So you have to take a little bit of pride of being in this room together and being aligned with professionals like you."

He frames membership as an honor and a mutual commitment: "You guys is just gonna expedite any growth that I have and it's the same thing with me. We now can be connected in that sense."

He ties this back to the attendance expectation: "That's why attendance, that's a given. Anybody else could have filled these seats, but we're in here together, so I think honored that a little bit."

### Implicit Criteria

Though never formally stated as a checklist, the transcript reveals several implicit criteria for group membership:

1. **Discipline uniqueness:** Each member represents a distinct professional discipline. The group is designed so disciplines don't overlap (no two financial planners, no two attorneys, etc.).
2. **Client base overlap:** The disciplines should share a common client base (small business owners, affluent families, professionals) so cross-pollination is natural.
3. **Character and trust:** Zac's introduction of each member emphasizes personal knowledge and long-standing relationships. He tells the group about Ryan: "He's always been an upstanding man," and later says of Derek: "I've gotten to know Derek over the years. I know exactly how they handle clients and business."
4. **Growth orientation:** Repeatedly emphasized. Members should be "growth minded" and wanting "to continuously be getting better."
5. **Practitioners, not corporate representatives:** Everyone in the room is a practitioner or principal in their firm, not a middle manager sent by a larger organization. This appears deliberate but is never explicitly stated as criteria.

## 10. Tensions and Unresolved Points

### The AI Member's Fit

While Phil explicitly calls Colin's AI/technology background "the unique thing" and "interesting," there is an implicit tension in the group's composition. Every other member (financial planning, law, insurance, accounting) serves a highly overlapping client base of affluent individuals and small business owners. Colin's work at BayOne is primarily enterprise-level (Cisco, eBay, Quest Diagnostics, Sephora, Home Depot). He acknowledges this himself: "We are less so in the truly financial services industry, but more with like a CFO inside of a bigger organization."

Colin bridges this gap by:
- Framing his role as educational/advisory on AI for the group's benefit
- Expressing genuine interest in learning from their industries
- Noting that principles transfer between large and small business contexts

The group appears to fully embrace this, but it's worth flagging that Colin's professional client base is fundamentally different from the others'. His value to the group is as a technology/AI subject matter expert and sounding board, not as a referral partner in the traditional sense.

### Referrals vs. Development: Truly Resolved?

While everyone verbally agrees that development comes first and referrals are secondary, the conversation naturally drifts toward referral-adjacent topics multiple times. Derek's comment about learning other disciplines specifically notes "that also opens the door for you guys" (i.e., leads to referrals). Zac's entire framing of the Derek relationship is referral-based: "If I have a certain business client that would be a good fit, I'm going to bring his name up."

This is not necessarily a contradiction. It may be more accurate to say the group rejects the *mechanics* of BNI (mandatory referral tracking, quotas, formalized lead passing) while embracing the *outcome* (natural referrals flowing from trust and familiarity). The distinction is one of structure and pressure, not of goals.

### Group Size and Future Additions

Zac mentions wanting to stay under 9-10 members but explicitly tables the conversation about who to add: "Don't need answers today. That's something for us to think about in the future." No specific candidates are discussed. No criteria for new disciplines are articulated beyond the implicit pattern (unique discipline, shared client base, personal trust).

### Formality Level

There's an unresolved question about how formal or informal the group will be. Zac presents a written agenda and mission statement principles, suggesting some structure. But the meeting itself is extremely casual (food ordering interruptions, jokes about lost wedding rings, swearing). The group seems to naturally lean informal, but the structural elements (attendance expectations, owned meetings, mission statement) suggest a desire for accountability. This tension is productive rather than problematic, but it's unresolved as of this meeting.
