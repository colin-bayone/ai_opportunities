## What We're Looking For

This role is about finding someone who can be the Director of AI's right hand. The person we hire will run the AI engineering team day-to-day, handle problems independently, and free up the Director to focus on higher-level concerns. If this person can't make the Director's life easier, they're not the right fit.

We need a **hands-on technical leader**, not a pure people manager. This person should still be close to the code. They should want to get in the trenches with their team, work through hard problems together, and contribute directly when needed. If someone says "I don't code anymore" or treats engineering as beneath them, they're not who we're looking for.

We need someone with **recent AI leadership experience**. This is critical. We're not interested in people who led teams in other domains and want to pivot to AI, or people whose AI work was years ago. Their most recent role should be leading an AI team. Three or more years of that experience is the baseline.

We need a **builder**. The right person is excited about building production solutions, growing a team, and expanding BayOne's AI portfolio. They're not looking to maintain the status quo or coast on past accomplishments. They want to create something.

We need someone with **good judgment on when to escalate**. This person will make many decisions independently, and that's expected. But they also need to recognize when something is significant enough to bring to the Director. We don't want someone who escalates everything (that defeats the purpose), and we don't want someone who goes rogue and makes major decisions without input.

Finally, we need **technical breadth**. The AI field is wide, and we need someone who has depth in multiple areas, not just one specialty. This could be LLMs and agentic AI, machine learning and data engineering, computer vision, infrastructure, or modern tooling. At minimum, two of these areas should be strong.

This person will be the **bridge between the India engineering team and US leadership**. They need to operate effectively across time zones and keep both sides aligned without requiring constant check-ins.

## Hard Filters

These are immediate disqualifiers. If a candidate does not meet these criteria, do not advance them.

| Requirement | How to Verify |
|-------------|---------------|
| **3+ years leading an AI team** | Must be on resume. If they led a non-AI team, or this wasn't their most recent role, disqualify. |
| **Most recent role was AI leadership** | Check resume chronology. If their last role was IC, a different domain, or they've been out of leadership for 2+ years, disqualify. |
| **Python as primary language** | Ask directly. If they say Java, C++, or "I manage engineers who write Python," disqualify. They must code in Python themselves. |
| **Azure experience** | Must have hands-on Azure experience, not just AWS or GCP. |
| **~8 years total experience** | Verify from resume. This is a guideline, not a hard cutoff. Adjacent fields include statistics, data science, data engineering, ML engineering, or software engineering with AI focus. A typical strong profile shows progression: statistics or mathematics background into data engineering or data science, then into ML, then into AI leadership. |
| **At least 1 year in AI leadership** | Within their 3+ years of leadership, at least one full year should be at a single organization. This shows another company trusted them with that responsibility. |

## Technical Depth Evaluation

The candidate should demonstrate depth in **at least two** of these areas. More is better. This is assessed in the technical interview, but recruiters should look for evidence of these on the resume.

Budget flexibility correlates with technical breadth. Candidates who check more boxes here warrant consideration at the higher end of the compensation range.

### 1. Agentic AI and Large Language Models
- Has built production systems using LLMs
- Understands prompt engineering, context management, output parsing
- Experience with agent frameworks (LangGraph, LangChain, AutoGen, etc.)
- Knows limitations and failure modes of LLMs

### 2. Machine Learning, Statistical Modeling, and Data Engineering
- Has trained and deployed ML models
- Understands data pipelines, feature engineering, model evaluation
- Experience with frameworks like PyTorch, TensorFlow, scikit-learn
- Knows MLOps practices

### 3. Computer Vision and Manufacturing Applications
- Has worked with image/video processing
- Experience with CV frameworks (OpenCV, YOLO, etc.)
- Understands real-world deployment challenges (edge, latency, hardware)

### 4. AI Infrastructure and Production Deployment
- Has built infrastructure for AI workloads
- Understands GPU provisioning, model serving, scaling
- Experience with containerization, orchestration, monitoring

### 5. Modern AI Tooling and Frameworks
- Current with latest tools (not just legacy approaches)
- Uses AI-assisted development (Claude Code, Cursor, Copilot)
- Familiar with modern orchestration, vector databases, etc.

## Candidate Evaluation Notes

When evaluating candidates, keep the following in mind:

- When candidates describe their work, pay attention to whether they speak in terms of their personal contributions or only in terms of what "the team" accomplished. Strong candidates can articulate both.
- Candidates who take time to think through problems, sketch things out, or ask clarifying questions show good instincts. Rushed, surface-level answers are a concern.
- Someone held back by their organization isn't at fault. If their recent work and attitude are strong, don't penalize them for lack of opportunity at a previous employer.
- Versatility is a positive signal. Candidates who have held different types of roles demonstrate adaptability.

## Leadership Signals

### What We Want

| Signal | How It Shows Up |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Builder mindset** | Talks about what they want to create, not just maintain. Excited about growing a team and portfolio. |
| **Gets in the trenches** | Recent examples of hands-on contribution. Doesn't see coding as beneath them. |
| **Independent but aligned** | Makes decisions confidently. Also knows when to check in. Has examples of both. |
| **Earns respect through competence** | Engineers they've managed would vouch for their technical judgment. |
| **Develops people** | Specific examples of mentoring, growing engineers, promotions they enabled. |
| **Organized and detail-oriented** | Tracks projects methodically. Doesn't let things slip through cracks. |

### Red Flags

| Red Flag | What It Sounds Like |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Hands-off manager** | "I set direction and let my team execute." No recent coding examples. |
| **Claims team accomplishments** | When asked about their work, they only describe what their team did. Unable to articulate their own personal contributions. |
| **All talk, no build** | Lots of strategy, no shipped products. Can't describe concrete systems. |
| **Goes rogue** | "I just made the call and told my manager later." No examples of seeking input. |
| **Over-escalates** | "I always loop in leadership to be safe." No examples of independent judgment. |
| **Dated experience** | Most relevant AI work was 3+ years ago. Recent roles are adjacent but not AI. |
| **Title-focused** | More interested in the title than the work. Asks about direct reports before asking about the mission. |
| **Super senior misfit** | 25+ years experience but recent work isn't relevant. Looking to coast, not build. |
| **High rate, low involvement** | Candidates asking for top-of-range compensation but whose experience suggests they've moved away from hands-on work. |
