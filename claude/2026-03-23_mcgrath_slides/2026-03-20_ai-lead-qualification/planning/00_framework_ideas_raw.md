## Idea 1: Standard Information Handoff and Tracking System

**What:** Clearly define what information must be handed off from sales, and where it gets tracked and captured.

**Where to track:** Salesforce. Not emails, not Excel sheets, not Teams chats. Salesforce is already set up for this purpose. Any other tools (Anand's meeting planner, intake forms, etc.) feed INTO Salesforce. They are entry points, not the source of truth.

**Rationale:**
- Emails are fine for quick messages but are not a formal tracking system
- The longer we avoid using tools already set up to do the job, the more we'll create custom workarounds that make things worse
- Change management is happening anyway, so better to fix this from the start
- An Excel sheet would make an already bad problem worse by creating yet another unstructured system
- Leverage what already exists internally rather than inventing new tracking

**Additional benefits of Salesforce as single source of truth:**
- Enables transparency and accountability across the org
- Enables visibility via standard reporting tools, including Salesforce's built-in reporting (not being used today)
- Reporting exists out of the box, is readily available, and easily accessible
- Eliminates the need for custom reporting solutions

## Idea 2: Decision-Making Power Qualification

**What:** Does the person sales is talking to have decision-making authority that could fund work for BayOne?

**Rationale:** This is a fundamental sales question. It must be known before escalation. A senior engineering manager or a mid-level director who can't sign contracts or allocate budget is not enough to justify technical involvement.

---

## Idea 3: Financial Qualification / Opportunity Sizing

**What:** Even if the client is interested, is there actual project revenue here? What kind of opportunity is this for BayOne? How much is it worth?

**Purpose:**
- Instantly filter out "little fish" that don't justify the investment of time
- Not just a yes/no on whether money exists, but a sizing of the opportunity
- Prevents the logo-hunting behavior where every potential MSA gets the same treatment as a million-dollar engagement

---

## Idea 4: Priority Assessment (With Nuance)

**What:** Priority is tricky to hard-code because sometimes lower-value work creates strategic inroads. But that logic has guardrails.

**When strategic inroads justify lower-value work:**
- There's a broader scope vision with tangible evidence of more opportunity
- The inroad leads to something concrete and verifiable, not hypothetical

**When strategic inroads do NOT justify it:**
- A manager-level person (no strategic power) says "we'd definitely be interested in more" - that doesn't count
- The "could lead to" justification without evidence of bigger opportunity
- No decision maker in the conversation who can actually expand the relationship

**Key distinction:** Strategic value must be backed by tangible indicators, not speculation from someone without authority.

## Idea 5: Standard Communication Protocol and Meeting Discipline

### Communication:
- Stop creating Teams chats with 30 people copied for visibility
- Information should be in Salesforce so it's asynchronously visible to everyone who needs it
- Teams chats and emails for quick coordination, not for opportunity tracking

### Meeting discipline:

**Pre-meeting requirements:**
- Do not schedule meetings without confirming internal availability first
- Do not schedule meetings without confirming the opportunity has been qualified
- It is not the technical team's job to save the day because sales didn't finish their job
- Meetings must be put on the calendar at the time they are requested (not minutes before they happen)

**Minimum notice:**
- Bare minimum 48-72 hours weekday notice for the technical team to prep for a client meeting
- Once a meeting is set, it's set. A new meeting cannot overrule an existing one just because a higher-level leader happens to be available. That requires a prioritization discussion.

**Internal pre-meeting and post-meeting:**
- There should always be an internal pre-meeting with all people who will be on the client call
- There should always be an after-the-meeting discussion
- Sales is responsible for recording these interactions. The AI team can help enable it, but the business ownership of recording customer interactions falls to sales.

**Core principle:** Don't try to schedule a meeting without asking people first. Don't assume someone will drop everything to join because you requested it. Calls should be with purpose, and calls should happen when ready. If information hasn't been collected, there's no point in a call, even internally.

## Idea 6: Client Meeting Staffing and Role Clarity

### Anchor person:
- Every engagement must have one person responsible for the whole engagement from the business side (start to finish)
- This person owns the relationship and the continuity

### Technical involvement:
- Technical team is brought in when the conversation gets technical
- This is a firm boundary

### What this does NOT mean:
- Sales does NOT get people on the phone and then hand off to the technical team
- Sales must be able to fundamentally do qualification themselves
- If sales cannot do qualification, they cannot be selling AI products. Period.

---

## Idea 7: No Promises Without Technical Team Approval

**What:** Any promises for demos, case studies, capabilities, or deliverables must be requested from the AI team IN ADVANCE of telling the customer.

**The language shift:**
- WRONG: "We have a chatbot demo, let me set up a call with our AI team to show you"
- RIGHT: "Let me check with the technical team and we'll get back to you"

**Simple as that.** No commitments to clients about what the technical team will present, deliver, or demo without checking first.

**Enforcement (dual-layer):**
1. **Policy from leadership.** This should be a formal, leadership-endorsed policy. Not just Colin's preference, but an organizational standard.
2. **Personal enforcement via leverage.** Colin will not provide slides, support, or technical participation unless the process was followed. This is the cleanest change management: follow the process, or it doesn't work.

**Tone:** Make it as easy as possible to follow. Don't sound severe. But be firm and set the boundary. Even if people are slow to adopt, the reality is they won't get help in the meantime. The process protects everyone.
