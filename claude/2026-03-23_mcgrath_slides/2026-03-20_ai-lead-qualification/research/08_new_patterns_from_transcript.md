# New Patterns Identified from Transcript

Beyond the five patterns already documented in the skill playbook, the transcript reveals several additional patterns and deeper problems.

---

## Pattern 6: Fabricating the State of an Opportunity

**What happens:** Sales presents an opportunity to leadership and to Colin as being further along than it actually is. Known work streams are claimed when none exist. Client interest is asserted when it hasn't been confirmed. When the team shows up to the meeting, the client has no idea what they're talking about.

**Source incidents:** Tailored Brands (Rima told the CEO there were known work streams; the client wasn't ready to discuss anything)

**Why it's dangerous:** This isn't just wasting Colin's time. It's wasting the CEO's time, making BayOne look disorganized to clients, and building fake pipeline that leadership might mistake for real business.

**Root cause:** Salespeople are under pressure to show activity and pipeline. Fabricating the state of an opportunity creates urgency and gets leadership attention, even if there's nothing behind it.

---

## Pattern 7: Fabricated Case Studies

**What happens:** Sales creates case studies using ChatGPT that are not based on actual completed work. Staffing engagements are reframed as solutions engagements. Case studies are generated for work that hasn't been finished yet, or work that was done at Colin's prior company (not BayOne).

**Source incidents:** Tailored Brands follow-up (Colin flagged to CTO that case studies are being fabricated), the broader conversation with Surej/leadership

**Why it's dangerous:** If a client digs into a fabricated case study and asks for details, BayOne cannot defend it. This destroys trust and credibility. Colin's quote: "Not an if, but a when."

**Root cause:** BayOne is early in its AI practice and doesn't have many completed AI solutions projects. Sales feels pressure to present a fuller portfolio than exists. Rather than owning the gap honestly, they fill it with AI-generated content.

---

## Pattern 8: Persistent Re-engagement After Being Told No

**What happens:** After Colin pushes back on an opportunity or declines involvement, the salesperson continues finding new angles to pull him back in. Each message reframes the situation slightly to make it sound like Colin needs to be involved.

**Source incidents:** Tailored Brands (Rima kept coming back with "they want to hear about AI," "some customers don't have pain points yet," "we could still help")

**Why it's problematic:** This creates an infinite loop where Colin has to keep pushing back on the same opportunity. It's exhausting and makes Colin look like the difficult one when in reality the salesperson won't accept the answer.

**Root cause:** The salesperson doesn't want to go back empty-handed. They'd rather keep trying to pull Colin in than admit there's nothing there.

---

## Pattern 9: No Minimum Information Provided

**What happens:** Colin is asked to join a meeting with nothing more than a job title and a time slot. No company name, no problem statement, no context. A "calendar invite with vibes."

**Source incidents:** Han's request (VP of Global Marketing Ops, 9am Monday, "share your perspective on AI and data analytics")

**Why it's problematic:** Colin cannot assess whether his time is justified without basic information. The sales team is treating his calendar as a free resource that doesn't need justification.

---

## Pattern 10: Sales Creating Work Without Capacity to Deliver

**What happens:** Sales volunteers deliverables (case studies, presentations, POVs, use case documents) on client calls without checking whether anyone has the capacity to produce them. Colin then gets asked to create materials for commitments he didn't make.

**Source incidents:** Tailored Brands meeting (case studies for unfinished work volunteered), broader capacity conversation

**Why it's problematic:** This creates unfunded mandates. Someone promises the client they'll get X, and then Colin has to either produce X on top of his actual workload or be the person who says no.

---

## Pattern 11: Dodging Direct Questions

**What happens:** When challenged by someone with authority (CTO, technical lead), the salesperson deflects with vague responses like "looking forward to discussing further" or reframes their original ask as if it was always what they meant.

**Source incidents:** Rima dodging Priya's direct question about retail experience; Rima's "that's all I was saying too" after being corrected

**Why it matters for the framework:** This pattern means that verbal commitments from sales about what a meeting is about cannot be trusted. The framework needs written documentation of meeting scope and objectives, not verbal assurances.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### The "Little Red Hen" Problem
Colin's metaphor: he's being asked to identify leads, qualify opportunities, join discovery calls, do the solutioning, and deliver. If he's doing all of that, what is the sales team's function?

### AI Literacy Gap
The sales team doesn't understand AI well enough to:
- Qualify whether an opportunity is realistic
- Set appropriate client expectations
- Distinguish between staffing engagements and solutions work
- Recognize when a LinkedIn post is AI-generated
- Have a basic conversation about AI without needing Colin in the room

### The Capacity Bottleneck
Colin is the single point of contact for:
- Delivery on all active projects
- Management of the India team
- Recruiting for AI roles
- Supporting the sales function (slides, presentations, case studies)
- TalentAI internal initiative

He's at his limit, and every unqualified lead that gets pushed to him competes with actual client delivery work.

### The Accountability Inversion
When sales over-promises or under-qualifies, Colin absorbs all the consequences:
- Scrambles to deliver what was promised, OR
- Pushes back and looks "difficult"
- Sales faces no consequences because Colin absorbs the fallout
