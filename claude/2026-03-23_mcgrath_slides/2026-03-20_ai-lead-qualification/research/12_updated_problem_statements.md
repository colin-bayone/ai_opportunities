## NEW Problem 9: No Supply Qualification
**Statement:** Even when demand is real, there's no process to assess whether BayOne can actually deliver before committing. The workshop facilitator explicitly rejected this as his concern, calling it "delivery's problem."

**Why it matters:** Signing up for work you can't staff or deliver is worse than not signing up at all. It damages client relationships and burns Colin's team.

**Framework requirement:** Supply qualification checklist (capacity, capability alignment, staffing readiness) that runs alongside demand qualification.

## NEW Problem 10: No Written Documentation Culture
**Statement:** Sales communicates via calls and Teams chats, not written documentation. There's nothing in Salesforce. Colin is expected to attend meetings and then do 3x the work to get information down on paper. Requests come as "call me and I'll tell you about this opportunity" rather than written briefs.

**Why it matters:** Without written documentation, there's no way to qualify, triage, or prioritize. Everything stays verbal, which means it's subject to overstatement, misrepresentation, and memory drift.

**Framework requirement:** Written intake is mandatory. No qualification review happens without a filled-out form/document. "Call me about it" is not an acceptable way to bring an opportunity forward.

## NEW Problem 11: No Prioritization or Triage Mechanism
**Statement:** There are no regular sessions where opportunities are reviewed, prioritized, and resourced. Sales throws opportunities at Colin ad hoc, each claiming to be the most important thing. There's no collective view of the pipeline or agreement on what to pursue.

**Why it matters:** Without triage, every new lead competes equally with every other lead and with active delivery work. Colin can't plan his time because the incoming stream is unfiltered and unranked.

**Framework requirement:** Periodic triage sessions (proposed cadence: biweekly or weekly) with Colin, Suva, and at least one leadership member (Yogesh or Rahul).

## NEW Problem 12: Perverse Sales Incentive Structures
**Statement:** Salespeople are incentivized to sign MSAs and claim accounts regardless of whether actual projects materialize. Getting a client's name associated with them means exclusive territorial rights to that client. This rewards quantity of accounts over quality of opportunities.

**Why it matters:** Logo hunting fills the pipeline with accounts that have no revenue potential but look like activity. It creates the illusion of a healthy pipeline while wasting technical resources on dead ends.

**Framework requirement:** Minimum value threshold for technical involvement. Not every account or MSA justifies AI resource engagement. The framework should define when an opportunity is large enough to warrant Colin's time.

---

## NEW Problem 13: Speculative "Could Lead To" Justification
**Statement:** Leads are justified not by present-tense qualification but by hypothetical future value ("could lead to an MSA," "other opportunities"). This reasoning is unfalsifiable and can justify literally any meeting.

**Why it matters:** It prevents anyone from saying no. Every contact at every company "could" lead to business. Without a present-tense qualification bar, the pipeline is infinite and none of it is real.

**Framework requirement:** Qualification must be based on current, verifiable indicators of interest, problem, budget, and authority, not speculative future potential.

## NEW Problem 14: No Gatekeeping by Sales Leadership
**Statement:** Unqualified requests flow directly from individual salespeople to Colin with no filter. The VP of Sales is not screening or qualifying before escalation.

**Why it matters:** Colin has to be the person saying no to every bad lead, which makes him look difficult. That filtering should happen within the sales org before it reaches technical resources.

**Framework requirement:** Sales leadership (VP of Sales) should review and approve opportunities before they're brought to the technical lead. The framework should define this as an explicit role.

## NEW Problem 15: No Role Differentiation for Technical Sales
**Statement:** Many requests that come to Colin (generic solutions pitches, partnership explorations, introductory AI discussions) would be appropriate for a sales engineer or technical sales person, not the Director of AI. That role doesn't exist at BayOne yet.

**Why it matters:** Colin's time is being spent on meetings that don't require his seniority or specialization. The framework should distinguish between meetings that need Colin and meetings that need a different technical sales role.

**Framework requirement:** Define tiers of technical involvement. Not every AI-adjacent meeting requires the Director of AI.

---

## NEW Problem 16: Staffing Mindset Applied to Solutions Selling
**Statement:** BayOne's sales culture was built on staffing, where promising a capability you don't have is low-risk (you just hire someone). That logic is being applied to solutions selling, where promising a capability you don't have creates commitments you can't meet.

**Why it matters:** This is the root cultural explanation for Patterns 3, 4, and 7 (fabricating capabilities, fabricating case studies, fabricated case study origin story). In staffing, "fake it till you make it" is a viable strategy. In solutions, it's a credibility-destroying one.

**Framework requirement:** The framework must explicitly call out that solutions selling requires different rules than staffing selling. Capabilities must be verified, not assumed.

## NEW Problem 17: Kitchen Sink Presentations / Fishing Expeditions
**Statement:** Sales builds massive, generic decks (40-60 slides for 20-minute meetings) covering every possible BayOne service, hoping one hook lands. Client meetings about a specific topic get derailed into pitches for unrelated services.

**Why it matters:** From the client side (Colin experienced this as a BayOne customer at Coherent): it's intimidating, signals that the vendor doesn't care about the client's actual need, and makes BayOne look disorganized. It's the opposite of the problem-first philosophy that works.

**Framework requirement:** Meetings should have a specific scope tied to the client's stated interest. The framework should prohibit "boil the ocean" pitches and require tailored, focused materials.

## NEW Problem 18: No Quality Gate for Client-Facing Materials
**Statement:** There is no standard for quality of materials sent to clients. Unformatted Word documents with typos get sent to SVPs. Hastily assembled presentations with no branding. AI-generated case studies with no review.

**Why it matters:** First impressions matter. Poor quality materials undermine BayOne's credibility before the conversation even starts.

**Framework requirement:** A minimum quality standard for anything client-facing, with a review step before distribution.
