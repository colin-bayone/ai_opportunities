Talk Like a Human: A Guide for AI Communications
Why This Matters
AI-generated text has infected professional communication with a distinctive "TED talk style" that readers
immediately recognize as inauthentic. Whether you're writing customer emails, internal memos, sales
pitches, or social media posts, these patterns destroy credibility. This guide helps you write like a human,
not an algorithm. Feel free to attach to your next chat session and ask any model to follow these guidelines!
The Most Common AI Red Flags (And How to Fix Them)
1. Em-Dashes (The worst of them all)
AI loves dashes—it uses them constantly—even when commas would work better.
❌ AI: "Our solution—powered by cutting-edge technology—delivers results."
✓ Human: "Our solution, powered by cutting-edge technology, delivers results."
Fix: Replace 90% of em-dashes with commas or periods. Save dashes for true interruptions or emphatic
breaks. (For clarity, an em-dash is a “—“ character)
2. Contrastive Rhetorical Framing
AI constantly uses "It's not just X, it's Y" constructions. Once you notice, you never stop noticing!
❌ AI: "This isn't just about efficiency. It's about transformation."
✓ Human: "This improves efficiency and transforms operations."
❌ AI: "We're not just building software. We're building the future."
✓ Human: "We're building software that shapes how teams work."
Fix: State what something IS, not what it ISN'T. Be direct.
3. Rhetorical Questions with Fragment Answers
AI asks and immediately answers its own questions.
❌ AI: "The result? Unprecedented growth."
✓ Human: "The result was unprecedented growth."
❌ AI: "Our secret? Relentless innovation."
✓ Human: "We succeed through relentless innovation."
Fix: Make statements. Don't interview yourself. Be assertive and direct.
4. Triple Patterns and Triplet Framing
AI obsessively groups things in threes, including bullet list items.
❌ AI: "Fast. Reliable. Secure."
✓ Human: "Fast and secure" or "Reliable performance"
❌ AI: "We innovate, integrate, and iterate."
✓ Human: "We continuously improve through integration."
Fix: Vary your groupings. Use pairs, singles, or longer lists.
5. The Inspirational Pivot
AI shifts from specific to abstract to create fake profundity.
❌ AI: "This isn't just about data. It's about human potential."
✓ Human: "Better data helps teams make confident decisions."
Fix: Stay concrete. Avoid grand abstractions.
6. Universal Claims Without Evidence
AI makes sweeping statements without sources.
❌ AI: "Studies show that 87% of executives prefer our approach."
✓ Human: "In our 2024 client survey, 87% of executives preferred our approach."
❌ AI: "Everyone knows that digital transformation is essential."
✓ Human: "Digital transformation has become essential for competitive advantage."
Fix: Cite real sources or avoid universal claims.
7. Emoji Overload
AI uses emojis like confetti at a child's party.
❌ AI: "We're excited to help you succeed! 🚀💪🌟 Your journey starts here! 🎯✨"
✓ Human: "We're ready to help you get started."
Fix: Maximum one emoji per message in casual contexts. Zero in formal communication.

Context-Specific Guidelines
Customer Emails
Avoid:
Opening with rhetorical questions
Multiple exclamation points
"I hope this email finds you well"
Contrastive framing in the subject line
Do:
Start with their specific situation
Use their company name naturally
Reference actual previous interactions
Include specific next steps with dates
Internal Communications
Avoid:
TED talk inspirational tone
Abstract mission statements
Triple-pattern bullet points
Fake urgency or drama
Do:
Lead with decisions or actions needed
Use colleague names when relevant
Reference real projects and deadlines
Write like you talk in meetings
Sales Pitches
Avoid:
Watched a VP completely lose her team's trust in 30 seconds today.
Team presents 6 months of work. She immediately points out what's wrong.
No acknowledgment of what worked. No questions about their process. Just
a grocery list of "concerns."
You could see everyone deflate. One engineer actually closed his laptop
mid-meeting.
Here's what kills me - she was technically right about most issues. But
being right doesn't matter if your team stops listening.
I've made this mistake too. You get stressed, you see problems, you want
to fix them NOW. But leadership isn't code review.
The best manager I ever had would always ask "What are you most proud of
here?" before anything else. Took her 30 seconds. Changed everything.
Anyone else struggle with giving feedback when you're stressed? How do
you catch yourself?
"We're not just another vendor"
Triplet value propositions
Abstract transformation promises
Rhetorical question openings
Emoji explosions
Generic benefit claims
Do:
Start with their specific challenge
Provide concrete examples from similar clients
Include real metrics and timelines
Admit what you're NOT good at
Make clear, specific offers
Sound like you understand their actual problem
Blog Posts and Articles
Avoid:
"In today's fast-paced world" openings
Contrastive framing in headlines
Perfect three-part structures
Abstract conclusions
Overuse of subheadings with emojis
Do:
Open with a specific example or data point
Use varied section lengths
Include real case studies
End with actionable takeaways
Write like you're explaining to a colleague
Technical Documentation
Avoid:
Inspirational introductions
Emoji-decorated section headers
Marketing language
Hiding limitations or bugs
Over-explaining basic concepts
Do:
Start with requirements and constraints
Include actual code examples
Mention known issues upfront
Use version numbers and specifics
Write like a developer leaving notes
Advanced Detection Avoidance
Sentence Structure Variety
AI tends toward predictable patterns:
Medium sentence. Another medium sentence. Short punch.
Question? Fragment answer.
Setup, elaboration, and conclusion.
Human writing varies:
Mix very short sentences with longer, more complex ones that develop ideas fully. Then surprise with
brevity.
Sometimes start with "And" or "But"
Occasionally use fragments. Like this. (But only occasionally!)
Let some paragraphs run long, others stay brief
Natural Imperfection
AI writing is suspiciously polished:
Perfect grammar always
Flawless parallel structure
Never uses contractions
Human writing includes:
Occasional contractions (it's, won't, can't)
Minor inconsistencies in style
Personal voice quirks
Industry-specific shorthand
Specific Over Generic
AI generalizes:
"Enhance your workflow"
"Drive success"
"Unlock potential"
Humans specify:
"Cut proposal writing time from 3 hours to 45 minutes"
"Helped Acme Corp close 2 more deals per quarter"
"Reduced customer response time to under 2 hours"
The Ultimate Test
Before sending any professional communication, ask:
1. Would I actually say this out loud to this person?
2. Did I include specific details only I would know?
3. Does it sound like me on a normal Tuesday, not me giving a TED talk?
4. Can I remove any em-dashes, rhetorical questions, or "it's not just" phrases?
5. Is every claim supported by specific evidence?

The Bottom Line
Good professional writing sounds like a competent human wrote it quickly, not like an algorithm trying to
inspire. Your readers can spot AI patterns immediately (for instance, saying “the bottom line” as Bay-I did here!)
They want clear communication from a real person, not dramatic flourishes from a chatbot.
Write like you're explaining something to a smart colleague over coffee. Because that's exactly what
you're doing.
